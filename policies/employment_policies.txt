11. EMPLOYMENT POLICY

11.1 Purpose and Organizational Philosophy
11.1.1 Why This Policy Exists
— The Foundation of the Employment Relationship. The Employment Policy is one of the most fundamental documents in the Company's entire policy framework. It establishes the principles, standards, and mutual obligations that govern the relationship between the Company and every individual it employs — from the moment of recruitment through the entire duration of employment and beyond. Where the Code of Conduct referenced as HR-COC-001 governs how employees behave as professionals, and the Data Security Policy referenced as HR-DSP-001 governs how employees handle information, this policy governs the very structure of the employment relationship itself — the terms and conditions under which work is performed, the rights and responsibilities of both parties, the standards that determine career progression and professional development, and the framework through which the employment relationship may be modified or concluded. Employment is not merely a transactional exchange of labor for compensation. It is a professional partnership built on mutual respect, clear expectations, and shared commitment to organizational success, and this policy is the foundation on which that partnership is built.
— The Organizational Imperative. The Company operates in a competitive and dynamic business environment where the quality of its workforce is the single most important determinant of its ability to achieve its strategic objectives, serve its clients effectively, and maintain its position in the marketplace. The Company's success depends fundamentally on its ability to attract, retain, develop, and deploy talented individuals who are committed to excellence, aligned with the Company's values, and equipped with the skills and capabilities required to perform their roles effectively. This policy exists to create a framework that enables the Company to build and maintain such a workforce — through fair and transparent recruitment processes, clear and consistent employment terms, meaningful opportunities for professional growth, performance management systems that recognize and reward excellence, and employment practices that comply with all applicable employment law and regulatory requirements. A workplace that lacks clear employment standards, that applies policies inconsistently, or that fails to provide employees with a clear understanding of their rights and responsibilities is a workplace that cannot attract or retain the talent it needs to succeed.
— The Human Imperative. This policy has been written with a genuine recognition that employment is not merely a legal or administrative relationship — it is a deeply personal and professionally significant aspect of every employee's life. The terms of employment, the opportunities for advancement, the standards of performance evaluation, the processes for addressing concerns, and the framework for professional development all have profound implications for an employee's career trajectory, financial security, work-life balance, and sense of professional fulfillment. This policy is not a declaration of unilateral authority. It is a commitment to fairness, transparency, and respect — a clear, comprehensive, and accessible guide that gives every employee a complete understanding of what they can expect from the Company, what the Company expects from them, and how the employment relationship will be managed throughout its duration. The Company invests in this policy, and in the systems and processes that support it, because it genuinely believes that employees who understand their rights and responsibilities, who are treated with fairness and respect, and who have clear pathways for professional growth are the most engaged, productive, and committed members of the workforce.
— The Philosophy of Mutual Commitment. The employment relationship is built on a foundation of mutual commitment — the Company's commitment to provide fair compensation, meaningful work, opportunities for growth, and a respectful and inclusive workplace environment, and the employee's commitment to perform their role with competence, integrity, and dedication, to comply with all applicable policies and standards, and to contribute to the Company's success through their professional efforts. This policy is built on the conviction that both parties to the employment relationship have rights that must be respected and responsibilities that must be fulfilled, and that the relationship functions most effectively when both parties understand and honor their respective obligations. Employment is not a one-sided arrangement in which the Company sets terms unilaterally, nor is it an arrangement in which employees can expect to receive benefits without fulfilling their professional obligations. It is a balanced partnership, and this policy is the framework that ensures that balance is maintained.
— A Living and Evolving Document. Like all enterprise policies, the Employment Policy is a living document that will be reviewed, updated, and strengthened as the business environment evolves, as applicable employment law develops, as the Company's strategic objectives and organizational structure change, and as practical experience provides insights into how the policy can be made more effective. Every employee is expected to engage with this policy seriously, to seek clarification where needed, and to treat it as a genuine and relevant guide to one of the most important professional relationships they will have in their career.

11.2 Scope and Applicability
11.2.1 Who This Policy Applies To
— Universal Application to All Employees. This policy applies universally and without exception to every individual engaged by the Company in any employment capacity — regardless of seniority, tenure, professional standing, department, business unit, geographic location, employment classification, or any other factor. There are no exemptions based on role, title, or perceived importance to the organization. The most senior executive in the organization is subject to exactly the same employment policy framework as the most junior team member, and any perception that seniority or expertise grants an individual the right to operate outside the standards established in this policy will be firmly and consistently rejected at every level of the organization.
— All Categories of Employment Covered. This policy covers permanent full-time employees, part-time employees, employees on fixed-term or rolling contracts, interns and trainees, employees on secondment or temporary assignment, and any other individual engaged in an employment relationship with the Company. Each of these individuals, regardless of the nature or duration of their employment, is entitled to the rights and subject to the responsibilities established in this policy from the first day of their employment. The specific application of certain policy provisions may vary based on employment classification — for example, benefit eligibility may differ between full-time and part-time employees — but the fundamental framework of rights, responsibilities, and standards applies to all employees equally.
— Seniority Confers Heightened Responsibility, Not Exemption. Senior leaders, managers, and those with elevated organizational responsibilities are not exempt from this policy — they are held to a higher standard because of the greater impact of their conduct on the organization, the powerful signal that their behavior sends to the rest of the workforce, and the additional responsibilities they bear for managing the employment relationships of their team members. A leader who bypasses employment standards — even for apparently legitimate operational reasons — communicates to their team that the standards are optional, which is a message the Company will not tolerate being sent at any level.
11.2.2 Where This Policy Applies
— All Work Locations and Arrangements. This policy applies in every location and arrangement in which Company work is performed — whether on Company premises, at client sites, at home in a remote or hybrid arrangement, while traveling on Company business, or in any other location where an employee is performing work on behalf of the Company. The employment relationship and the rights and responsibilities it entails do not diminish based on the employee's physical location. An employee working from a remote location has exactly the same employment rights and obligations as an employee working at the Company's headquarters.
— All Phases of the Employment Relationship. This policy applies throughout the entire lifecycle of the employment relationship — from the recruitment and selection process, through onboarding and probation, during active employment, through any periods of leave or absence, during performance management and disciplinary processes, and through the conclusion of the employment relationship. The standards and processes established in this policy govern every phase of employment without exception.
11.2.3 Relationship With Other Policies
— An Integrated Employment Framework. This Employment Policy operates as one component of the Company's broader human resources and governance framework. It must be read and applied in conjunction with the Code of Conduct referenced as HR-COC-001, the Data Security Policy referenced as HR-DSP-001, the Leave Policy referenced as HR-LP-001, the Performance Management Policy referenced as HR-PMP-001, the Work From Home Policy referenced as HR-WFH-001, and all other applicable Company policies. Where a specific policy provides more detailed guidance on a particular aspect of employment — such as the Leave Policy's treatment of leave entitlements and application processes — that specific policy governs. Where an employment concern arises that falls outside the scope of any specific policy, this Employment Policy provides the governing framework.
— Conflict Resolution. In the event of any apparent conflict between the standards established in this policy and those established in any other Company policy, employees must seek guidance from HR before taking action. Employees must not act unilaterally on their own interpretation of any apparent conflict between policies and must not use an apparent conflict as justification for departing from employment standards that would otherwise apply.

11.3 Employment Classifications and Categories
11.3.1 The Classification Framework
— Why Classification Matters. The appropriate terms and conditions of employment, including compensation structure, benefit eligibility, working hours expectations, and notice periods, depend fundamentally on the classification of the employment relationship. The Company's employment classification framework provides every employee with a clear, consistent, and legally compliant system for understanding their employment status and the rights and responsibilities that flow from that status. Every individual engaged by the Company falls within one of the employment classifications described below, and every employee must be aware of their classification and the implications it has for their employment relationship.
— Full-Time Permanent Employees. Full-time permanent employees are individuals engaged in an ongoing employment relationship with the Company with no predetermined end date, who work a standard full-time schedule as defined by the Company — typically a minimum of forty hours per week or the equivalent as determined by local employment law and Company practice. Full-time permanent employees are eligible for the complete range of Company benefits, including health insurance, retirement plans, paid leave entitlements, professional development opportunities, and all other benefits available to employees in their geographic location and role category. Full-time permanent employees are subject to the standard notice periods for resignation and termination as established in Section 5.18, are eligible for performance reviews and career development programs, and are entitled to all protections and rights established under applicable employment law and this policy.
— Part-Time Permanent Employees. Part-time permanent employees are individuals engaged in an ongoing employment relationship with the Company with no predetermined end date, who work a reduced schedule compared to the standard full-time schedule — typically less than forty hours per week or the equivalent as determined by local employment law and Company practice. Part-time permanent employees are eligible for prorated benefits based on their working hours, including prorated health insurance, prorated paid leave entitlements, and access to professional development opportunities. Part-time employees are subject to the same employment standards, performance expectations, and policy requirements as full-time employees, adjusted proportionally to reflect their working hours. Part-time employees may be eligible to transition to full-time status based on business needs and performance, subject to the availability of full-time positions and the employee's demonstrated capability.
— Fixed-Term Contract Employees. Fixed-term contract employees are individuals engaged for a specific duration or for the completion of a specific project or assignment, with a predetermined end date established at the commencement of employment. Fixed-term contracts may be used for project-based work, seasonal requirements, coverage for employees on extended leave, or other temporary business needs. The terms and conditions of fixed-term employment, including compensation, benefits, and notice periods, are established in the individual employment contract. Fixed-term employees are eligible for benefits as specified in their contract and may be eligible for conversion to permanent status based on business needs, performance, and the availability of permanent positions. The Company will not use fixed-term contracts as a means of avoiding its obligations to permanent employees, and the use of fixed-term contracts will be limited to circumstances where a genuine temporary need exists.
— Interns and Trainees. Interns and trainees are individuals engaged in structured learning and development programs designed to provide practical work experience, typically as part of an educational program or as a bridge between education and professional employment. Internships and traineeships are typically of limited duration — ranging from three months to twelve months — and are structured to provide meaningful learning opportunities while contributing to the Company's work. Interns and trainees receive compensation as specified in their internship or traineeship agreement, which may take the form of a stipend, salary, or educational credit. Interns and trainees are subject to the Company's Code of Conduct and applicable employment policies, are eligible for limited benefits as specified in their agreement, and may be eligible for conversion to permanent employment based on performance and business needs. The Company is committed to providing interns and trainees with genuine learning opportunities and meaningful work assignments, not merely administrative or menial tasks.
— Consultants and Independent Contractors. Consultants and independent contractors are individuals engaged to provide specialized services to the Company under a service agreement rather than an employment contract. Consultants and independent contractors are not employees of the Company, are not subject to this Employment Policy, and are governed by the terms of their individual service agreements. The distinction between an employee and an independent contractor is determined by applicable employment law based on factors including the degree of control exercised by the Company, the integration of the individual's work into the Company's operations, and the nature of the working relationship. The Company will not misclassify employees as independent contractors to avoid employment obligations, and any individual who believes they have been misclassified may raise the matter with HR for review.
11.3.2 Classification Assignment and Changes
— Classification at the Point of Hire. The employment classification of every individual is established at the point of hire and is clearly communicated in the offer letter and employment contract. The classification reflects the nature of the work to be performed, the duration of the engagement, and the business need that the engagement is designed to address. Employees must receive clear written communication of their classification, the implications of that classification for their terms and conditions of employment, and any circumstances under which the classification might change.
— Changes in Classification. An employee's classification may change during the course of their employment based on business needs, performance, and mutual agreement. Examples include a part-time employee transitioning to full-time status, a fixed-term employee being offered permanent employment, or an intern being converted to a permanent role. Any change in classification must be documented in writing, must be communicated clearly to the employee, and must be reflected in updated employment documentation. Changes in classification that result in reduced benefits or less favorable terms require the employee's explicit consent and may be subject to consultation requirements under applicable employment law.

11.4 Recruitment and Selection
11.4.1 Principles of Fair and Merit-Based Recruitment
— Recruitment Based on Merit and Capability. The Company's recruitment and selection processes are designed to identify and attract the most qualified candidates for every position based on their skills, experience, qualifications, and demonstrated capability to perform the role effectively. All recruitment decisions are made on the basis of merit, without regard to race, color, religion, gender, gender identity, sexual orientation, age, national origin, disability, marital status, pregnancy, veteran status, or any other characteristic protected by applicable law. The Company is committed to building a diverse and inclusive workforce and recognizes that diversity strengthens the organization's capability to innovate, solve problems, and serve its clients effectively. Merit-based recruitment does not mean that the most experienced candidate is always selected — it means that the candidate who demonstrates the best combination of capability, potential, cultural fit, and alignment with the Company's values is selected, regardless of their personal characteristics.
— Transparency and Consistency in Recruitment Processes. All recruitment processes are conducted with transparency, consistency, and fairness. Every candidate for a position receives clear information about the role, the selection process, the timeline for decisions, and the criteria that will be used to evaluate candidates. The selection process is structured to provide every candidate with a fair opportunity to demonstrate their capability, and all candidates are evaluated against the same criteria using the same assessment methods. The Company does not engage in informal or ad hoc recruitment processes that bypass standard procedures, and all recruitment decisions are documented and retained in accordance with applicable record-keeping requirements.
11.4.2 The Recruitment Process
— Job Posting and Candidate Sourcing. All positions are posted internally and, where appropriate, externally to ensure that qualified candidates have the opportunity to apply. Internal postings provide existing employees with opportunities for career advancement and role transitions, while external postings enable the Company to access the broader talent market. Job postings include clear descriptions of the role, required qualifications, key responsibilities, and the application process. The Company may use multiple channels for candidate sourcing, including internal job boards, external job boards, recruitment agencies, professional networks, and employee referrals, always ensuring that the sourcing process is fair and does not create barriers for qualified candidates.
— Application and Screening. All candidates submit applications through the Company's standard application process, which may include submission of a resume, completion of an application form, responses to screening questions, and submission of work samples or portfolios where relevant to the role. Applications are reviewed against the stated requirements for the position, and candidates who meet the minimum qualifications proceed to the next stage of the selection process. The screening process is designed to identify candidates who have the potential to succeed in the role, not to create unnecessary barriers to application. The Company does not use automated screening systems that create discriminatory barriers, and all screening decisions are subject to review to ensure fairness and consistency.
— Interview and Assessment. Candidates who pass the initial screening participate in structured interviews and assessments designed to evaluate their technical capability, problem-solving ability, communication skills, cultural fit, and alignment with the Company's values. The interview process typically includes multiple stages, may involve multiple interviewers, and may include practical assessments, case studies, or other evaluation methods relevant to the role. All interviewers are trained in fair and effective interviewing techniques, are provided with structured interview guides, and are required to document their assessments consistently. The Company does not use interview questions that are irrelevant to job performance, that create discriminatory barriers, or that probe into protected characteristics.
— Reference Checks and Background Verification. Before making a final offer, the Company conducts reference checks with previous employers and may conduct background verification as described in Section 5.5. Reference checks are designed to verify the candidate's employment history, to understand their performance in previous roles, and to assess their suitability for the position. Background verification is conducted in accordance with applicable law, with the candidate's explicit consent, and in a manner that respects the candidate's privacy rights. The Company does not make employment decisions based on information obtained through reference checks or background verification that is not relevant to job performance or that creates discriminatory barriers.
— Offer and Onboarding. Successful candidates receive a formal written offer that includes the position title, reporting relationship, start date, compensation structure, benefits eligibility, and any other relevant terms and conditions of employment. Offers are conditional upon successful completion of background verification, where applicable, and acceptance of the Company's standard employment terms. Once an offer is accepted and all conditions are satisfied, the candidate proceeds to the onboarding process described in Section 5.6.

11.5 Background Verification and Pre-Employment Screening
11.5.1 The Purpose and Scope of Background Verification
— Why Background Verification Is Conducted. The Company conducts background verification and pre-employment screening to validate information provided by candidates, to ensure that candidates meet the organization's standards for employment, to protect the safety and security of the workplace, and to comply with applicable legal and regulatory requirements. Background verification is not a tool for creating discriminatory barriers or for making employment decisions based on irrelevant information. It is a legitimate business process designed to ensure that the Company makes informed hiring decisions and that employees are qualified, trustworthy, and suitable for their roles.
— What Is Verified. The scope of background verification varies based on the role, the level of responsibility, and applicable legal requirements. Common elements of background verification include identity verification through government-issued identification documents, educational qualification verification through contact with educational institutions, employment history verification through contact with previous employers, professional reference checks with individuals who can speak to the candidate's professional capability and character, criminal background checks where permitted by law and relevant to the role, credit checks for positions involving financial responsibilities, and drug screening for safety-sensitive positions or where required by law. The specific elements of background verification for each role are determined by HR in conjunction with the hiring manager and are communicated to candidates before verification commences.
11.5.2 The Background Verification Process
— Conditional Offers and Candidate Consent. All employment offers are conditional upon successful completion of background verification. Candidates are informed of this condition at the time the offer is made and are required to provide explicit written consent before any background verification is conducted. The consent process includes clear information about what will be verified, who will conduct the verification, how the information will be used, and the candidate's rights in relation to the verification process. The Company does not conduct background verification without the candidate's consent, except where required by law.
— Third-Party Verification Services. The Company may use reputable third-party agencies to conduct background verification, always ensuring that these agencies comply with applicable law, maintain appropriate data security standards, and conduct verification in a fair and non-discriminatory manner. The Company retains responsibility for ensuring that background verification is conducted appropriately and that employment decisions based on verification results are fair and legally compliant.
— Confidentiality and Data Protection. All information obtained through background verification is treated as confidential, is used solely for employment decision-making purposes, is stored securely in accordance with the Data Security Policy referenced as HR-DSP-001, and is retained only for the period required by applicable law. Candidates have the right to access information about themselves obtained through background verification, subject to applicable legal restrictions, and the Company will provide candidates with copies of verification reports upon request.
— Adverse Action and Candidate Rights. If information obtained through background verification may result in withdrawal of an offer or other adverse employment action, the candidate is informed of the specific findings, is provided with a copy of the verification report, is given a reasonable opportunity to dispute or explain any discrepancies, and is allowed sufficient time to respond before any final decision is made. The Company does not make adverse employment decisions based on information that is inaccurate, outdated, or not relevant to job performance, and all adverse decisions are subject to review to ensure fairness and legal compliance.
— Falsification and Misrepresentation. Providing false information during the recruitment process, failing to disclose required information, or misrepresenting qualifications, experience, or background is grounds for immediate withdrawal of an offer or termination of employment if discovered after hire. The Company takes falsification seriously because it undermines the integrity of the recruitment process and the trust that is fundamental to the employment relationship.

11.6 Onboarding and Integration
11.6.1 The Purpose and Importance of Onboarding
— Onboarding as a Foundation for Success. Effective onboarding is not merely an administrative process for completing paperwork and receiving equipment. It is a comprehensive integration program designed to provide new employees with the knowledge, resources, relationships, and cultural understanding they need to become productive, engaged, and successful members of the organization. Research consistently demonstrates that employees who receive effective onboarding are more likely to perform well, to remain with the organization, and to feel satisfied with their employment experience. The Company invests in comprehensive onboarding because it recognizes that the first weeks and months of employment set the foundation for the entire employment relationship.
11.6.2 The Onboarding Process
— Pre-Start Preparation. Before the new employee's first day, HR coordinates with the hiring manager to ensure that the workspace is prepared, that equipment and systems access are provisioned, that the first week's schedule is planned, and that the new employee receives necessary information about their start date, location, and what to expect on their first day. The new employee receives a welcome package that may include information about the Company, their role, the onboarding schedule, and practical information about the workplace.
— First Day Orientation. The new employee's first day includes a comprehensive orientation session conducted by HR that covers the Company's history, mission, values, and strategic objectives, an overview of the organizational structure and key functions, introduction to the Company's policy framework and key policies including the Code of Conduct, Data Security Policy, and this Employment Policy, information about benefits, payroll, and administrative processes, and practical information about the workplace, facilities, and resources. The first day also includes meetings with the hiring manager to discuss role expectations, introductions to team members, and initial work assignments designed to provide immediate engagement with the role.
— First Week Integration. During the first week, the new employee continues to receive structured orientation, begins to engage with their role responsibilities, participates in team meetings and activities, completes any required training programs, and establishes relationships with colleagues and key stakeholders. The hiring manager conducts regular check-ins to answer questions, provide guidance, and ensure that the new employee feels supported and integrated.
— First Month Foundation Building. During the first month, the new employee becomes increasingly independent in their role, continues to build relationships and understanding of the organization, receives feedback on their initial performance, and identifies areas where additional support or development may be needed. The hiring manager provides ongoing guidance, establishes clear performance expectations, and creates opportunities for the new employee to contribute meaningfully to team objectives.
— Ongoing Integration and Development. Onboarding does not end after the first month. The integration process continues throughout the probationary period and beyond, as the new employee becomes increasingly capable and confident in their role, builds deeper relationships and understanding of the organization, and begins to contribute at a higher level. The hiring manager continues to provide support, feedback, and development opportunities, and the new employee continues to take initiative for their own learning and integration.
11.6.3 Onboarding Documentation and Compliance
— Required Documentation. During onboarding, new employees complete all required employment documentation, including employment contracts, tax forms, benefit enrollment forms, policy acknowledgments, and any other documentation required by applicable law or Company policy. HR ensures that all documentation is completed accurately and on time, and that the new employee understands what they are signing and why.
— Policy Acknowledgment. New employees are required to formally acknowledge that they have read, understood, and agree to comply with all applicable Company policies, including this Employment Policy, the Code of Conduct, the Data Security Policy, and any other policies relevant to their role. Policy acknowledgments are captured through the HRMS and are maintained in the employee's personnel file. Failure to complete policy acknowledgments within the required timeframe will be addressed as a compliance concern.
— Systems Access and Security. During onboarding, new employees receive access to Company systems and are required to complete mandatory security training, including data security awareness, password management, and phishing awareness. Systems access is provisioned in accordance with the principle of least privilege described in the Data Security Policy, and the new employee is responsible for maintaining the security of their credentials and complying with all security requirements.

11.7 Probationary Period
11.7.1 The Purpose and Nature of Probation
— Probation as a Mutual Evaluation Period. The probationary period is a structured evaluation period during which both the Company and the new employee assess whether the employment relationship is a good fit — whether the employee has the capability to perform the role effectively, whether the role meets the employee's expectations and career objectives, and whether the employee's values and work style align with the Company's culture and requirements. Probation is not a period of reduced rights or protections. It is a period of focused evaluation and support, during which the employee receives regular feedback, clear performance expectations, and opportunities to demonstrate their capability and potential.
— Probation Applies to New Employees and Role Transitions. All new employees serve a probationary period upon joining the Company. Employees who are promoted or transferred to significantly different roles may also serve a probationary period in their new role, typically of shorter duration than the initial probationary period. The purpose of probation for role transitions is to ensure that the employee has the capability to succeed in the new role and that the role transition is successful for both the employee and the Company.
11.7.2 Probationary Period Duration and Structure
— Standard Probationary Periods. The standard probationary period for new employees is ninety days (three months) from the date of joining, unless a different duration is specified in the employment contract or required by applicable law. The probationary period for employees promoted or transferred to new roles is typically sixty days (two months), unless a different duration is specified. The probationary period begins on the employee's first day of employment and runs continuously, including any periods of approved leave, unless the probationary period is extended as described below.
— Probationary Period Extensions. In exceptional circumstances, the probationary period may be extended for up to an additional ninety days to provide additional time for evaluation or to address specific performance concerns. Extensions are not used as a means of delaying permanent employment decisions, and are granted only where there is a genuine need for additional evaluation time or where the employee has demonstrated improvement potential but requires additional time to meet performance standards. The employee is informed of any extension in writing, is provided with clear reasons for the extension, and is given specific objectives and timelines for improvement where applicable.
11.7.3 Probationary Period Evaluation and Feedback
— Regular Check-Ins and Feedback. During the probationary period, the employee's manager conducts regular check-ins to provide feedback, answer questions, address concerns, and assess progress. Formal check-ins are conducted at thirty days, sixty days, and at the end of the probationary period, with informal check-ins occurring more frequently as needed. The employee is encouraged to ask questions, seek clarification, and provide feedback about their experience during probation.
— Performance Expectations and Objectives. At the beginning of the probationary period, the employee receives clear performance expectations, specific objectives, and success criteria for their role. These expectations are established in writing, are discussed with the employee, and form the basis for evaluation throughout the probationary period. The employee is expected to demonstrate progress toward meeting these expectations and to seek support where needed.
— Formal Probationary Review. Before the end of the probationary period, the manager conducts a formal probationary review that evaluates the employee's performance, conduct, cultural fit, and potential for success in the role. The review includes input from team members and other stakeholders who have worked with the employee, is documented in writing, and is discussed with the employee. The employee has the opportunity to provide their own perspective and to discuss their experience during probation.
11.7.4 Probationary Period Outcomes
— Successful Completion of Probation. If the employee has demonstrated satisfactory performance, conduct, and cultural fit, the probationary period is successfully completed, and the employee becomes a permanent employee with full rights and protections under this policy and applicable employment law. The employee is informed of the successful completion in writing, and the completion is documented in their personnel file.
— Extension of Probation. If the employee has demonstrated improvement potential but requires additional time to meet performance standards, the probationary period may be extended as described above. The employee is informed of the extension, is provided with specific improvement objectives and timelines, and receives additional support and feedback during the extended period.
— Termination During Probation. If the employee has not demonstrated the capability to perform the role effectively, has engaged in conduct that is inconsistent with the Company's standards, or if the employment relationship is not a good fit for either party, employment may be terminated during or at the end of the probationary period. Termination during probation is subject to the notice periods and procedures described in Section 11.18, adjusted for the probationary context. The employee is informed of the termination decision, is provided with reasons for the decision, and is given an opportunity to discuss the decision with HR.

11.8 Terms and Conditions of Employment
11.8.1 Employment Contracts and Written Terms
— Written Employment Contracts Are Standard. All employees receive a written employment contract that clearly sets out the terms and conditions of their employment, including the position title, reporting relationship, start date, employment classification, working hours, compensation structure, benefits eligibility, notice periods, and any other relevant terms. The employment contract is provided before the employee commences employment, is reviewed with the employee during onboarding, and is retained in the employee's personnel file. The contract may be supplemented by this policy and other Company policies, which form part of the overall terms and conditions of employment.
— Contract Terms Must Comply With Applicable Law. All employment contracts comply with applicable employment law, including minimum wage requirements, maximum working hours, leave entitlements, notice periods, and any other legal requirements. Where the Company provides terms that are more favorable than the legal minimum — for example, higher compensation, more generous leave entitlements, or longer notice periods — the more favorable terms apply. The Company does not use employment contracts to avoid legal obligations or to create terms that are less favorable than the legal minimum.
— Standard Working Hours. The standard working hours for full-time employees are established in the employment contract and typically align with the Company's standard business hours, which may vary by location, department, or role. The Company recognizes that different roles may have different working hour requirements, and working hours are established based on business needs, role requirements, and applicable employment law. Employees are expected to work the hours required to fulfill their role responsibilities effectively, which may include occasional additional hours to meet business needs or deadlines.
— Flexible Working Arrangements. The Company recognizes that employees have diverse needs and responsibilities outside of work, and is committed to providing flexible working arrangements where operationally feasible. Flexible working arrangements may include flexible start and end times, compressed work weeks, part-time schedules, job sharing, and other arrangements that enable employees to balance work and personal responsibilities. Requests for flexible working arrangements are considered based on business needs, role requirements, and the employee's circumstances, and are approved where feasible. The process for requesting flexible working arrangements is described in the Work From Home Policy referenced as HR-WFH-001.
— Overtime and Additional Hours. Employees may be required to work additional hours beyond their standard schedule to meet business needs, project deadlines, or operational requirements. Additional hours are compensated in accordance with applicable employment law and the employee's employment contract. For exempt employees, additional hours are typically compensated through the salary structure, while non-exempt employees receive overtime compensation as required by law. The Company does not expect employees to work excessive hours on a regular basis, and managers are responsible for ensuring that work demands are reasonable and sustainable.
11.8.3 Place of Work
— Primary Work Location. The primary work location for each employee is established in the employment contract and may be a Company office, a client site, a remote location, or another location as determined by business needs and role requirements. The Company may require employees to work from different locations, to travel for business purposes, or to relocate based on business needs, subject to the terms of the employment contract and applicable law.
— Remote and Hybrid Work. The Company supports remote and hybrid work arrangements where operationally feasible and where the employee's role can be performed effectively in a remote or hybrid arrangement. Remote and hybrid work arrangements are subject to the Work From Home Policy referenced as HR-WFH-001, which establishes the standards, requirements, and processes for remote work. Employees working remotely or in hybrid arrangements are subject to the same employment standards, performance expectations, and policy requirements as employees working on Company premises, and are responsible for maintaining a professional and secure work environment.

11.9 Compensation and Benefits
11.9.1 Compensation Philosophy and Structure
— Fair and Competitive Compensation. The Company is committed to providing fair and competitive compensation that reflects the value of each role, the skills and experience of each employee, and the employee's contribution to the Company's success. Compensation is determined based on role requirements, market benchmarks, the employee's qualifications and experience, and performance. The Company regularly reviews compensation to ensure that it remains competitive and fair, and conducts pay equity analyses to ensure that compensation decisions are not influenced by protected characteristics.
— Compensation Structure. Employee compensation typically includes a base salary or wage, which is the fixed component of compensation paid on a regular schedule. Some roles may also include variable compensation components such as performance bonuses, commission, profit sharing, or other incentive payments. The compensation structure for each role is established in the employment contract and is reviewed annually as part of the performance management process. The Company provides employees with clear information about their compensation structure, how it is determined, and how it may change over time.
— Compensation Reviews and Adjustments. Employee compensation is reviewed annually as part of the performance management process, and may be adjusted based on performance, market conditions, role changes, and other relevant factors. 11.10 Performance Management
11.10.1 The Purpose and Philosophy of Performance Management
— Performance Management as a Development Tool. Performance management is not merely a process for evaluating employees and making compensation decisions. It is a comprehensive system designed to support employee development, to align individual performance with organizational objectives, to recognize and reward excellence, to identify and address performance concerns, and to create a culture of continuous improvement and accountability. The Company's performance management philosophy is based on the belief that employees perform best when they have clear expectations, regular feedback, meaningful development opportunities, and recognition for their contributions.
— Performance Management as a Continuous Process. Performance management is not an annual event that occurs once per year. It is a continuous process that includes regular goal setting, ongoing feedback, periodic formal reviews, development planning, and performance improvement processes where needed. Managers and employees share responsibility for effective performance management — managers are responsible for providing clear expectations, regular feedback, and development support, while employees are responsible for understanding expectations, seeking feedback, taking initiative for their own development, and performing their roles effectively.
11.10.2 Performance Expectations and Goal Setting
— Clear Performance Expectations. Every employee receives clear performance expectations that are established at the beginning of each performance period, are aligned with the employee's role responsibilities and organizational objectives, and are documented in writing. Performance expectations include both what the employee is expected to accomplish (objectives and goals) and how the employee is expected to accomplish it (behaviors and competencies). Expectations are specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound, and are reviewed and updated as needed throughout the performance period.
— Goal Setting and Alignment. Employee goals are established in collaboration between the employee and their manager, are aligned with team and organizational objectives, and reflect both the employee's current role responsibilities and their development objectives. Goals may include quantitative targets, qualitative objectives, project deliverables, skill development targets, and behavioral expectations. The goal-setting process provides employees with clarity about what success looks like, creates a framework for ongoing feedback and evaluation, and enables employees to understand how their work contributes to organizational success.
11.10.3 Ongoing Feedback and Development
— Regular Feedback Is Essential. Effective performance management requires regular, timely, and constructive feedback that helps employees understand how they are performing, what they are doing well, and where they can improve. Managers are expected to provide feedback on an ongoing basis, not only during formal review periods, and to create an environment in which employees feel comfortable seeking feedback and discussing their performance. Feedback should be specific, actionable, balanced (including both strengths and areas for improvement), and delivered in a respectful and supportive manner.
— Development Planning and Support. Every employee has a development plan that identifies learning and growth objectives, skill development needs, career aspirations, and the support and resources needed to achieve development goals. Development plans are created in collaboration between the employee and their manager, are reviewed and updated regularly, and are supported through training, mentoring, stretch assignments, and other development opportunities. The Company is committed to investing in employee development because it recognizes that employee growth is essential to organizational success.
11.10.4 Formal Performance Reviews
— Annual Performance Review Process. All employees participate in an annual formal performance review that evaluates their performance against established expectations and goals, assesses their demonstration of required competencies and behaviors, identifies strengths and areas for improvement, and establishes objectives and development plans for the coming year. The review process includes self-assessment by the employee, assessment by the manager, discussion and calibration, and documentation of the review outcomes. Reviews are conducted in a fair, consistent, and transparent manner, and employees have the opportunity to provide their perspective and to discuss their performance openly.
— Mid-Year Check-Ins. In addition to annual reviews, employees participate in mid-year check-ins that provide an opportunity to review progress against goals, discuss performance and development, and make adjustments to objectives or development plans as needed. Mid-year check-ins are less formal than annual reviews but provide important opportunities for feedback and course correction.
— Performance Review Outcomes. Performance reviews result in performance ratings that reflect the employee's level of performance, and these ratings inform compensation decisions, promotion considerations, development planning, and other employment decisions. Performance ratings are based on objective assessment against established criteria, not on subjective impressions or personal relationships, and are calibrated across the organization to ensure consistency and fairness.
11.10.5 Performance Improvement and Disciplinary Action
— Addressing Performance Concerns. When an employee's performance does not meet expectations, the Company follows a structured performance improvement process designed to help the employee succeed. The process begins with clear communication of performance concerns, identification of specific improvement objectives, provision of support and resources, and establishment of a timeline for improvement. The employee is given a fair opportunity to improve, receives regular feedback on their progress, and is supported through the improvement process. If performance improves to the required level, the improvement process concludes successfully. If performance does not improve despite support and effort, the Company may take further action, up to and including termination of employment, in accordance with the disciplinary framework described in Section 5.19.
— Performance Improvement Plans. Employees who are not meeting performance expectations may be placed on a formal Performance Improvement Plan (PIP) that clearly identifies performance concerns, establishes specific improvement objectives, provides a timeline for improvement, and outlines the consequences of not meeting improvement objectives. PIPs are not disciplinary actions in themselves, but are tools for providing structured support and clear expectations. Employees on PIPs receive regular feedback, additional support, and opportunities to demonstrate improvement. Successful completion of a PIP results in removal from the plan and return to normal performance management processes.

11.14 Grievance and Dispute Resolution
11.14.1 The Right to Raise Concerns
— Employees Have the Right to Be Heard. All employees have the right to raise concerns, complaints, or grievances about their employment, their working conditions, their treatment by colleagues or managers, or any other aspect of their employment relationship. The Company is committed to addressing employee concerns fairly, promptly, and effectively, and to creating an environment in which employees feel safe and supported in raising concerns without fear of retaliation. Employees who raise concerns in good faith will not face adverse consequences, and any retaliation against employees who raise concerns will be treated as a serious violation of this policy.
— Informal Resolution Is Encouraged. Many concerns can be resolved effectively through informal discussion between the employee and their manager, or through other informal channels. Employees are encouraged to raise concerns informally first, and managers are expected to listen to employee concerns, take them seriously, and work with employees to resolve issues constructively. Informal resolution is often faster, less stressful, and more effective than formal processes, and the Company encourages employees and managers to work together to resolve concerns at the informal level where possible.
11.14.2 Formal Grievance Process
— When to Use the Formal Process. If a concern cannot be resolved informally, or if the employee prefers to use the formal process, employees may raise a formal grievance through the grievance procedure established in this policy. Formal grievances may be raised about any aspect of employment, including working conditions, treatment by colleagues or managers, policy violations, discrimination, harassment, or any other employment-related concern. Employees are not required to exhaust informal resolution before raising a formal grievance, and may choose to use the formal process at any time.
— Grievance Procedure. The formal grievance procedure includes submission of a written grievance to HR, investigation of the grievance by HR or a designated investigator, opportunity for the employee to provide information and evidence, opportunity for the subject of the grievance to respond, review and decision by HR or a designated decision-maker, communication of the outcome to the employee, and implementation of any corrective action or remedies. The grievance procedure is designed to be fair, thorough, and timely, and employees are kept informed of the progress of their grievance throughout the process.
— Grievance Outcomes and Remedies. Grievance investigations may result in various outcomes, including confirmation that no violation occurred, identification of policy violations and implementation of corrective action, resolution of the underlying concern through mediation or other means, or other appropriate remedies. The specific outcome depends on the nature of the grievance, the findings of the investigation, and the circumstances of the case. Employees are informed of the outcome of their grievance and any remedies or corrective action that will be implemented.
11.14.3 External Dispute Resolution
— Right to External Remedies. Employees who are not satisfied with the outcome of internal grievance procedures, or who believe their rights have been violated, may have the right to pursue external remedies through government agencies, courts, or other dispute resolution mechanisms. The Company respects employees' rights to pursue external remedies and will cooperate with external investigations and proceedings as required by law. Employees who pursue external remedies are not subject to retaliation, and the Company will work constructively to resolve disputes through external processes where applicable.

11.19 Disciplinary Framework
11.19.1 Principles of Fair Disciplinary Action
— Disciplinary Action as a Corrective Tool. Disciplinary action is not designed to punish employees, but to correct behavior, address performance concerns, and maintain workplace standards. The Company's disciplinary framework is based on principles of fairness, consistency, proportionality, and due process. Disciplinary action is taken only where there is a legitimate basis, is proportionate to the seriousness of the conduct or performance concern, and provides employees with opportunities to improve and to respond to concerns.
— Progressive Discipline. The Company follows a progressive discipline approach that provides employees with opportunities to correct behavior or improve performance before more serious consequences are imposed. Progressive discipline typically begins with informal counseling or verbal warnings, progresses to written warnings, and may escalate to suspension, demotion, or termination of employment if concerns are not addressed. The specific progression depends on the nature and seriousness of the conduct or performance concern, and more serious violations may result in immediate termination without progressive steps.
11.19.2 Categories of Violations
— Minor Violations. Minor violations are instances of conduct or performance that do not meet expectations but do not constitute serious misconduct. Examples include isolated instances of tardiness, minor policy violations, or performance concerns that can be addressed through coaching. Minor violations are typically addressed through informal counseling, verbal warnings, or written warnings, and employees are given opportunities to improve before more serious consequences are imposed.
— Moderate Violations. Moderate violations are more serious instances of conduct or performance that require formal disciplinary action. Examples include repeated minor violations, more significant policy violations, or performance concerns that have not improved despite prior feedback. Moderate violations are typically addressed through written warnings, performance improvement plans, or other formal disciplinary actions, and may result in more serious consequences if not addressed.
— Serious and Gross Violations. Serious violations are significant instances of misconduct or performance failure that may result in immediate termination of employment. Examples include theft, fraud, harassment, discrimination, serious policy violations, or performance failures that cannot be addressed through improvement processes. Gross violations are the most serious forms of misconduct that fundamentally destroy the employment relationship, such as deliberate sabotage, violence, or other conduct that makes continued employment impossible. Serious and gross violations may result in immediate termination without notice or payment in lieu of notice, subject to applicable law.
11.19.3 Disciplinary Process
— Investigation and Due Process. Before imposing disciplinary action, the Company conducts a fair and thorough investigation of the conduct or performance concern, provides the employee with notice of the concern and an opportunity to respond, considers the employee's perspective and any mitigating circumstances, and makes a decision based on the facts and circumstances of the case. The disciplinary process is designed to be fair, transparent, and consistent, and employees are provided with opportunities to present their case and to respond to concerns.
— Disciplinary Outcomes and Appeals. Disciplinary action may take various forms, including warnings, performance improvement plans, suspension, demotion, or termination of employment, depending on the nature and seriousness of the violation. Employees who are subject to disciplinary action are informed of the action, the reasons for it, and their right to appeal. Appeals are reviewed by a senior manager or HR, and employees are provided with a fair opportunity to present their case and to have the decision reviewed.

The Company recognizes that mandatory training, however well-designed and well-delivered, is insufficient by itself to create and sustain a culture of fair employment practices. Fair employment practices must be a daily commitment — present in every employment decision, in every performance conversation, in every interaction between managers and employees, and in every aspect of the employment relationship. The Company will therefore supplement mandatory training with regular communications about employment practices, recognition of managers who demonstrate excellence in people management, feedback mechanisms that enable employees to raise concerns, and the creation of an organizational environment in which fair employment practices are genuinely valued, consistently applied, and never compromised for operational convenience.

11.21 Policy Compliance, Governance, and Review
11.21.1 Responsibility for Policy Compliance
— Every Employee's Personal Responsibility. Compliance with this Employment Policy is the personal and non-delegable responsibility of every employee without exception. Employees are responsible for reading and understanding this policy in full, for applying its standards in every aspect of their employment, for seeking guidance from HR when they encounter a situation that is not clearly covered by this policy or where they are uncertain about the correct course of action, for raising concerns through the appropriate channels without delay, and for completing all mandatory employment policy training within the required timeframes. Compliance with this policy is not optional, is not dependent on personal convenience, and is not subject to individual interpretation or selective application.
— Manager and Leadership Responsibility. Managers and leaders bear a heightened and additional responsibility for ensuring compliance with employment policies within their teams. They must model the employment standards expected of all employees in their own conduct at all times, must ensure that their team members complete mandatory training within the required timeframes, must create an environment in which team members feel safe and encouraged to raise concerns without fear of adverse consequence, must not create or condone workarounds that bypass employment standards for operational convenience, and must cooperate fully with any employment investigation or audit that involves their function or their team.
— HR Responsibility. HR is responsible for administering this policy, for providing employees and managers with the training, guidance, and support they need to comply with their obligations, for conducting employment investigations and disciplinary processes, for managing the grievance procedure, for ensuring that employment practices comply with applicable law, and for maintaining employment records and documentation. HR is available to answer questions, provide guidance, and assist employees and managers with employment-related matters.
11.21.2 Employee Acknowledgment
— Formal Acknowledgment Is Mandatory. All employees are required to formally acknowledge that they have read, understood, and agree to comply with this Employment Policy. Acknowledgment will be captured through the HRMS at the time of onboarding for all new employees and upon each substantive update to the policy for all existing employees. The acknowledgment record will be maintained in the employee's personnel file. Failure to complete the acknowledgment within the timeline specified by HR will be treated as a compliance concern and addressed accordingly. Acknowledgment of this policy does not substitute for completion of the mandatory employment policy training program — both requirements are independent and both must be fulfilled.
11.21.3 Annual Policy Review
— Regular Review to Maintain Relevance and Currency. This Employment Policy will be formally reviewed on an annual basis by HR in conjunction with Legal, or earlier if required by a material change in applicable employment law, a significant development in the Company's business environment or organizational structure, the identification of a gap in the current policy revealed by an employment issue, investigation finding, or legal development, or feedback from employees, managers, or external assessors that reveals an area for improvement or updating. All updates will be approved by the Chief Human Resources Officer in conjunction with Legal, communicated to all employees through the HRMS and official Company communication channels, and formally acknowledged by all employees within fifteen working days of communication. The Company's commitment to maintaining a current, rigorous, and genuinely effective Employment Policy is both a legal obligation and a fundamental expression of its commitment to fair, respectful, and successful employment relationships.
