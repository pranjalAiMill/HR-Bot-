1. Attendance Policy

1.1 Purpose and Organizational Philosophy
The purpose of this Attendance and Time Management Policy is to establish a comprehensive, clearly defined, and consistently applied framework that governs how all employees of the Company are expected to manage their time, fulfill their attendance obligations, and conduct themselves with professionalism in relation to their working hours.
This policy has been developed with two equally important objectives in mind. The first objective is operational. The Company, as an enterprise organization with complex internal operations, client-facing commitments, cross-functional dependencies, and multi-layered team structures, depends fundamentally on the predictable and consistent presence of its workforce. When employees are present, punctual, and available during their designated working hours, the organization functions efficiently. Deadlines are met, client expectations are honored, team collaboration is effective, and business performance is sustained. When attendance is inconsistent, unpredictable, or poorly managed, the consequences are real and far-reaching. Colleagues must absorb additional workload, managers must redirect attention toward coverage and crisis management rather than strategic leadership, clients experience disruptions in service quality, and the overall culture of the organization is gradually eroded by the message that professional commitments are optional.
The second objective of this policy is human and relational. The Company recognizes without reservation that its employees are human beings with lives, health challenges, family responsibilities, and unpredictable personal circumstances that will occasionally make it genuinely difficult to arrive on time, remain for the full working day, or be present at all. This policy is not designed to punish the inevitable realities of human life. It is designed to create a fair, transparent, and consistently applied set of expectations that allow the Company to manage its operations reliably while also treating employees with the dignity, respect, and flexibility that a progressive enterprise organization owes its people.
The philosophy underlying this policy is therefore one of mutual accountability. The Company commits to communicating its expectations clearly, applying its standards consistently and without favoritism, providing employees with the tools and systems they need to fulfill their attendance obligations, and treating each situation with appropriate judgment and proportionality. In return, employees are expected to take their attendance obligations seriously, to communicate proactively when circumstances arise that affect their ability to meet those obligations, and to approach this policy in the spirit of professional responsibility rather than minimal compliance.
This policy is not a static document. It is a living framework that will be reviewed, refined, and updated as the organization evolves, as workforce needs change, and as applicable legislation develops. Every provision within it has been carefully constructed to balance organizational need with individual fairness, and every employee is encouraged to read it fully, ask questions where clarification is needed, and engage with it as a professional partner rather than as a subject of control.

1.2 Scope and Applicability
- 1.2.1 Who This Policy Applies To
This policy applies universally and without exception to every individual engaged with the Company in any employment or engagement capacity. This includes permanent full-time employees who form the core of the Company's workforce, part-time employees engaged to work regular but reduced hours, employees on fixed-term or rolling contracts for specific projects or defined periods, interns and trainees who are engaged as part of structured learning or educational programs, and external consultants who work on-site at Company premises or who access Company systems and are expected to adhere to the Company's time management standards as part of their engagement terms.
The policy applies across all departments, business units, functional areas, and levels of organizational hierarchy, from the most junior team member to the most senior executive. No individual is exempt from the standards established in this policy on the grounds of their seniority, tenure, or professional standing unless a specific and formally documented accommodation has been approved in writing by Human Resources and countersigned by the relevant Business Head.
- 1.2.2 Where This Policy Applies
The policy applies equally to employees working on-site at Company premises, employees working from home under a formally approved remote work arrangement, employees working in a hybrid arrangement that combines office and remote work, and employees deputed to client sites, field assignments, or external locations. While the specific mechanisms through which attendance is recorded may differ across these different work arrangements, the underlying standards of professional availability, accountability, and timely communication apply uniformly and without distinction.
- 1.2.3 Relationship With Other Policies
This policy must be read in conjunction with the Company's Work From Home Policy referenced as HR-WFH-001, the Leave Policy referenced as HR-LEV-001, the Payroll and Compensation Policy referenced as HR-PAY-001, and the Code of Conduct Policy referenced as HR-COC-001. Together, these policies form an integrated framework governing the employee's professional obligations, entitlements, and conduct. In the event of any apparent conflict between this policy and any other Company policy, HR will determine the applicable provision based on the specific circumstances, and the employee should seek clarification through the HR Helpdesk before acting unilaterally.

1.3 Standard Working Hours
- 1.3.1 Definition of Standard Working Hours
The Company's standard working day runs from 9:00 AM to 6:00 PM, Monday through Friday, constituting a five-day working week. The total mandatory working time per week is 40 hours, calculated after excluding the one-hour lunch break that is provided daily to all employees. The lunch break, while important for employee well-being and sustained productivity, is not counted as part of the 40-hour working week and is not compensated as working time.
This structure has been deliberately established to align with the Company's operational requirements, its commitments to clients and business partners, the coordination needs of its internal teams, and the legislative standards governing working hours in the jurisdictions in which it operates. Employees are expected to understand that these hours are not arbitrary but are the foundation of the Company's ability to deliver consistently on its commitments.
- 1.3.2 Readiness to Work at the Start of the Day
Being at work means more than physically arriving at the premises or logging into a computer. It means being ready to commence productive professional activity at the designated start time. Employees are expected to be at their workstation, physically settled, mentally prepared, and genuinely ready to begin working at 9:00 AM. Activities such as making coffee, settling in, having informal conversations unrelated to work, or completing personal tasks after the official start time are not appropriate uses of the Company's working hours. The working day begins at 9:00 AM, and that is when productive professional activity is expected to start. This standard applies equally to on-site employees who must be seated at their workstation and to remote employees who must be logged into all required systems and communication platforms.
- 1.3.3 The Grace Period — Its Meaning and Limitations
The Company extends a grace period of 15 minutes to all employees, which means that attendance will be formally recorded as late only when an employee arrives after 9:15 AM. This grace period exists as a practical acknowledgment of the minor, occasional delays that are an unavoidable reality of daily life — a brief traffic disruption, a minor personal matter, or a momentary delay that no reasonable person could have anticipated or prevented.
It is critically important, however, that employees understand what the grace period is and what it is not. The grace period is a professional courtesy, not an entitlement. It is not a built-in extension of the start time. It is not permission to routinely arrive between 9:00 AM and 9:15 AM. Employees who consistently arrive at 9:10 AM or 9:14 AM every day are not arriving within a permitted window — they are consistently failing to meet their obligation to begin work at 9:00 AM, and this pattern will be recognized and addressed as such. The grace period is intended to absorb genuinely exceptional minor delays, and any employee who uses it as a regular feature of their daily schedule is misusing it and may be subject to a formal conversation with their manager despite not technically breaching the 9:15 AM threshold.
- 1.3.4 Lunch Break — Duration, Timing, and Obligations
The standard lunch break is provided between 1:00 PM and 2:00 PM and is one hour in duration. This break is provided to support employee well-being, rest, and sustained afternoon productivity. Employees are expected to use this time for personal rest and to return to their workstation and resume productive work at 2:00 PM promptly. Where operational requirements make the standard lunch break timing impractical for a particular team or role, alternative break timing arrangements may be agreed between the reporting manager and the employee, with HR informed and the arrangement documented in the HRMS.
Extending the lunch break beyond one hour without prior managerial approval is not permitted and will be treated as time absent from work. Repeated instances of extended lunch breaks without authorization will be addressed through the same progressive disciplinary mechanisms that apply to other attendance violations. Employees are encouraged to fully utilize their lunch break for genuine rest and personal recharging, as this is beneficial both for their own sustained performance and for the overall productivity culture of the organization.
- 1.3.5 End of the Working Day
The standard working day concludes at 6:00 PM. Employees are expected to remain at their workstation and continue productive professional activity until 6:00 PM. Packing up, shutting down systems, and preparing to leave should begin only at or after 6:00 PM. Any departure from the premises or disconnection from Company systems before 6:00 PM without prior managerial approval constitutes an early departure and is governed by the provisions of Section 1.6 of this policy.
Where an employee is required to work beyond 6:00 PM, this must be pre-approved by the reporting manager and documented appropriately in the HRMS. Overtime provisions, where applicable, will be governed by applicable labor legislation and the Company's internal policies relating to overtime compensation as detailed in Section 1.10 of this policy.
- 1.3.6 Variations in Working Hours for Specific Roles
Certain roles within the organization may require working hours that differ from the standard 9:00 AM to 6:00 PM schedule due to the nature of the work, client time zone requirements, shift-based operational structures, or other legitimate business reasons. Where a specific role carries a different working hours arrangement, this will be clearly communicated in the employee's offer letter and employment contract. Employees in such roles are bound by the working hours specified in their individual contracts and must adhere to the attendance standards of this policy as adapted to their specific hours. All other provisions of this policy apply equally to employees with non-standard working hours unless specifically modified in their contract or in a separate written agreement countersigned by HR.

1.4 Attendance Marking and Time Tracking
- 1.4.1 The Importance of Accurate Attendance Marking
Accurate and timely attendance marking is not a procedural formality. It is a professional obligation with direct consequences for the employee's compensation, leave balances, and compliance record. The Company's attendance data forms the basis for payroll processing, leave management, workforce planning, statutory compliance reporting, and organizational analytics. When attendance is marked inaccurately, whether through negligence, oversight, or deliberate misrepresentation, the integrity of these downstream processes is compromised, creating financial errors, compliance risks, and operational disruptions that have a real cost to the organization and to colleagues. For this reason, the Company treats attendance marking with the same seriousness as any other professional obligation.
- 1.4.2 Attendance Marking for On-Site Employees
All employees working on-site at Company premises are required to mark their attendance using the biometric device installed at the designated entry and exit points of the premises. Attendance must be marked at the beginning of the working day upon arrival and again at the end of the working day upon departure. This dual-marking system — commonly referred to as in-punch and out-punch — is essential to accurately capture the employee's actual working hours and cannot be substituted with any informal alternative.
Employees must use their own biometric credentials to mark their attendance. No employee may mark attendance on behalf of another employee under any circumstances whatsoever. Proxy attendance marking — whether by swiping a colleague's card, using another individual's biometric data with their cooperation, or any other mechanism designed to record an attendance entry that does not correspond to the employee's actual presence — constitutes a deliberate falsification of official Company records. This is a serious act of misconduct and will be treated as a gross misconduct violation, subject to the most severe disciplinary consequences up to and including immediate termination of employment, regardless of the seniority or tenure of the individual involved.
- 1.4.3 Attendance Marking for Remote Employees
Employees working from home under a formally approved Work From Home arrangement are required to mark their attendance through the Company's official Human Resource Management System portal. The HRMS portal provides a remote attendance marking facility that records the employee's in-time and out-time on each working day. Remote attendance must be marked at the beginning of the working day within the window specified by HR and again at the end of the working day within the applicable close-of-business window.
The responsibility for marking remote attendance accurately and consistently rests entirely with the employee. The fact that the employee is working from a location other than Company premises does not dilute or modify this obligation in any way. Remote attendance marking is as mandatory as on-site biometric marking, and failure to mark attendance remotely will be treated with exactly the same consequences as failure to mark attendance on-site.
- 1.4.4 Attendance Marking for Field and Client-Site Employees
Employees who are engaged on field assignments, client visits, customer site work, or any other form of deputation to an external location on a given day are required to mark their attendance in the HRMS with the specific location details of their external assignment. In addition to marking attendance in the HRMS, the employee must inform their reporting manager at the start of the working day — either through email, the Company's approved messaging platform, or a phone call — of the location they will be working from and the nature of the assignment. The reporting manager is responsible for validating and approving the field attendance entries submitted by their team members within the HRMS within 24 hours of the entry being made.
Employees who regularly work on client sites or in the field should establish a routine of daily attendance marking and manager communication as a non-negotiable professional habit, regardless of how routine or unremarkable the field activity may be on a given day. Consistency and discipline in attendance marking are always required, irrespective of the familiarity of the routine.
- 1.4.5 System Failure and Technical Malfunction Procedure
Occasions may arise where the biometric device or the HRMS portal is unavailable due to a technical malfunction, a power failure, a system outage, or scheduled maintenance. In such situations, the employee is required to take immediate and documented action. The employee must notify their reporting manager via email or the Company's approved communication platform at the earliest possible opportunity on the day of the malfunction, explicitly stating that they were unable to mark attendance due to the technical issue. The employee must simultaneously notify the HR department using the same or a parallel communication, providing their full name, employee ID number, the date of the issue, and a brief description of the technical problem encountered.
Where available, the employee should seek and retain any supporting evidence that corroborates their presence at work during the affected period. This may include email correspondence timestamped during working hours, access control logs from building entry and exit systems, calendar entries reflecting meetings attended, project system activity logs, or any other verifiable digital record that confirms the employee's professional activity during the affected time.
The HR team will make every reasonable effort to investigate the technical issue through available system records and to correct the attendance entry for the affected employee based on verified information. Employees must understand, however, that this provision exists exclusively to address genuine, confirmed technical failures and must not be misused as a mechanism to retroactively fabricate or alter attendance records. Any employee found to be misusing the system failure provision to compensate for an actual absence or late arrival will be subject to disciplinary action for falsification of records in addition to the underlying attendance violation.
- 1.4.6 Attendance Data Integrity and Anti-Manipulation Policy
The integrity of attendance data is of fundamental importance to the Company's operations, compliance, and culture. The Company employs a range of technical controls, periodic audits, and management oversight mechanisms to protect the accuracy and reliability of its attendance records. Any attempt to manipulate, falsify, circumvent, or interfere with the attendance marking system — whether by proxy marking, system manipulation, unauthorized access to the HRMS, collusion with colleagues to cover absences, or any other means — will be treated as a matter of the most serious disciplinary gravity. Such conduct represents a breach of trust, a violation of the Code of Conduct, and in some cases may constitute fraud. The Company will not hesitate to pursue appropriate disciplinary action, up to and including termination of employment and referral to law enforcement authorities where the circumstances warrant it.

1.5 Late Arrivals — Comprehensive Policy, Procedure, and Consequences
- 1.5.1 Definition of a Late Arrival
A late arrival is formally defined as any instance where an employee's recorded attendance entry for the beginning of the working day falls after 9:15 AM. The 15-minute grace period means that arrivals up to and including 9:15 AM will not be recorded as late. Any arrival recorded at 9:16 AM or later will be recorded in the HRMS as a late arrival for that day, and the provisions of this section will apply.
The definition of a late arrival is objective and system-based. The biometric device and HRMS system record the actual time of attendance marking without adjustment or interpretation. Subjective assessments of whether an arrival felt close enough to the start time are not relevant. If the recorded time is 9:16 AM or later, the day is recorded as a late arrival. This objectivity ensures that the policy is applied consistently and without bias across all employees at every level of the organization.
- 1.5.2 The Obligation to Communicate Before or During a Late Arrival
When an employee is aware, at any point before or during their commute, that they will arrive after 9:15 AM, they are required to proactively notify their reporting manager of this fact. This notification must be made before the employee's expected arrival time, not after. The notification should be made via email to the manager's official Company email address, or through the Company's approved internal messaging platform. The message should clearly communicate that the employee will be arriving late, the approximate time of expected arrival if known, and the reason for the delay.
This requirement to notify the manager is not merely procedural. It reflects a basic standard of professional courtesy and operational accountability. When a manager knows that a team member will be late, they can plan accordingly — reassigning time-sensitive tasks, communicating adjusted timelines to clients, and making informed decisions about workload coverage. Failure to notify the reporting manager of a late arrival will be treated as an aggravating factor when the attendance pattern is reviewed. It will not remove the late arrival from the employee's record, and it will reflect unfavorably on the employee's overall attitude toward professional accountability when the matter is formally reviewed.
- 1.5.3 Leave Deduction for Accumulated Late Arrivals
The Company applies a structured and transparent leave deduction mechanism to manage the accumulation of late arrivals within a calendar month. Every three late arrivals recorded within a single calendar month will result in the automatic deduction of one day of Casual Leave from the employee's leave balance in the HRMS. This deduction is applied at the close of each calendar month as part of the monthly attendance review process.
A single, isolated late arrival is a minor event that, while not ideal, does not in itself represent a significant breach of professional standards. However, when an employee arrives late on three or more occasions within a single month, the cumulative impact becomes material. The leave deduction reflects this cumulative impact in a proportionate and transparent way that is communicated clearly to the employee in advance.
If, at the time the deduction is applied, the employee does not have an available Casual Leave balance to absorb the deduction, the equivalent deduction will be applied as Leave Without Pay. Leave Without Pay has a direct and immediate financial consequence for the employee, resulting in a corresponding reduction in the net salary credited for that month. Employees should therefore be aware that repeated or accumulated late arrivals carry not only a professional consequence but also a direct financial consequence.
- 1.5.4 Escalated Consequence for Five or More Late Arrivals in a Month
When an employee records five or more late arrivals within a single calendar month, the situation moves beyond the routine leave deduction mechanism and into formal disciplinary review territory. Five late arrivals in a single month represents a clear and documented pattern of repeated failure to meet a basic professional obligation and requires a structured organizational response.
Upon identification of five or more late arrivals in a month, HR will notify the reporting manager and initiate a formal review process. The reporting manager is required to schedule a documented one-on-one conversation with the employee within five working days of receiving the notification. The purpose of this conversation is to understand whether underlying circumstances are contributing to the pattern, to communicate the Company's expectations clearly and unambiguously, and to create a formal record that the concern has been identified and addressed. The outcome, including any commitments made and support measures agreed upon, will be documented and retained in the employee's personnel file.
- 1.5.5 Medical and Emergency Exceptions — Compassionate Consideration
The Company fully acknowledges that genuine medical emergencies, acute illness, significant public transport disruptions, natural disasters, family emergencies, and other extraordinary circumstances beyond the employee's control may occasionally cause late arrivals that are entirely unavoidable. When such circumstances arise, the employee is responsible for communicating the situation to their reporting manager and HR as early as possible on the affected day, providing as much factual information as possible and, where reasonably available, supporting documentation such as a medical certificate, a hospital visit record, or any other verifiable evidence.
HR will review such cases individually and with appropriate sensitivity and judgment. Where the circumstances are verified, clearly extraordinary, and genuinely beyond the employee's control, the late arrival may be regularized without penalty at HR's discretion in consultation with the reporting manager. However, employees are expected not to abuse this provision by characterizing routine or foreseeable delays as emergencies, as doing so constitutes a misrepresentation of fact and will be treated accordingly.

1.6 Early Departures — Authorization, Procedure, and Consequences
- 1.6.1 Definition of an Early Departure
An early departure is any instance where an employee leaves their workstation and exits Company premises, disconnects from Company systems while working remotely, or otherwise ceases professional availability before 6:00 PM on any regular working day. Any departure that occurs before 6:00 PM, regardless of how small the difference from the official end time, constitutes an early departure for the purposes of this policy and requires the procedural steps described in this section.
- 1.6.2 Advance Request and Approval Requirement
The Company requires that employees who need to leave before 6:00 PM submit a request for early departure to their reporting manager at least two hours before the intended departure time. This two-hour advance notice gives the manager adequate time to assess the workload implications, arrange for critical pending tasks to be reassigned or deferred appropriately, communicate the situation to colleagues or clients who may be affected, and make an informed decision about whether to approve the request.
The request must be submitted through the HRMS leave and attendance management module or through the Company's approved communication platform, with a clear record including the date, time, reason for the early departure, intended departure time, and relevant details about the status of pending work. The manager's approval must be documented in the HRMS to create a clear and auditable record of the authorization.
- 1.6.3 Emergency Early Departure
Genuine emergencies do not always allow for advance notice. In a situation involving a genuine personal or family emergency — a sudden serious illness, a medical emergency affecting an immediate family member, an accident, or a significant personal crisis — the employee is expected to prioritize the emergency and communicate with their manager as soon as it is practically possible. The employee should send a brief message to their reporting manager through any available channel to inform them that they are leaving due to an emergency. The formal early departure documentation in the HRMS should be completed by the employee, or on their behalf by HR with the employee's confirmation, on the employee's return to work.
Managers are expected to approach genuine emergency early departures with empathy and flexibility. The policy does not expect employees to delay responding to a genuine emergency in order to complete administrative procedures. What the policy does expect is that communication happens as soon as reasonably possible and that formal documentation follows in a timely manner upon the employee's return.
-1.6.4 Half-Day Leave Deduction for Significant Early Departures
Where an early departure results in the employee being present for fewer than four hours of the standard working day, the absence for the remaining portion of the day may be treated as a half-day leave deduction, subject to the reporting manager's assessment and HR's discretion. Whether the deduction is applied as Casual Leave or Leave Without Pay will depend on the employee's available leave balance and the circumstances of the early departure. This provision ensures that there is no financial or administrative ambiguity when an employee's early departure is substantial enough to constitute a half-day absence.
- 1.6.5 Consequences of Frequent or Unapproved Early Departures
Occasional early departures that are genuinely necessary and properly authorized are an accepted part of managing the intersection of professional and personal life in a progressive workplace. However, employees who frequently request early departures or who depart early without authorization on multiple occasions create a pattern that is inconsistent with professional accountability and operational reliability. Such patterns will be identified through the monthly attendance review process and addressed through the progressive disciplinary framework described in Section 1.9 of this policy.

1.7 Unauthorized Absence and No-Show Policy
- 1.7.1 Definition of Unauthorized Absence
An unauthorized absence is any instance where an employee fails to attend work on a scheduled working day without having obtained prior leave approval through the HRMS and without having communicated a valid reason to their reporting manager and HR. An unauthorized absence is a more serious matter than a simple late arrival or an approved absence, because it combines both the fact of the absence and the failure to communicate or seek approval, creating a dual breach of the employee's professional obligations.
An unauthorized absence is distinct from a Sick Leave absence, which, while unplanned, involves communication to the manager and formal leave application through the HRMS. An unauthorized absence is also distinct from a genuine emergency situation in which the employee was physically incapable of communicating. The Company will always consider the specific circumstances of each case before making a determination about whether an absence was genuinely unauthorized or whether there were extenuating circumstances that justify a different treatment.
- 1.7.2 No-Show Without Communication
A no-show is a specific category of unauthorized absence that occurs when an employee fails to appear for work on a scheduled working day and also fails to make any communication whatsoever to their manager or HR about the absence before or during the working day. A no-show is treated with particular seriousness because it compounds the disruption of the absence itself with a complete failure of professional communication and accountability.
When an employee is a no-show on a given day, the reporting manager is required to attempt to contact the employee through all available channels — phone, email, and any emergency contact information on file — within the first two hours of the working day. If the employee cannot be reached and provides no explanation by midday, the manager must formally notify HR. HR will then initiate the standard unauthorized absence process, which involves documenting the no-show in the employee's attendance record, applying Leave Without Pay for the day, and initiating a formal conversation with the employee upon their return.
- 1.7.3 Consequences of Unauthorized Absence
Every unauthorized absence will result in the application of Leave Without Pay for the duration of the absence, regardless of whether the employee has an available paid leave balance. The Company will not convert an unauthorized absence into a paid leave deduction retroactively after the fact, as doing so would undermine the integrity of the leave approval process and the professional obligation to seek approval in advance. In addition to the financial consequence of Leave Without Pay, repeated unauthorized absences will be addressed through the progressive disciplinary process described in Section 1.9, and may also be taken into account in the employee's formal performance review as evidence of professional reliability and commitment.
- 1.7.4 Abandonment of Employment
In exceptional cases where an employee is absent without any communication for a consecutive period of five or more working days, and where all reasonable attempts to contact the employee have been made and documented without success, the Company may treat the situation as a potential abandonment of employment. In such circumstances, HR will initiate a formal investigation, attempt to contact the employee through all available means including registered post to the employee's last known address, and seek to understand whether any extraordinary personal or health circumstances are responsible for the absence. The Company will not make a determination of abandonment without first exhausting all reasonable avenues of communication and investigation. Where abandonment is ultimately confirmed, the Company reserves the right to terminate the employment relationship in accordance with applicable labor legislation.

1.8 Habitual Absenteeism — Definition, Identification, and Response
- 1.8.1 Definition of Habitual Absenteeism
Habitual absenteeism is defined as a pattern of frequent, repeated absences from work that, when viewed in aggregate, represents a level of attendance that is materially below the standard expected by the Company and that has a demonstrable impact on team performance, operational continuity, and client delivery. Habitual absenteeism is distinct from a single prolonged absence due to a serious illness or an approved leave of absence. It refers specifically to a pattern of short, repeated absences that individually may appear minor but collectively constitute a significant and ongoing attendance problem.
The Company uses specific measurable thresholds to identify habitual absenteeism. An employee who is absent for eight or more days within a rolling 12-month period, excluding approved annual leave, public holidays, and formally approved extended leave such as maternity, paternity, or medical leave, will be flagged by the HRMS for habitual absenteeism review. An employee who is absent on more than three separate occasions within a single calendar quarter will similarly be flagged for review. These thresholds are not punitive targets but are reasonable benchmarks that distinguish normal, expected levels of occasional absence from a pattern that requires organizational attention and support.
- 1.8.2 The Bradford Factor — A Diagnostic Tool
The Company may utilize the Bradford Factor as one of several diagnostic tools in identifying and assessing patterns of habitual absenteeism. The Bradford Factor is a formula used widely in human resources management to give greater weight to frequent, short absences relative to a smaller number of longer absences, on the basis that short, frequent absences are typically more disruptive to team operations than a single longer absence. The Bradford Factor is calculated using the formula: the number of separate absence occasions in a rolling 12-month period, squared, multiplied by the total number of working days absent in the same period.
The Bradford Factor is used as a diagnostic indicator only and does not by itself determine any disciplinary outcome. It is one piece of information that helps HR and managers identify patterns that may warrant further investigation and support. The Bradford Factor score will be reviewed alongside the specific circumstances of each absence, the employee's overall attendance history, and any relevant personal or health context before any formal action is taken.
- 1.8.3 The Return-to-Work Interview
Every time an employee returns to work following an absence of any duration — including a single day — the reporting manager is expected to conduct a brief, informal return-to-work interview with the employee. The return-to-work interview is not an interrogation or a disciplinary exercise. It is a structured, supportive conversation designed to welcome the employee back, check on their well-being, ensure that they are fit and ready to resume their duties, update them on any developments that occurred during their absence, and understand the circumstances of the absence so that appropriate support can be provided if needed.
The return-to-work interview also serves as a gentle but consistent signal that the Company is attentive to attendance and values the presence of every member of its team. Research in human resources management consistently demonstrates that the implementation of return-to-work interviews leads to measurable reductions in absenteeism rates, because employees are aware that their absence will be acknowledged and discussed rather than simply recorded and ignored. Managers are required to complete a brief return-to-work record in the HRMS following each return-to-work interview, noting the date, the reason for the absence as provided by the employee, and any action or support agreed upon.
- 1.8.4 Organizational Response to Habitual Absenteeism
When an employee's attendance data triggers the habitual absenteeism threshold, HR will initiate a formal attendance review meeting with the employee and their reporting manager. The purpose of this meeting is not immediately disciplinary but is first and foremost investigative and supportive. HR and the manager will seek to understand whether the pattern of absences reflects an underlying health condition, a personal or family circumstance, a mental health challenge, a workplace relationship issue, a commute or logistical problem, or any other factor that the Company may be able to address through appropriate support, adjustment, or accommodation.
Where the review reveals a genuine underlying cause that the Company can reasonably accommodate — for example, a chronic health condition that may qualify the employee for a reasonable adjustment, or a temporary personal crisis that may be addressed through a short period of flexible working — the Company will work with the employee to identify and implement appropriate support measures. Where the review reveals no genuine underlying cause and the pattern reflects a simple absence of professional commitment to attendance standards, the progressive disciplinary process described in Section 1.9 will be initiated.

1.9 Progressive Disciplinary Action for Attendance Violations
- 1.9.1 Philosophy of Progressive Discipline
The Company's approach to disciplinary action for attendance violations is grounded in the belief that most employees, when faced with clear and consistent feedback about performance concerns, are capable of and willing to make the necessary adjustments. Progressive discipline is therefore the Company's chosen methodology — a graduated sequence of responses that begins with communication and support and escalates only when the employee demonstrates a sustained inability or unwillingness to meet the required standards despite being given a fair and reasonable opportunity to do so.
The progressive disciplinary process is not intended to be punitive or to rush toward termination. Its purpose is to create a structured, documented, and fair process that gives the employee every reasonable opportunity to correct their attendance behavior while also protecting the Company's legitimate operational interests. Every step of the disciplinary process will be conducted in accordance with the principles of natural justice. The employee will be informed of the specific concern in clear and factual terms, given a full and genuine opportunity to present their perspective and explain any contributing circumstances, and all communications and decisions will be documented formally and retained in the employee's personnel file.
- 1.9.2 First Violation — Verbal Warning
The first formal step in the progressive disciplinary process for attendance violations is the issuance of a verbal warning by the reporting manager, conducted in the presence of an HR representative where the seriousness of the situation warrants it. Despite being referred to as a verbal warning, this is not an informal conversation. It is a formal meeting with a specific and documented purpose.
Before the meeting, the manager and HR will review the employee's attendance records in full and prepare a clear summary of the specific violations being addressed — the dates, times, and nature of the attendance failures. During the meeting, the manager will present this summary factually and without editorializing. The employee will be given adequate time and space to respond, to explain their perspective and any circumstances that may be contributing to the attendance pattern, and to ask questions. The manager will clearly communicate the attendance standard expected going forward and explain that continued violations will result in escalated disciplinary action. Following the meeting, a written record of the verbal warning will be prepared by HR and retained in the employee's personnel file. The employee will be asked to sign an acknowledgment confirming that the meeting took place and that they have been informed of the concern. This signature is an acknowledgment of receipt and communication, not an admission of fault.
- 1.9.3 Second Violation — Formal Written Warning
If attendance concerns persist after the verbal warning has been issued, a formal written warning will be issued by HR in consultation with the reporting manager. The formal written warning is a significantly more serious action than the verbal warning and represents the Company's formal documented position that the employee's attendance is unsatisfactory.
The written warning will be a formal document, prepared by HR on Company letterhead, that clearly states the specific violations that have continued since the verbal warning, the content of the verbal warning previously issued, the attendance standard expected, the period within which improvement must be demonstrated, and the explicit statement that failure to demonstrate the required improvement will result in further disciplinary escalation. The written warning will be issued to the employee in person in a formal meeting, the employee will be required to sign an acknowledgment of receipt, and a copy will be retained in the employee's personnel file. The employee will be provided with a copy for their own records.
- 1.9.4 Third Violation — Final Written Warning and Performance Improvement Plan
If attendance concerns persist after the formal written warning, a final written warning will be issued and a formal Attendance Performance Improvement Plan will be initiated simultaneously. The final written warning is the last formal warning stage before employment consequences and will clearly document the full history of the attendance concern, the previous warnings issued, the improvement period provided, and the specific ways in which the employee has continued to fall short.
The Attendance Performance Improvement Plan will specify the precise attendance standard that must be achieved over a defined monitoring period of not less than 30 days and not more than 90 days, the specific support measures the Company is making available to the employee, and an unambiguous statement of the consequence of failing to achieve the required standard within the monitoring period. The employee will be required to formally sign the PIP to confirm that they have read, understood, and received a copy of the document.
- 1.9.5 Fourth Violation — Consideration of Employment Termination
If the employee fails to demonstrate the required improvement within the PIP monitoring period, or if a material attendance violation occurs during the PIP period, the Company will convene a formal disciplinary hearing to consider whether termination of employment is the appropriate consequence. The disciplinary hearing will be conducted in accordance with the Company's formal disciplinary procedure and all applicable principles of natural justice and labor law.
At the hearing, the employee will be provided with a full written summary of the complete history of the attendance concern and the disciplinary process followed. The employee will be given a full and genuine opportunity to present their case, to be accompanied by a colleague or employee representative if they wish, and to raise any mitigating circumstances or new information. The outcome will be determined objectively, based on the totality of the evidence and the employee's response, and communicated to the employee in writing within five working days of the hearing. Where termination is determined to be the appropriate outcome, it will be carried out in full compliance with the employee's contractual notice provisions and all applicable labor legislation.

1.10 Compensatory Off and Overtime Provisions
- 1.10.1 Understanding Compensatory Off
Compensatory Off, commonly referred to as Comp Off, is the provision through which an employee who has been formally required to work on a designated holiday, a weekend, or beyond their standard working hours on a regular working day is entitled to claim an equivalent period of paid time off in lieu of the additional time worked. The Company recognizes that asking employees to contribute time beyond their contractual working hours is a significant request, and the Compensatory Off provision is the Company's formal acknowledgment of and response to that contribution.
Compensatory Off is not an automatic entitlement that arises simply because an employee worked additional hours on their own initiative. It is a structured, pre-approved benefit that applies specifically when additional or weekend work has been formally requested and approved by the reporting manager for a documented operational reason. Employees who work beyond their standard hours voluntarily, without a prior request or approval from their manager, are not automatically entitled to Compensatory Off for that additional time.
- 1.10.2 Eligibility Criteria for Compensatory Off
To be eligible for Compensatory Off, three conditions must be satisfied. First, the additional or weekend work must have been formally requested by the reporting manager in writing through the HRMS or the Company's approved communication platform, with the operational necessity documented at the time of the request. Second, the employee must have actually performed the work as requested, and this must be verifiable through HRMS records, project logs, output records, or other documented evidence. Third, the additional work must have amounted to a minimum of four hours on a given day to qualify for a half-day Comp Off, or a minimum of eight hours to qualify for a full-day Comp Off. Work of less than four hours on a given day may not qualify for Comp Off at all, though this will be assessed at the discretion of the reporting manager and HR based on the specific circumstances.
- 1.10.3 Process for Claiming Compensatory Off
An employee who has performed approved additional or weekend work must submit a Compensatory Off application through the HRMS within five working days of the date on which the additional work was performed. The application must reference the specific date and occasion of the additional work, confirm that the work was manager-approved, and identify the proposed date on which the employee wishes to avail the Comp Off. The proposed date must be agreed upon with the reporting manager and must fall within 30 calendar days of the date the Comp Off was earned. Compensatory Off that is not claimed and availed within 30 days of the date it was earned will lapse and will not be carried forward or converted to any other form of leave or financial compensation. Managers are responsible for ensuring that Comp Off approvals are granted fairly, recorded accurately in the HRMS, and availed within the permitted timeframe.
- 1.10.4 Overtime Work — Authorization and Compensation
Overtime is defined as any work performed beyond the standard 40-hour working week, or beyond the standard 9:00 AM to 6:00 PM working day on a regular weekday, that has been formally pre-approved by the reporting manager. The Company does not encourage or expect employees to routinely work overtime. The expectation is that the standard working hours are sufficient for employees to fulfill their professional responsibilities when time is managed effectively and workloads are appropriately distributed and prioritized.
However, the Company acknowledges that there are legitimate business situations in which overtime work may be necessary and unavoidable. In such situations, the reporting manager must approve the overtime formally and in advance, document the business reason for the requirement, and ensure that the employee is aware of the compensation arrangement — whether Compensatory Off, overtime pay, or another agreed arrangement — before the overtime work begins. Overtime compensation will be governed by applicable labor legislation and the Company's internal compensation policies.
Employees must not work overtime on their own initiative without prior managerial approval and then claim compensation for that overtime. Similarly, managers must not pressure or expect employees to routinely work beyond their standard hours without formal authorization, appropriate compensation, and a genuine operational justification. The Company is committed to a culture in which the standard working hours are respected as the norm and overtime is the exception rather than the expectation.

1.11 Attendance and Leave Interaction
- 1.11.1 Understanding the Relationship Between Attendance and Leave
Attendance and leave are two interdependent dimensions of the employee's professional obligations. Attendance governs the employee's obligation to be present and available during their designated working hours. Leave governs the employee's entitlement to authorized, planned, or emergency time away from work. Understanding how these two elements interact is essential for employees to manage their professional obligations effectively and to avoid inadvertent attendance violations or leave balance discrepancies.
An employee who is absent from work on any day that has not been designated as a public holiday is considered to be on leave for that day. If the absence is covered by an approved and available leave balance — whether Annual Leave, Casual Leave, Sick Leave, or another approved leave type — then the absence is a formally authorized leave day and will not be treated as an attendance violation. If the absence is not covered by an approved leave balance, or if leave was not sought in advance where advance notice was practicable, the absence will be treated as Leave Without Pay for the affected day.
- 1.11.2 Leave Must Be Applied For and Approved Before the Absence
Except in the case of genuine emergencies or sudden illness, leave must be applied for through the HRMS and formally approved by the reporting manager before the absence begins. Employees who take leave without seeking prior approval — on the assumption that approval will be granted retroactively — are taking an unauthorized absence for the duration of the unapproved period. Retroactive leave approval is granted at the discretion of the reporting manager and HR and is not guaranteed. Employees should never assume that leave will be approved retroactively and should always seek approval in advance wherever circumstances allow them to do so.
- 1.11.3 Sandwich Leave Rule
The Company applies a Sandwich Leave Rule to manage the interaction between weekends, public holidays, and adjacent leave days. Under the Sandwich Leave Rule, if an employee takes leave on the working day immediately before a public holiday or weekend and also takes leave on the working day immediately after the same public holiday or weekend, the intervening non-working days — the weekend days or the public holiday — will be counted as leave days and deducted from the employee's applicable leave balance. This rule exists to prevent the systematic extension of leave periods by strategically sandwiching non-working days between approved leave days, which, if left unaddressed, would significantly erode the Company's effective working capacity during peak periods. The Sandwich Leave Rule will be applied consistently and communicated to employees through the HRMS at the time of leave approval where the rule is triggered.
- 1.11.4 Leave Balance Management and Planning
Employees are responsible for monitoring their own leave balances through the HRMS on a regular basis and for planning their leave within the boundaries of their available entitlement throughout the leave year. Employees who exhaust their leave balance before the end of the leave year and subsequently need to take further time off will have any additional absences treated as Leave Without Pay, which carries a financial consequence in the form of a salary deduction. Employees are therefore encouraged to plan their leave thoughtfully throughout the year, to use leave in a balanced and consistent manner rather than accumulating it all and using it in a single extended period, and to communicate their leave plans to their manager early to enable effective team workload planning.

1.12 Flexible Working Arrangements and Attendance
- 1.12.1 Flexible Working as an Organizational Offering
The Company recognizes that a one-size-fits-all approach to working hours may not serve all employees or all business contexts equally well. Where the nature of a role, the requirements of the business, and the individual circumstances of an employee make a flexible working arrangement operationally viable and mutually beneficial, the Company is open to considering and approving such arrangements on a structured, formal, and time-limited basis.
Flexible working arrangements may include adjusted start and finish times that differ from the standard 9:00 AM to 6:00 PM schedule while maintaining the required 40 hours per week, compressed working weeks in which the employee works their standard 40 hours over four days rather than five, annualized hours arrangements in which working time is calculated and managed over a full year rather than a weekly basis, and job-sharing arrangements in which two part-time employees share the responsibilities and working hours of a single full-time role.
- 1.12.2 Application and Approval Process for Flexible Working
Flexible working arrangements are not an automatic entitlement. They are a discretionary accommodation that the Company may offer where the arrangement is consistent with business needs, does not adversely affect team performance, client service, or operational continuity, and is supported by a demonstrable business case. Employees who wish to request a flexible working arrangement must submit a formal written request to their reporting manager, clearly describing the proposed arrangement, the duration for which the arrangement is sought, the impact on their role responsibilities and team collaboration, and the reasons why they believe the arrangement is operationally viable.
The manager will assess the request in consultation with HR and will communicate a decision in writing within 15 working days of the formal request being submitted. If approved, the arrangement will be documented in a formal flexible working agreement that specifies the terms, the duration, the review date, and any conditions attached to the approval. Flexible working arrangements are subject to periodic review and may be modified or withdrawn if business needs change or if the arrangement is not working as intended.
- 1.12.3 Attendance Obligations Under Flexible Working Arrangements
Employees working under a formally approved flexible working arrangement are subject to the same attendance standards as employees working standard hours, adapted to reflect the specific hours agreed in their flexible working agreement. The obligation to mark attendance accurately, to communicate absences and late arrivals proactively, to be available during core hours where applicable, and to comply with all other provisions of this policy applies in full to flexible workers. Flexible working arrangements do not exempt employees from attendance obligations — they simply modify the specific hours to which those obligations apply.

1.13 Attendance During the Probationary Period
- 1.13.1 Elevated Expectations During Probation
The probationary period is the most critical phase of any new employee's journey with the Company. It is the period during which the employee's skills, performance, professionalism, conduct, and cultural alignment are all being actively evaluated by their manager and HR. Every professional behavior demonstrated during probation — including attendance — contributes to the overall assessment of the employee's suitability for a long-term role within the organization.
For this reason, the Company holds probationary employees to an elevated standard of attendance and punctuality. Probationary employees are expected to be present on every scheduled working day, to arrive on time consistently, to depart at the end of the working day, and to demonstrate through their attendance behavior that they are reliable, committed, and serious about their professional obligations. The Company understands that probationary employees are also going through a period of adjustment, learning, and sometimes personal stress, and it approaches probation attendance concerns with appropriate sensitivity. However, it cannot and will not overlook attendance patterns during probation that would be unacceptable for any permanent employee, simply because the employee is new to the organization.
- 1.13.2 Attendance Monitoring During Probation
HR and the reporting manager will monitor attendance during the probationary period with particular attention and will review the attendance data at the 30-day check-in meeting and the 60-day check-in meeting that are standard components of the probation process. Any attendance concerns identified at these check-in points will be discussed with the employee directly, and the feedback will be formally documented as part of the probation review record. This gives the employee a clear, structured opportunity to understand the concern and to demonstrate improvement before the formal probation decision is made at the 60 to 90 day review.
- 1.13.3 Consequences of Poor Attendance During Probation
Poor attendance during the probationary period will be taken into account in the overall probationary assessment. Depending on the severity and frequency of the attendance concerns, the probationary period may be extended beyond its original duration, with the specific attendance improvements required for confirmation clearly communicated to the employee in writing. In cases where the attendance during probation has been significantly and repeatedly deficient and where the employee has not demonstrated a genuine and sustained improvement following feedback, the Company may consider early termination of employment in accordance with the probationary notice provisions and applicable labor legislation.

1.14 Remote Work Attendance — Specific Provisions
- 1.14.1 Attendance Obligations Apply Equally to Remote Employees
The Company's decision to approve a Work From Home arrangement for an employee reflects a genuine trust in that employee's professional discipline and self-management capability. It is not, under any interpretation, a relaxation of attendance obligations. Remote employees are subject to exactly the same attendance requirements as on-site employees, and all provisions of this policy apply to them in full. The only difference between on-site and remote attendance is the mechanism through which attendance is marked — biometric device on-site versus HRMS portal remotely. The underlying obligation and the consequences for non-compliance are identical.
- 1.14.2 Core Hours for Remote Employees
Remote employees are required to be genuinely available and responsive during the Company's designated core business hours, which run from 10:00 AM to 4:00 PM on all standard working days. Being available during core hours means being reachable by telephone, email, and the Company's approved messaging platform, actively participating in all scheduled meetings and calls, and being in a position to respond to reasonable communications from colleagues, managers, and stakeholders within a timeframe that reflects genuine availability and professional engagement. Core hours exist to ensure that despite the physical separation of remote working, the organization can function as a cohesive and coordinated unit.
- 1.14.3 Technology and Connectivity Requirements for Remote Employees
Employees who work from home are personally responsible for maintaining a reliable, stable, and secure internet connection that is adequate to support all of their work activities, including video conferences, access to cloud-based systems, email, and other productivity tools. An inability to work due to a home internet outage or connectivity failure does not constitute an approved absence and does not automatically entitle the employee to paid leave for the affected period. Where a connectivity failure is brief and resolved quickly, the employee should communicate the disruption to their manager and make up any lost time within the same working day. Where the connectivity failure is prolonged and fundamentally prevents the employee from performing their duties, the employee should apply for emergency leave or discuss an alternative work arrangement with their manager.
- 1.14.4 Consequences of Unavailability During Core Hours
Remote employees who are found to be unavailable or unresponsive during core hours without having given their manager prior notice of the unavailability will be treated as absent for the affected period. Depending on the duration of the unavailability, this may result in a partial or full day being recorded as Leave Without Pay. Repeated or unexplained unavailability during core hours will be treated as a breach of both the attendance policy and the conditions of the Work From Home arrangement, and may result in the suspension or permanent revocation of the WFH privilege in addition to the progressive disciplinary consequences applicable to attendance violations.
- 1.14.5 Interaction with the Work From Home Policy
Employees who are working from home must read and familiarize themselves with the full provisions of the Work From Home Policy referenced as HR-WFH-001, which contains detailed guidance on all expectations, obligations, and conditions that govern remote work arrangements. The WFH Policy and this Attendance Policy are complementary documents, and compliance with both is required of all remote employees.

1.15 Attendance for Employees with Disabilities or Chronic Health Conditions
- 1.15.1 The Company's Commitment to Reasonable Accommodation
The Company is fully committed to providing a workplace that is inclusive, accessible, and supportive of employees with disabilities, chronic health conditions, or any other long-term health challenges that may affect their ability to maintain standard attendance patterns. The Company recognizes that a rigid, unmodified application of the attendance standards in this policy to employees with genuine medical conditions may be discriminatory and may also fail to retain and support talented individuals who, with appropriate accommodation, can make a full and valuable contribution to the organization.
Where an employee has a disability or a chronic health condition that is relevant to their attendance, the Company will engage in a good-faith, collaborative process with the employee to identify and implement reasonable adjustments to their working arrangements, attendance expectations, or leave entitlements that would enable the employee to meet their professional obligations in a manner consistent with their health needs. This process is referred to as a Reasonable Accommodation Assessment and will be conducted by HR in consultation with the employee, the reporting manager, and where appropriate, the employee's medical practitioner.
- 1.15.2 Reasonable Accommodation Assessment Process
An employee who has a disability or chronic health condition that affects their attendance should disclose this to HR at the earliest opportunity. Disclosure is voluntary, and the Company recognizes that some employees may be concerned about the implications of disclosure for their employment. The Company wishes to assure all employees that disclosure of a medical condition will be treated with absolute confidentiality, will not be used against the employee in any employment decision, and will be used solely for the purpose of identifying and implementing appropriate support and accommodation.
Following disclosure, HR will initiate the Reasonable Accommodation Assessment, which will involve a structured conversation with the employee to understand the nature of the condition and its impact on attendance, a review of any relevant medical documentation provided by the employee, a consultation with the reporting manager to understand the operational implications of any proposed adjustment, and where appropriate, input from an occupational health professional. The outcome of the Assessment will be documented in a formal Reasonable Adjustment Agreement signed by the employee, the reporting manager, and HR, and will be reviewed at regular intervals to ensure that it remains appropriate and effective.
- 1.15.3 Attendance Monitoring for Employees with Reasonable Adjustments
Employees who have formal Reasonable Adjustment Agreements in place will have their attendance monitored in accordance with the modified standards agreed in their individual agreements, rather than the standard thresholds in this policy. The progressive disciplinary process for attendance violations will not be applied mechanically to these employees in respect of absences that are directly related to and consistent with their documented medical condition. The Company will, however, continue to monitor attendance overall and will seek to understand and address any patterns that suggest the accommodation is not sufficient or appropriate, or that additional support is needed.

1.16 Public Holidays, Special Working Days, and Weekend Work
- 1.16.1 Public Holidays
The Company observes a defined set of national, regional, and local public holidays each calendar year, which are communicated to all employees through the official holiday calendar published by HR at the beginning of each financial year. The holiday calendar will specify which holidays are observed company-wide and which may vary by location or state, in accordance with applicable regional legislation. Employees are not required to work on designated public holidays unless the nature of their role involves shift-based working or unless they are specifically requested to do so by their manager due to a genuine operational requirement.
Where an employee is required to work on a public holiday due to genuine operational necessity, this will be communicated by the reporting manager with as much advance notice as possible, and the applicable compensation arrangements — whether a compensatory day off to be taken on a mutually agreed alternative date, or overtime pay at the applicable statutory or contractual rate — will be communicated clearly before the work is undertaken.
- 1.16.2 Optional or Restricted Holidays
In addition to mandatory public holidays, the Company may offer a defined number of Optional Holidays per year, allowing employees to choose from a list of designated optional holidays based on their personal, cultural, or religious observances. The number of optional holidays available to each employee and the process for selecting and applying for them will be communicated in the annual holiday calendar and through the HRMS. Optional holidays must be applied for and approved through the HRMS in the same manner as any other form of leave and will be deducted from the employee's Optional Holiday entitlement, not from their Annual Leave balance.
- 1.16.3 Special Working Days
In exceptional circumstances, the Company may designate a normally non-working day — such as a Saturday — as a Special Working Day to meet a critical client commitment, respond to a business emergency, or make up for working time lost due to an unforeseen disruption. Special Working Days will be communicated to all affected employees with as much advance notice as is practically possible and will always be accompanied by a clear explanation of the business reason for the designation. Employees who are genuinely unable to work on a designated Special Working Day due to pre-existing personal commitments must notify their manager immediately upon receiving the communication and must apply for leave through the HRMS for the affected day.

1.17 Monthly Attendance Review and Quarterly Audit
- 1.17.1 Monthly Attendance Review Process
The Company conducts a formal review of attendance records for all employees on the last working day of each calendar month. This review is carried out by the HR department using the consolidated attendance reports generated automatically by the HRMS. The reports are distributed to all reporting managers, who are required to review the attendance records for their respective teams, validate the accuracy of the data, identify any discrepancies or anomalies, and submit their validated reports to HR within five working days.
Managers who identify discrepancies in their team's attendance data must flag these to HR within the five-day window. HR will then investigate the discrepancy using all available corroborating information and make the appropriate correction to the official record before the payroll cycle closes. At the conclusion of the monthly review, HR will identify all employees whose attendance records require follow-up action and will initiate the appropriate response within the following month's working cycle.
- 1.17.2 Quarterly Attendance Audit
In addition to the monthly review, HR conducts a comprehensive quarterly attendance audit across all departments, business units, and locations. The quarterly audit is a deeper and more analytical exercise than the monthly review, designed to identify systemic patterns and trends in attendance data that may not be visible at the level of individual monthly reviews. These patterns provide HR and business leadership with valuable, data-driven insights that inform operational decisions, management interventions, policy updates, and workforce planning.
The findings of the quarterly audit are compiled into a formal report shared with senior business leadership and relevant HR leadership in aggregate form. The aggregate report does not identify individual employees by name but presents patterns and trends at team, department, and organizational levels. Where the audit identifies specific individuals with systemic, unaddressed attendance violations, appropriate individual action will be initiated through the progressive disciplinary process.
- 1.17.3 Annual Attendance Performance Report
At the close of each calendar year, HR prepares a comprehensive Annual Attendance Performance Report covering the full 12-month period. This report includes the overall organizational attendance rate, a department-by-department analysis of attendance performance, identification of the most common reasons for absence where data is available, trends in late arrivals, early departures, and unauthorized absences, the number of disciplinary actions taken in relation to attendance violations during the year, and recommendations for any policy adjustments or operational interventions for the following year. The Annual Attendance Performance Report is presented to the Chief Human Resources Officer and to senior business leadership, and its findings are used to inform the annual policy review process.
- 1.17.4 Employee Responsibility to Flag Discrepancies
Employees who identify errors or discrepancies in their own attendance records must flag these through the HR Helpdesk or directly with HR within five working days of the calendar month closing. Discrepancies raised within the five-day window will be investigated and, where substantiated, corrected before the payroll cycle closes for that month. Discrepancies raised after the five-day window may not be retroactively corrected for the same payroll cycle and may be carried forward for consideration in the following month's payroll, subject to the nature of the discrepancy and the merits of the case. Employees are therefore advised to review their HRMS attendance records regularly — ideally weekly — rather than waiting until after the month has closed.

1.18 Attendance Data, Privacy, and Record Retention
- 1.18.1 Attendance Data as Personal Data
The Company's attendance records contain personal data relating to individual employees, including information about their working hours, absences, health-related reasons for absence, and location data where applicable. This data is subject to all applicable data protection legislation, including the provisions of the Company's Data Protection and Privacy Policy referenced as HR-DPP-001. Attendance data will be collected, stored, processed, and retained only to the extent necessary for the legitimate business, operational, and compliance purposes described in this policy.
- 1.18.2 Access to Attendance Data
Access to individual employee attendance data is restricted on a strict need-to-know basis. The employee themselves may access their own attendance records through the HRMS self-service portal at any time. The employee's reporting manager may access the attendance records of their direct reports for the purposes of monitoring, validation, and the management processes described in this policy. HR has access to attendance records across the organization for the purposes of payroll administration, disciplinary processes, audit, and policy compliance. Senior leadership may access aggregated and anonymized attendance data for the purposes of workforce planning and organizational performance management. No other individual within the organization may access another employee's attendance records without explicit authorization from HR.
- 1.18.3 Retention Period for Attendance Records
Attendance records will be retained in the HRMS for a minimum period of seven years from the date of the record, or for the duration of the employee's employment plus three years following the cessation of employment, whichever is longer. This retention period reflects the requirements of applicable labor and tax legislation, as well as the need to maintain complete and accurate records for the purpose of addressing any future employment disputes, regulatory inquiries, or legal proceedings. At the end of the applicable retention period, attendance records will be securely deleted or anonymized in accordance with the Company's data retention and destruction procedures.

1.19 Manager Responsibilities and Accountability
- 1.19.1 The Manager's Role in Attendance Management
Managers occupy a critically important position in the effective implementation of this policy. They are the first line of observation, the first point of communication for employees experiencing attendance challenges, and the primary drivers of team attendance culture. A manager who models punctuality, enforces standards consistently, communicates with their team about attendance expectations regularly, and addresses concerns promptly and fairly creates a team environment in which attendance is taken seriously and professional accountability is the norm. Conversely, a manager who ignores attendance violations, applies standards inconsistently, fails to report concerns to HR, or implicitly condones habitual late arrivals and early departures creates a permissive culture that ultimately harms the team, the department, and the organization.
- 1.19.2 Specific Manager Obligations Under This Policy
Managers are required to monitor the attendance of their direct reports on a daily and ongoing basis through the HRMS manager dashboard and through their direct observation of team behavior. Managers must validate and approve or reject attendance entries submitted by their team members — including field attendance entries and remote attendance records — within 24 hours of the entry being submitted. Managers must address late arrivals and other attendance concerns with the employee promptly and directly, without waiting for HR to initiate the conversation. Managers must escalate patterns of concern to HR within the timeframes specified in this policy and must not attempt to manage systemic attendance violations informally or without HR involvement. Managers must conduct return-to-work interviews with every returning employee and document them in the HRMS as required by this policy. Managers must not condone, excuse, or retroactively authorize attendance violations after the fact without genuine justification and without HR concurrence.
- 1.19.3 Manager Accountability for Failure to Enforce This Policy
Managers who are found to have knowingly tolerated attendance violations within their teams without reporting them to HR, who have applied this policy inconsistently or selectively within their teams, or who have actively or passively colluded in attendance falsification, will be held personally accountable. Such conduct represents a failure of managerial responsibility and a breach of the manager's own professional obligations under the Code of Conduct. Depending on the severity and nature of the failure, the consequences for the manager may include a formal written warning, a review of the manager's own performance rating, mandatory management training, or further disciplinary action.

1.20 Employee Rights and Grievance in Attendance Matters
- 1.20.1 Employee Right to Raise Concerns About Attendance Records
Every employee has the right to review their own attendance records through the HRMS at any time. If an employee believes that their attendance record contains an error — whether because a marked absence was in fact an approved leave day, a late arrival was caused by a system failure, or an attendance entry was not captured due to a biometric malfunction — the employee has the right to formally dispute the record through the HR Helpdesk within the timeframes specified in this policy. The Company will investigate every formally raised dispute and will correct records where the available evidence supports the correction.
- 1.20.2 Employee Right to Representation in Disciplinary Proceedings
At every formal stage of the progressive disciplinary process described in this policy, the employee has the right to be accompanied by a colleague of their choice, a trade union representative where the Company recognizes such representation, or any other appropriate employee representative as permitted by applicable law and the Company's disciplinary procedure. This right exists to ensure that the employee has appropriate support and advocacy during what may be a stressful and consequential formal process, and the Company will always communicate this right clearly to the employee at the outset of any formal disciplinary meeting.
- 1.20.3 Right to Appeal Disciplinary Decisions
An employee who disagrees with a disciplinary decision made in relation to an attendance violation — including the issuance of a written warning, the initiation of a Performance Improvement Plan, or a decision to terminate employment — has the right to formally appeal the decision. The appeal must be submitted in writing to the Head of Human Resources within five working days of the disciplinary decision being communicated to the employee. The appeal will be reviewed by an HR representative who was not involved in the original disciplinary decision, and the outcome of the appeal will be communicated to the employee in writing within ten working days of the appeal being received. The appeal process is a genuine mechanism for fairness and not merely a procedural formality, and the Company will approach every appeal with an open mind and a commitment to getting the right outcome.

1.21 Policy Compliance, Governance, and Review
- 1.21.1 Responsibility for Policy Compliance
Compliance with this policy is the personal and non-delegable responsibility of every employee without exception. Employees are responsible for understanding the provisions of this policy in full, for marking their attendance accurately and consistently every working day, for communicating proactively when circumstances affect their ability to meet attendance standards, and for engaging constructively with any feedback or concern raised by their manager or HR in relation to their attendance.
Reporting managers are responsible for monitoring the attendance of their direct reports on an ongoing basis, for addressing concerns promptly and through the appropriate channels, for validating and approving attendance entries in the HRMS accurately and on time, and for escalating systemic attendance concerns to HR in a timely and complete manner. HR is responsible for administering this policy, maintaining the integrity of the HRMS attendance data, conducting monthly reviews and quarterly audits, issuing formal disciplinary communications at the appropriate stages of the progressive process, and ensuring that the policy is applied consistently and without bias across all employees and all levels of the organization.
- 1.21.2 Employee Acknowledgment
All employees are required to formally acknowledge that they have read, understood, and agree to comply with this policy. Acknowledgment will be captured through the HRMS at the time of onboarding for new employees and upon each update to the policy for existing employees. The acknowledgment record will be maintained in the employee's personnel file. Failure to complete the acknowledgment process within the timeline specified by HR will be flagged as a compliance matter and addressed accordingly.

This policy will be formally reviewed on an annual basis by the HR department, or earlier if required by a material change in applicable labor legislation, a significant organizational restructuring, a major operational development, or feedback from managers, employees, or audit findings that reveals a material gap or inadequacy in the policy's provisions.
