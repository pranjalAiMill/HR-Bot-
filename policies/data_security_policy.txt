POLICY 2: DATA SECURITY POLICY

1. PURPOSE
This Data Security Policy establishes the organization's requirements and obligations for protecting all data assets — including personal data, proprietary business information, client data, and intellectual property — from unauthorized access, disclosure, modification, destruction, or misuse. Data security is a shared responsibility. Every employee, contractor, and third-party with access to organizational data has a critical role in safeguarding it.

2. SCOPE
This policy applies to:

- All permanent employees, part-time staff, temporary workers, interns, and volunteers.
- All contractors, consultants, third-party vendors, and outsourcing partners with access to organizational systems or data.
- All data processed, stored, or transmitted using organizational systems, applications, networks, devices, or cloud services.
- All physical locations where organizational data is accessed or stored, including offices, remote work locations, and third-party premises.


3. POLICY STATEMENT
The organization is committed to maintaining the confidentiality, integrity, and availability (the "CIA Triad") of all data assets at all times. The organization complies with all applicable data protection legislation, including but not limited to GDPR, the Information Technology Act, the Personal Data Protection Act (or applicable national equivalent), and any sector-specific regulations. Non-compliance with this policy is treated as a serious disciplinary matter and may result in termination of employment and, where applicable, referral to law enforcement authorities.

4. DEFINITIONS
- Data: Any information in any format — digital, physical, or verbal — that is created, received, stored, processed, or transmitted by or on behalf of the organization.
- Personal Data: Any information that identifies or could identify a living natural person, directly or indirectly (e.g., name, email address, employee ID, biometric data).
- Sensitive Personal Data / Special Category Data: Personal data revealing racial or ethnic origin, political opinions, religious beliefs, health data, biometric data, criminal records, financial data, or sexual orientation.
- Confidential Information: Proprietary business data including trade secrets, client lists, pricing, contracts, strategic plans, source code, financial projections, and unreleased product information.
- Data Breach: Any accidental or unlawful destruction, loss, alteration, unauthorized disclosure, or access to data.
- Data Controller: The organization entity that determines the purposes and means of processing personal data.
- Data Processor: A third party that processes personal data on behalf of the organization.
- Authorized User: An employee or third party who has been formally granted access to specific systems and data in accordance with their job role.

5. DATA CLASSIFICATION
All organizational data must be classified into one of the following four categories:
Level 1 — Public: Information that is approved for public release or that poses no risk if disclosed externally. Examples: published marketing materials, public-facing website content, approved press releases.
Level 2 — Internal: Information intended for internal use by employees. Disclosure externally could cause minor harm. Examples: internal newsletters, general process documents, non-sensitive policies.
Level 3 — Confidential: Sensitive business or personal information that is restricted to specific individuals or teams. Unauthorized disclosure could cause significant harm to the organization or individuals. Examples: employee personnel files, client contracts, financial statements, project plans, source code.
Level 4 — Restricted / Top Secret: Highly sensitive information whose unauthorized disclosure could cause severe legal, financial, reputational, or safety consequences. Examples: acquisition plans, board deliberations, cryptographic keys, regulatory investigation data, personal health records.
All data must be labeled with its classification level in the document header or metadata. Employees must handle data strictly in accordance with the controls applicable to its classification level.

6. ACCESS CONTROL
6.1 Principle of Least Privilege
Access to data and systems is granted on the principle of least privilege — employees are given only the minimum level of access necessary to perform their job duties. Requests for access beyond one's standard role must be formally approved by the line manager and the IT Security team.
6.2 User Account Management
All employees are assigned a unique user account. Shared accounts are strictly prohibited. Accounts are provisioned upon commencement of employment and deprovisioned within twenty-four (24) hours of employment termination. IT administrators perform a quarterly access rights review to ensure all access remains appropriate and current.
6.3 Privileged Access
Access to administrative, root-level, or privileged accounts is restricted to authorized IT and Security personnel only. All privileged access sessions are logged and monitored. Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) is mandatory for all privileged accounts.
6.4 Third-Party Access
Third-party vendors and contractors are granted access only to the specific systems and data required for their engagement. All third-party access is time-limited, documented via a formal Access Request Form, and governed by a signed Data Processing Agreement (DPA) or Non-Disclosure Agreement (NDA).

7. PASSWORD & AUTHENTICATION STANDARDS
All employees must adhere to the following password and authentication standards:

- Passwords must be a minimum of fourteen (14) characters in length.
- Passwords must contain a combination of uppercase letters, lowercase letters, numbers, and at least one special character.
- Passwords must not contain the employee's name, username, company name, or easily guessable words.
- Passwords must be changed every ninety (90) days. The system will enforce this requirement.
- Password reuse — using any of the last twelve (12) passwords — is prohibited.
- Passwords must never be written down, shared with colleagues, disclosed verbally, or stored in plain text in any file or document.
- Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) is mandatory for all remote access, email, financial systems, HR systems, cloud applications, and any system classified as Confidential or Restricted.
- The use of a corporate-approved password manager is strongly recommended.


8. DEVICE & ENDPOINT SECURITY
8.1 Corporate Devices
All corporate devices — including laptops, desktops, mobile phones, tablets, and USB drives — must be registered with IT. Corporate devices must have the following controls enabled at all times:

- Full-disk encryption (e.g., BitLocker or FileVault).
- Endpoint protection and anti-malware software approved by IT Security.
- Automatic operating system and software security patches applied within seventy-two (72) hours of release.
- Automatic screen lock after a maximum of five (5) minutes of inactivity.
- Remote wipe capability enabled.

8.2 Personal Devices (BYOD)
Use of personal devices to access corporate systems or data is only permitted through the organization's Mobile Device Management (MDM) platform and is subject to written approval from IT Security. Employees using personal devices under BYOD must accept the corporate MDM profile, which enables remote wipe of corporate data (not personal data) in the event of loss, theft, or employment termination.

8.3 Physical Device Security
Employees must never leave corporate devices unattended in public spaces. Devices must be locked when not in use. Corporate devices must not be lent to family members, friends, or any unauthorized individuals. Loss or theft of a corporate device must be reported to IT Security and HR within one (1) hour of discovery.

9. NETWORK SECURITY
9.1 Corporate Network
Employees must only connect to corporate systems via the organization's secured, encrypted network. When working remotely, employees must connect via the organization's Virtual Private Network (VPN) at all times before accessing corporate resources.
9.2 Public Wi-Fi
Use of public or unsecured Wi-Fi networks to access corporate systems is strictly prohibited unless a VPN is active. Employees should treat all public networks as hostile environments.
9.3 Wireless Network
Home Wi-Fi networks used for remote work must be secured with WPA2 or WPA3 encryption with a strong, unique password. Default router passwords must be changed.

10. EMAIL & COMMUNICATION SECURITY

- Corporate email accounts must be used exclusively for business communications. Personal use of corporate email accounts is not permitted.
- Employees must not open email attachments or click on links from unknown or suspicious senders.
- Confidential or Restricted data must not be transmitted via personal email, personal messaging apps, or any communication platform not approved by IT Security.
- All external email attachments containing Confidential or Restricted data must be encrypted before sending.
- Employees must be vigilant about phishing attacks. Any suspicious email must be reported to the Information Security team immediately using the designated phishing report button or by emailing security@[company].com.
- Bulk forwarding of corporate emails to personal email accounts is strictly prohibited and constitutes gross misconduct.


11. CLOUD SERVICES & SOFTWARE
- Employees may only use cloud services and software applications that have been approved by IT Security and appear on the organization's approved software list.
- Installing unauthorized software on corporate devices — including free tools, browser extensions, or personal applications — is prohibited without prior approval from IT Security.
- Corporate data must not be stored on personal cloud storage services (e.g., personal Google Drive, personal Dropbox, personal iCloud) without explicit written authorization from IT Security.
- All approved cloud services must be accessed using corporate credentials with MFA enabled.


12. DATA HANDLING & STORAGE
12.1 Data Storage
Confidential and Restricted data must be stored exclusively on organization-approved systems, such as the corporate network drives, approved cloud platforms, or encrypted local drives. Storing Confidential or Restricted data on personal devices or unauthorized cloud storage is a disciplinary offense.
12.2 Data in Transit
Confidential and Restricted data transmitted electronically must be encrypted using industry-standard encryption protocols (minimum TLS 1.2 for data in transit, AES-256 for data at rest).
12.3 Physical Data
Printed documents containing Confidential or Restricted information must be stored in locked cabinets when not in use. Unattended printing of sensitive documents in shared printers is prohibited. Confidential physical documents must be disposed of using the organization's secure cross-cut shredding facilities or locked confidential waste bins.
12.4 Data Retention
Data must be retained only for as long as necessary for its intended business purpose, and in line with the organization's Data Retention Schedule. Employees must not retain personal data beyond its specified retention period. All data disposal must follow approved secure disposal procedures.

13. CORPORATE ASSETS — DATA SYSTEMS
The following are considered critical corporate data assets and are subject to enhanced security controls:
- Customer Relationship Management (CRM) systems and all client data therein.
- Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) and financial systems.
- Human Resources Information System (HRIS) and all employee data.
- Intellectual Property including source code repositories, patents, trade secrets, and product roadmaps.
- Business intelligence platforms, dashboards, and analytics data.
- Email infrastructure and archived communications.
- Marketing databases and campaign data.
- Legal case management systems and regulatory correspondence.
Access to these assets is subject to formal access approval, MFA enforcement, audit logging, and quarterly access reviews.

14. INCIDENT REPORTING & DATA BREACH RESPONSE
14.1 Reporting Obligation
Every employee has a legal and contractual obligation to report any actual or suspected data security incident immediately. This includes:
- Unauthorized access to systems or data.
- Loss or theft of a device containing corporate data.
- Accidental disclosure of data to the wrong recipient.
- Discovery of malware or ransomware.
- Discovery of unauthorized data exfiltration.
- Suspicious system behavior suggesting a potential breach.
Reports must be made immediately to the Information Security team 
14.2 Breach Response Process
Step 1 — Containment: IT Security immediately acts to contain the breach and prevent further data loss.
Step 2 — Assessment: The scope and nature of the breach is assessed, including what data is affected, who is affected, and the likely impact.
Step 3 — Notification: Where required by law (e.g., GDPR requires notification within seventy-two hours), the relevant data protection authority and affected individuals will be notified.
Step 4 — Investigation: A root cause investigation is conducted. Where the breach is attributable to employee negligence or misconduct, HR will be engaged to consider appropriate disciplinary action.
Step 5 — Remediation: Controls are updated to prevent recurrence. Lessons learned are shared across the organization.
14.3 Non-Retaliation
No employee will be penalized for reporting a genuine security concern in good faith. Retaliation against employees who report security incidents is a disciplinary offense.

15. TRAINING & AWARENESS
All employees must complete the following mandatory training:
- Information Security Awareness Training — upon joining and annually thereafter.
- Phishing Simulation Exercises — conducted quarterly by IT Security.
- Data Protection and GDPR Training — upon joining and every two (2) years.
- Role-specific training for employees with access to Confidential or Restricted data — upon role assignment.

Failure to complete mandatory training within the stipulated timeframe will be flagged to the line manager and HR and may affect performance ratings.

16. DISCIPLINARY CONSEQUENCES
Violations of this policy are treated with the utmost seriousness. Depending on the nature, severity, and intent of the violation, consequences may include:
- Formal written warning.
- Suspension from access to specific systems.
- Demotion or role change.
- Termination of employment.
- Referral to law enforcement authorities and/or relevant data protection regulators.
- Civil legal action for damages.
