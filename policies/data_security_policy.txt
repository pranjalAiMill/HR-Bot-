Data Protection and Privacy Policy

4.1 Purpose and Organizational Philosophy
4.1.1 Why This Policy Exists
— The Foundation of Data Security. The Data Security Policy is one of the most operationally critical documents in the Company's entire policy framework. It establishes the principles, standards, and mandatory behaviors through which the Company protects the data it holds — data belonging to its employees, its clients, its business partners, and its own operations — from unauthorized access, misuse, loss, corruption, and disclosure. Where the Code of Conduct referenced as HR-COC-001 governs how employees behave as professionals and as people, and the Employee Helpdesk Policy referenced as HR-EHD-001 governs how employees are supported when they need assistance, this policy governs how the Company and every individual within it handles information — which in the modern operating environment is among the most valuable and most vulnerable assets the organization possesses. Data is not a byproduct of business. It is a core business asset, and the security of that asset is a core organizational responsibility.
— The Organizational Imperative. The Company operates in an environment where data is simultaneously a commercial asset and a legal responsibility. The personal data of employees, the confidential data of clients, and the proprietary operational data of the Company are all subject to sophisticated and persistent threats — from external cyber attackers deploying increasingly advanced techniques, from accidental internal mishandling by well-meaning employees who are unaware of the risk their actions create, and from deliberate insider misconduct by individuals who abuse their access to Company systems. A single data breach — whether caused by a phishing email opened in error, a laptop left unsecured in a public location, a misconfigured cloud storage system, or a deliberate act of data theft — can cause irreparable harm to the individuals whose data is affected, expose the Company to substantial regulatory penalties under applicable data protection law, destroy client relationships built over years, and inflict reputational damage that organizations rarely fully recover from. This policy exists to prevent those outcomes by establishing clear, unambiguous, and enforceable standards that every employee is required to understand and comply with in every aspect of their work.
— The Human Imperative. This policy has been written with a genuine recognition that data security is not a subject that comes naturally to everyone and that the overwhelming majority of employees who inadvertently create a security risk do so not out of carelessness or indifference but out of a genuine lack of awareness of what security-conscious behavior looks like in practice. This policy is not a declaration of distrust in the Company's people. It is a tool of empowerment — a clear, practical, and comprehensive guide that gives every employee the knowledge they need to handle data responsibly, to recognize a security risk when they encounter one, and to respond correctly when something goes wrong. The Company invests in this policy, and in the training and awareness programs that support it, because it genuinely believes that an informed and security-aware workforce is the most powerful defense the organization has against the threats it faces.
— The Philosophy of Shared Responsibility. Data security is not the exclusive responsibility of the IT department, the Information Security function, or the leadership team. It is the shared and personal responsibility of every single individual who handles Company data in any capacity. A security policy that lives only in the IT department is not a security policy — it is a technical program with a policy-shaped wrapper. This policy is built on the conviction that genuine data security requires every employee — regardless of their role, their seniority, their technical background, or their geographic location — to understand, internalize, and apply the standards described here in every interaction with data that they have in the course of their work. Security is a daily practice, not an annual checkbox, and this policy is the foundation on which that practice is built.
— A Living and Evolving Document. Like all enterprise policies, the Data Security Policy is a living document that will be reviewed, updated, and strengthened as the threat landscape evolves, as the Company's technology environment changes, as applicable data protection and cybersecurity legislation develops, and as practical experience provides insights into how the policy can be made more effective. Every employee is expected to engage with this policy seriously, to ask questions where clarification is needed, and to treat it as a genuine and relevant guide to one of the most important responsibilities they carry in their professional role.

4.2 Scope and Applicability
4.2.1 Who This Policy Applies To
— Universal Application Without Exception. This policy applies universally and without exception to every individual engaged with the Company in any employment or engagement capacity who handles, accesses, processes, stores, or transmits Company data or data entrusted to the Company by its clients, employees, or partners. There are no exemptions based on seniority, tenure, professional standing, department, business unit, geographic location, or technical background. The most senior executive in the organization is subject to exactly the same data security obligations as the most junior team member, and any perception that seniority or technical expertise grants an individual the right to operate outside the standards established in this policy will be firmly and consistently rejected at every level of the organization.
— All Categories of Engagement Covered. This policy covers permanent full-time employees, part-time employees, employees on fixed-term or rolling contracts, interns and trainees, external consultants and contractors working on Company premises or accessing Company systems, temporary or agency staff, and any other individual who handles Company data in any capacity. Each of these individuals, regardless of the nature or duration of their engagement, is required to comply with the full standards established in this policy from the first day of their engagement. The onboarding process will include a data security briefing and acknowledgment for all new engagements regardless of duration or type.
— Seniority Confers Heightened Responsibility, Not Exemption. Senior leaders, managers, and those with elevated system access privileges are not exempt from this policy — they are held to a higher standard because of the greater volume and sensitivity of data to which they have access, the greater potential impact of a security failure at their level, and the powerful signal that their conduct sends to the rest of the organization. A leader who bypasses security controls — even for apparently legitimate operational reasons — communicates to their team that the controls are optional, which is a message the Company will not tolerate being sent at any level.
4.2.2 Where This Policy Applies
— All Physical and Digital Environments. This policy applies in every environment in which Company data is accessed, processed, stored, or transmitted — whether on Company premises, at client sites, at home in a remote or hybrid arrangement, in a hotel or airport while traveling on Company business, or in any other location where an employee has occasion to interact with Company data. The obligation to handle data securely does not diminish based on the employee's physical location. An employee working from a hotel room with sensitive client data on their screen has exactly the same security obligations as an employee sitting at a desk in the Company's headquarters.
— All Data Environments and Systems. This policy applies to all systems, platforms, and environments through which Company data is handled — including Company-issued laptops, mobile phones, and tablets, Company servers and cloud-based storage platforms, email and communication systems, third-party software and applications approved for Company use, personal devices used for Company work under the BYOD framework described in Section 4.8, and any other technical environment through which an employee accesses, processes, or transmits data.
4.2.3 Relationship With Other Policies
— An Integrated Security Framework. This Data Security Policy operates as one component of the Company's broader security and governance framework. It must be read and applied in conjunction with the Code of Conduct referenced as HR-COC-001, the Data Protection and Privacy Policy referenced as HR-DPP-001, the Information Security Policy referenced as HR-ISP-001, the Work From Home Policy referenced as HR-WFH-001, and all other applicable Company policies. Where a specific policy provides more detailed guidance on a particular aspect of security — such as the Data Protection and Privacy Policy's treatment of personal data subject rights — that specific policy governs. Where a security concern arises that falls outside the scope of any specific policy, this Data Security Policy provides the governing framework.
— Conflict Resolution. In the event of any apparent conflict between the standards established in this policy and those established in any other Company policy, employees must seek guidance from the IT Security function or HR before taking action. Employees must not act unilaterally on their own interpretation of any apparent conflict between policies and must not use an apparent conflict as justification for departing from security standards that would otherwise apply.

4.3 Data Classification Framework
4.3.1 The Four Tiers of Data Classification
— Why Classification Matters. The appropriate security measures that must be applied to any piece of data depend fundamentally on how sensitive that data is and how serious the consequences of its unauthorized disclosure or loss would be. The Company's data classification framework provides every employee with a clear, consistent, and practical system for making this assessment and for applying the correct level of protection accordingly. Every piece of data that the Company holds — regardless of format, location, or system — falls within one of the four classification tiers described below, and every employee who handles data must be aware of its classification and apply the corresponding security standards at all times.
— Tier 1: Public Data. Public data is information that has been formally approved for unrestricted disclosure and is either already in the public domain or intended to be made publicly available. This includes published marketing materials, public-facing website content, press releases, and other information the Company has expressly authorized for external publication. Public data carries no confidentiality restriction and may be shared freely, but it remains subject to the Company's accuracy, brand, and intellectual property standards. Employees must not classify data as Public without explicit authorization from the relevant business owner or the Communications function.
— Tier 2: Internal Data. Internal data is information intended for use within the Company by employees and authorized third parties in the course of normal business operations, but which has not been approved for external disclosure. This includes general operational communications, internal procedures and process documentation, meeting records, and routine business data that is not sensitive in nature but should not be shared outside the organization without authorization. Internal data must not be transmitted to external parties through personal or unauthorized channels, and must be stored within approved Company systems.
— Tier 3: Confidential Data. Confidential data is sensitive business information whose unauthorized disclosure would cause significant harm to the Company, its clients, its employees, or its partners. This includes client contracts and client personal data, employee compensation and performance records, business strategies and financial forecasts, merger and acquisition information, proprietary methodologies and intellectual property, and any other information that a reasonable professional would recognize as requiring active protection against unauthorized access. Confidential data must be stored in access-controlled environments, transmitted only through encrypted and approved channels, shared only on a strict need-to-know basis, and never processed on personal devices or in unsecured locations.
— Tier 4: Restricted Data. Restricted data is the Company's most sensitive category of information — data whose unauthorized disclosure would cause severe and potentially irreversible harm to the Company, its clients, its employees, or the public. This includes data subject to specific legal or regulatory protection, such as payment card data, health records, and sensitive personal data as defined under applicable data protection law, as well as information relating to ongoing legal proceedings, regulatory investigations, or critical security vulnerabilities in Company systems. Restricted data is subject to the highest levels of access control, encryption, and handling restriction. Access to Restricted data is granted only by explicit authorization from the relevant data owner and the IT Security function and will be subject to enhanced monitoring and audit.
4.3.2 How Classification Is Assigned and Maintained
— Classification at the Point of Creation. The individual who creates a document, record, or dataset is responsible for assigning the correct classification to that data at the point of creation. Where an employee is uncertain about the appropriate classification for a particular item of data, they must seek guidance from their line manager or the IT Security function before creating, storing, or transmitting the data. Treating data as less sensitive than it actually is — whether through genuine uncertainty, convenience, or deliberate disregard — is a security failure that will be addressed accordingly.
— Classification Must Follow Data Throughout Its Lifecycle. A classification assigned to a piece of data at the point of creation must be maintained and honored throughout that data's lifecycle — during storage, transmission, sharing, archiving, and disposal. Where data is transferred from one system or format to another, the classification must be preserved in the new format. Where a document derived from Confidential or Restricted source data is created, the derived document carries the same classification as the source data unless the relevant data owner has formally authorized a reclassification.

4.4 Data Collection and Lawful Processing
4.4.1 Principles of Lawful Data Collection
— Data Is Collected Only for Legitimate Purposes. The Company collects and processes data only for legitimate, specified, and clearly defined purposes that are consistent with applicable data protection law and with the Company's documented data processing activities. Employees must not collect, store, or process data that is not required for a legitimate and authorized business purpose, must not collect more data than is strictly necessary for that purpose, and must not repurpose data collected for one legitimate purpose for a different purpose without appropriate authorization and, where required, the consent of the relevant data subjects.
— Transparency and Fairness in Data Collection. All data collection activities conducted on behalf of the Company must be transparent, fair, and consistent with the Company's published privacy notices and data processing disclosures. Employees who collect personal data — whether from clients, candidates, members of the public, or any other external individuals — must ensure that those individuals are informed about how their data will be used, how long it will be retained, and what rights they have in relation to it, in accordance with the Data Protection and Privacy Policy referenced as HR-DPP-001. Covert or deceptive data collection is absolutely prohibited.
4.4.2 Employee Data and Special Categories
— Employee Data Is Subject to the Same Standards. Data relating to employees — including payroll data, performance records, disciplinary records, health information, and any other personal data held as part of the employment relationship — is subject to the full standards of this policy. Employees who have access to colleague data through their professional role — HR professionals, payroll staff, managers, and others — must handle that data with the same care and discretion they would apply to client data. Employee personal data is processed lawfully, transparently, and only for the purposes for which it was collected, in accordance with applicable employment and data protection law.
— Special Categories of Data Require Enhanced Protection. Certain categories of personal data are classified as special categories under applicable data protection law and are subject to significantly enhanced protection requirements. These include data revealing racial or ethnic origin, political opinions, religious or philosophical beliefs, trade union membership, genetic data, biometric data, health data, data concerning sex life or sexual orientation, and data concerning criminal convictions or offenses. The processing of special category data is subject to strict legal requirements and must be authorized in advance by the Head of HR, the Data Protection Officer where appointed, and the IT Security function. Employees must never process special category data through informal channels, personal devices, or unapproved systems under any circumstances.

4.5 Data Storage and Retention
4.5.1 Approved Storage Environments
— Data Must Be Stored in Approved Systems Only. All Company data — regardless of its classification — must be stored exclusively within systems, platforms, and environments that have been formally approved by the IT Security function. Approved storage environments include the Company's designated cloud storage platforms, Company servers and network drives, the HRMS, the CRM, and other formally provisioned business applications. Employees must not store Company data on personal cloud services such as personal Google Drive accounts or personal Dropbox accounts, on personal laptops or personal external hard drives, in personal email accounts, or in any other environment that has not been formally approved and provisioned by IT. The use of unapproved storage environments — even for convenience, even temporarily — creates security and compliance risks that the Company is not able to accept and that will be treated as a policy violation.
— Storage Classification Alignment. Data must be stored in an environment whose security controls are appropriate for the data's classification tier. Public and Internal data may be stored in general-purpose approved environments. Confidential data must be stored in access-controlled environments with appropriate permission restrictions. Restricted data must be stored in purpose-designated secure environments with enhanced access controls, full audit logging, and encryption at rest, as described in Section 4.13. Where an employee is uncertain whether a particular storage environment is appropriate for a particular tier of data, they must consult the IT Security function before storing the data.
4.5.2 Data Retention Periods and Disposal
— Data Must Not Be Retained Beyond Its Legitimate Purpose. The Company maintains a formal Data Retention Schedule — administered by HR in conjunction with Legal and IT — that specifies the retention period applicable to each category of data held by the Company. Employees must not retain data beyond the period specified in the Retention Schedule for that data category. Retaining data indefinitely because disposal seems inconvenient, or because the employee believes the data might be useful at some unspecified future point, is not compliant with applicable data protection law and will be treated as a violation of this policy.
— Secure Disposal Is Mandatory. When data reaches the end of its retention period, it must be disposed of securely and completely in a manner that ensures it cannot be recovered or reconstructed by an unauthorized party. Electronic data must be deleted using approved secure deletion tools or processes, not simply moved to a recycling bin or deleted through standard operating system processes that leave recoverable data traces. Physical documents containing Confidential or Restricted data must be disposed of using the Company's approved shredding service or secure disposal facilities. Employees must never dispose of physical documents containing sensitive data in standard office waste bins.

4.6 Access Control and User Permissions
4.6.1 The Principle of Least Privilege
— Access Is Granted Only Where It Is Needed. The Company's access control framework is built on the principle of least privilege — the principle that every employee is granted access only to the systems, data, and functions that they genuinely need in order to perform their specific job responsibilities, and no more. The principle of least privilege is not an expression of distrust. It is a fundamental security design principle that limits the potential impact of any individual account being compromised — whether through external attack, internal misconduct, or accidental misuse. An employee who has access only to the data they need to do their job can cause significantly less unintended harm than an employee whose access is unrestricted.
— Access Rights Must Reflect Role, Not Convenience. Access to systems and data must reflect the employee's actual job responsibilities at the time the access is granted. It must not be granted on the basis of what might be useful someday, what the employee's previous role required, what a colleague in a similar role has access to, or any other basis that does not reflect a specific, current, documented operational need. Employees who believe they need access to a system or dataset that is not currently provisioned to their account must submit a formal access request through the IT Helpdesk and must not attempt to access systems or data for which they have not been authorized.
4.6.2 Access Provisioning, Review, and Deprovisioning
— Provisioning Through a Formal Process. All access to Company systems and data must be provisioned through the formal access request process managed by IT. Access requests must be authorized by the employee's line manager and, for Confidential or Restricted data environments, by the relevant data owner and the IT Security function. Access will be granted only after the request has been reviewed, the justification validated, and the appropriate approval obtained. IT will provision access within the agreed service level timeframe following receipt of a fully authorized request and will maintain a complete and auditable record of all access grants.
— Regular Access Reviews Are Mandatory. The IT Security function will conduct formal access reviews for all Company systems on at least a quarterly basis, and for Restricted data environments on a monthly basis. During each access review, the access rights of every user will be validated against their current role and responsibilities. Access that is no longer required — because the employee's role has changed, because a project has ended, or because a system is no longer used — will be revoked promptly and without exception. Line managers are responsible for notifying IT of any change in a team member's role that may affect their access requirements, and must do so within one business day of the change taking effect.
— Immediate Deprovisioning Upon Departure. When an employee leaves the Company for any reason — whether through resignation, redundancy, termination, or the natural conclusion of a fixed-term engagement — all of that employee's access to Company systems, data, and facilities must be revoked on the employee's last day of employment, or earlier where circumstances require it. HR is responsible for notifying IT of all departing employees at the earliest possible stage of the offboarding process to enable timely deprovisioning. Any delay in deprovisioning access that results in a former employee retaining system access beyond their last working day will be treated as a serious security failure and investigated accordingly.

4.7 Password and Credential Security
4.7.1 Password Standards and Requirements
— Strong Passwords Are a Non-Negotiable Minimum. Every Company account must be protected by a strong, unique password that complies with the minimum standards established by the IT Security function. At the time of publication of this policy, the minimum password standards require a minimum length of twelve characters, including a combination of uppercase letters, lowercase letters, numbers, and special characters, and prohibit the use of passwords that are based on personal information such as names, dates of birth, or common words that appear in any language dictionary. These minimum standards will be reviewed and updated by the IT Security function at least annually and may be strengthened as security requirements evolve. The current minimum standards are published on the Company intranet and in the IT Security guidance library.
— Passwords Must Be Unique Across Accounts. Employees must use a unique password for each Company account and must never reuse a password that is used for a personal account on any Company system, or a password that is used on one Company system on any other Company system. Password reuse is one of the most common and most preventable causes of credential compromise. The Company provides access to an approved enterprise password manager to enable employees to maintain strong, unique passwords across all their accounts without the practical burden of memorizing them all. Employees are strongly encouraged to use the approved password manager for all Company accounts and must not record passwords in insecure locations such as notebooks, spreadsheets, sticky notes, or personal email drafts.
— Credentials Must Never Be Shared. Every Company account is assigned to a specific individual and is that individual's personal responsibility. Employees must never share their credentials with any other person — including their line manager, an IT support agent, or a colleague covering their responsibilities during an absence. Legitimate IT support requests will never require an employee to disclose their password. Any request for an employee's password — regardless of the apparent source or justification — must be treated as a suspected phishing or social engineering attempt and reported to the IT Security function immediately in accordance with Section 4.15.
4.7.2 Multi-Factor Authentication
— MFA Is Mandatory for All Applicable Systems. Multi-factor authentication — the requirement to verify identity through a second factor in addition to a password — is mandatory for all Company systems and applications for which MFA functionality is available. This includes email, the HRMS, financial systems, cloud storage platforms, the VPN, and any other system designated by IT as requiring MFA. Employees must not attempt to circumvent, disable, or bypass MFA requirements on any system, and must not use account recovery options to access systems in a way that bypasses MFA controls. Where an employee experiences a technical issue with their MFA configuration, they must contact the IT Helpdesk through the standard support channel and must not attempt to resolve the issue in a way that compromises the security control.
— MFA Devices and Codes Must Be Protected. The second factor in an MFA process — whether delivered through an authenticator application, a hardware token, or an SMS code — is a security credential that must be protected with the same care as a password. Employees must never share their MFA codes with any other person under any circumstances, must keep their MFA device — whether a personal phone, a hardware token, or any other device — physically secure at all times, and must report the loss or suspected compromise of an MFA device to the IT Security function immediately.

4.8 Device and Endpoint Security
4.8.1 Company-Issued Device Security
— Company Devices Are Security-Controlled Assets. Laptops, mobile phones, tablets, and any other devices issued to employees by the Company are configured by IT with security controls designed to protect the data stored on and accessed through those devices. These controls include device encryption, endpoint security software, automatic screen lock, remote wipe capability, and centrally managed software update policies. Employees must not attempt to disable, bypass, or modify any of these security controls. Any modification of the security configuration of a Company device — regardless of the apparent operational justification — is a serious security violation that will be addressed under the disciplinary framework.
— Care and Physical Security of Company Devices. Every employee entrusted with a Company device is personally responsible for its physical security. This means never leaving a device unattended and unlocked in any environment, never leaving a device visible and unattended in a vehicle, never checking a device containing Company data as hold luggage during air travel, never lending a device to a family member, friend, or colleague who has not been authorized by IT to use it, and reporting any loss, theft, or damage of a Company device to the IT Security function immediately — regardless of the time of day or day of the week. Prompt reporting of a lost or stolen device enables IT to remotely wipe the device before any unauthorized party is able to access the data it contains. A delayed report that results in a data breach will be treated as a serious aggravating factor in any subsequent disciplinary process.
4.8.2 Personal Device Usage — BYOD
— Personal Devices May Only Be Used Within the BYOD Framework. The Company recognizes that employees may from time to time need to access Company data or systems from a personal device. This is permitted only within the Company's formal Bring Your Own Device framework, which is administered by IT and requires the device to be registered, the installation of approved security software, and compliance with all applicable security standards. Employees who use a personal device for Company work outside the BYOD framework — including accessing Company email through a personal device that has not been registered and secured — are creating an uncontrolled security exposure that the Company cannot monitor, manage, or remediate, and this constitutes a policy violation.
— BYOD Devices Are Subject to Security Standards. Personal devices registered under the BYOD framework are subject to the same security standards as Company-issued devices in relation to the Company data they access and store. This includes the requirement to maintain device encryption, to operate a current and updated operating system, to use a screen lock with a strong PIN or biometric authentication, and to report the loss or theft of the device to IT immediately. Employees must be aware that registering a personal device under the BYOD framework grants IT the ability to remotely wipe Company data from the device in the event of loss, theft, or compromise. This capability applies only to Company data stored on the device and does not extend to personal data stored separately.

4.9 Network and Communication Security
4.9.1 Secure Network Usage
— The Company VPN Is Required for Remote Access. When accessing Company systems, applications, or data from a location outside of Company premises, employees must connect through the Company's approved Virtual Private Network. The VPN creates an encrypted tunnel between the employee's device and the Company's network infrastructure, protecting the data transmitted through it from interception by unauthorized parties. Employees must not access Company systems or data from a remote location without first connecting to the VPN, and must not bypass or disable the VPN connection while working with Company data from a remote location.
— Public and Unsecured Wi-Fi Networks Are Prohibited for Company Work. Employees must not access Company systems or data through public or unsecured Wi-Fi networks — including networks in coffee shops, hotels, airports, and other public locations — without connecting through the Company VPN. Public Wi-Fi networks are inherently insecure and expose all data transmitted through them to potential interception by third parties. Where an employee needs to access Company data in a public location and does not have access to a secure private Wi-Fi connection, they must use a mobile data connection or a personal mobile hotspot in conjunction with the Company VPN rather than connecting through the public network. This restriction applies regardless of the perceived sensitivity of the data being accessed.
4.9.2 Email and Electronic Communication Security
— Email Is a High-Risk Channel for Sensitive Data. Email is one of the most commonly used communication channels in any organization and one of the most significant vectors for both outbound data loss and inbound cyber threats. Employees must treat all email interactions as potential security risks and must apply the following standards without exception. Confidential or Restricted data must never be transmitted by email to external recipients without the use of approved encryption or secure file transfer mechanisms. Internal email transmissions of Confidential data must use approved internal channels and must not include unnecessary recipients. Employees must verify the identity of email recipients before sending any sensitive information — particularly where a recipient's email address is being entered manually rather than selected from an approved contact list.
— Misdirected Email Is a Common and Serious Data Loss Risk. One of the most frequent causes of data breaches in any organization is the sending of sensitive information to an unintended recipient — caused by autocomplete selecting the wrong email address, by the careless use of reply-all, or by sending a message to the wrong individual or distribution list. Employees must exercise particular care when composing emails containing sensitive information, must double-check all recipient fields before sending, and must use the delay-send functionality where available to create an opportunity to catch errors before transmission. Where an employee discovers that they have sent sensitive data to an unintended recipient, they must report it to the IT Security function and HR immediately as a potential data breach, in accordance with Section 4.16.

4.10 Data Transmission and Sharing
4.10.1 Internal Data Sharing Standards
— Sharing Data Internally on a Need-to-Know Basis. Even within the Company, data must be shared only with colleagues who have a genuine and current need to access it for a legitimate business purpose. The fact that a colleague works for the same organization does not entitle them to access all of the data that their organization holds. Employees must not share access to Confidential or Restricted data with colleagues who have not been formally authorized to access it, and must challenge requests for data access that appear to exceed what the requesting colleague's role requires. Where an employee is uncertain whether a colleague is authorized to receive a particular piece of data, they must seek clarification from their line manager or the IT Security function before sharing.
— Approved Internal Sharing Channels. Internal data sharing must take place through approved Company systems — including the approved cloud storage platform, the internal file sharing system, the HRMS, and other formally provisioned business applications. Employees must not share data internally through personal email, personal messaging applications, or any other channel that is not formally approved and monitored by IT. Where an employee needs to share a large file or dataset with a colleague and is uncertain about the appropriate channel, they must consult the IT Helpdesk before transmitting the data.
4.10.2 External Data Sharing and Third-Party Transfers
— External Data Transfers Require Authorization. The transmission of Company data — including client data, employee data, and proprietary business information — to any external party requires formal authorization. For routine external data transfers that are part of approved business processes — such as sending client deliverables to clients or sharing data with approved vendors — the authorization is embedded in the relevant contractual and process framework. For ad hoc or non-routine external transfers, employees must obtain explicit authorization from their line manager and, for Confidential or Restricted data, from the IT Security function before transmitting. Employees must never transmit Confidential or Restricted data to an external party through personal email, personal cloud storage, or any other unauthorized channel under any circumstances.
— Secure Transmission Methods Must Be Used. Where external data transfer is authorized, it must be conducted through secure, approved transmission methods appropriate to the classification of the data being transferred. Public data may be transmitted through standard channels. Internal data transmitted externally — for example, to an approved vendor — must be sent through encrypted and approved channels. Confidential and Restricted data transmitted externally must use approved secure file transfer platforms, end-to-end encrypted communications, or password-protected encrypted files with the password communicated through a separate channel. The specific approved transmission methods for each data classification tier are published in the IT Security guidance library on the Company intranet.

4.11 Cloud Services and Third-Party Platforms
4.11.1 Approved Cloud Platforms and Services
— Only Approved Cloud Services May Be Used for Company Data. The use of cloud platforms and services for Company work — including cloud storage, cloud-based collaboration tools, cloud-hosted applications, and any other internet-based service through which Company data is processed or stored — is permitted only where the platform or service has been formally assessed and approved by the IT Security function. A complete list of approved cloud services is maintained by IT and published on the Company intranet. Employees must not use any cloud service that does not appear on the approved list — including popular personal services such as personal Google Drive accounts, personal Dropbox accounts, personal iCloud storage, personal WeTransfer accounts, or any other service that has not been assessed and approved — for any purpose that involves Company data.
— The Prohibition on Shadow IT. The use of unapproved technology tools, applications, or platforms — whether cloud-based or locally installed — for Company work is a practice known as shadow IT, and it is absolutely prohibited under this policy. Shadow IT creates uncontrolled and unmonitored data flows outside the Company's approved security perimeter, exposes Company data to risks that IT cannot assess or mitigate, and undermines the integrity of the Company's data security controls. Employees who identify a genuine operational need for a tool or platform that is not currently on the approved list must submit a request to IT for assessment and approval. They must not begin using the tool before approval is granted, regardless of how pressing the operational need appears to be.
4.11.2 Third-Party Vendor Security Assessment
— Approved Vendors Must Meet the Company's Security Standards. The Company's data is only as secure as the vendors and service providers to whom it is entrusted. Every third-party vendor, service provider, or cloud platform that processes Company data on behalf of the Company must be assessed against the Company's security requirements before the relationship commences, and must contractually commit to maintaining those standards for the duration of the relationship. The vendor security assessment process is managed by IT in conjunction with the Procurement and Legal functions. Employees must not engage a new vendor who will process Company data without first ensuring that the vendor assessment process has been completed and that the relevant contractual data processing agreements are in place.

4.12 Remote and Hybrid Work — Data Security
4.12.1 Security Standards for Remote Employees
— Remote Work Does Not Relax Security Standards. The decision to approve a remote or hybrid working arrangement reflects the Company's trust in the employee's professionalism and self-discipline. It is not, under any interpretation, a relaxation of data security standards. Every provision of this policy applies to remote and hybrid employees with exactly the same force and effect as to employees working on Company premises. The physical distance from a Company office does not modify an employee's data security obligations in any respect. Remote employees who handle Confidential or Restricted data must apply the same controls, the same care, and the same vigilance that would be expected of them in the office — and in some respects more, given the additional risks that home and public working environments present.
— Securing the Remote Working Environment. Employees working from home or other remote locations must take active steps to ensure that their working environment does not create unauthorized exposure of Company data. This includes positioning their screen so that it cannot be viewed by other household members or visitors, using a privacy screen filter where working in shared spaces or public locations, ensuring that sensitive conversations are not conducted where they can be overheard by unauthorized individuals, securing printed Company documents in a locked location when not in active use, and ensuring that household members — including family members — do not have access to Company devices, Company accounts, or Company documents of any sensitivity.
4.12.2 Physical Data Security in Remote Settings
— The Heightened Risk of Physical Exposure in Remote Environments. Remote environments present physical data security risks that are typically well-controlled in a Company office but may be significantly less controlled in a home or public setting. Employees working remotely must be particularly attentive to the physical security of their devices and documents, must ensure that Company devices are not left logged in and unattended in a location accessible to other people, must apply the clean desk and clear screen standards described in Section 4.17 to their remote working environment as far as practicable, and must dispose of physical documents containing Company data through secure means rather than placing them in household recycling or general waste.

4.13 Encryption Standards
4.13.1 Encryption in Transit and at Rest
— Encryption Is the Primary Technical Control for Sensitive Data. Encryption is the process of converting data into a format that can only be read by parties who possess the appropriate decryption key, and it is the primary technical control through which the Company protects sensitive data from unauthorized access — both when the data is in transit across a network and when it is stored at rest within a system or device. The Company applies encryption standards that are consistent with current industry best practice and with the requirements of applicable data protection legislation, and those standards are reviewed and updated by the IT Security function at least annually.
— Encryption in Transit. All Company data classified as Confidential or Restricted that is transmitted across any network — whether internal or external — must be encrypted using approved protocols. This applies to email transmissions using approved encrypted email services or secure file transfer tools, to remote access connections using the Company VPN, to web-based system access using HTTPS-secured connections, and to any other form of electronic data transmission. Employees must not transmit Confidential or Restricted data through channels that do not provide appropriate encryption, and must verify that any external platform used to transmit such data supports the required encryption standards before use.
— Encryption at Rest. All Company devices that store Company data — including Company-issued laptops, mobile phones, tablets, and server infrastructure — must be configured with full disk encryption. This configuration is applied by IT to Company-issued devices as part of the standard provisioning process. Employees must not disable disk encryption on any Company device and must ensure that any personal devices used for Company work under the BYOD framework are also configured with device encryption. Restricted data stored in cloud platforms or on servers must be encrypted at rest using approved encryption configurations, and the IT Security function must verify that the required encryption is active before any Restricted data is stored in a new environment.

4.14 Software, Application, and System Security
4.14.1 Patch Management and Software Updates
— Security Patches Must Be Applied Without Delay. Software vulnerabilities — flaws in the code of operating systems, applications, and system components — are one of the primary vectors through which external attackers gain unauthorized access to computer systems and the data they contain. Software vendors regularly release patches and updates that address known vulnerabilities, and the speed with which those patches are applied is one of the most important factors in determining whether a vulnerability can be exploited before it is closed. The Company operates a formal patch management program under which security patches for all Company systems are assessed, tested where required, and deployed within defined timeframes based on the severity of the vulnerability they address. Critical security patches are applied within forty-eight hours of release. High-severity patches are applied within seven days. Medium and low-severity patches are applied within the standard monthly maintenance cycle.
— Employee Responsibility for Device Updates. Employees are responsible for ensuring that their Company-issued devices are configured to receive and apply IT-managed software updates without delay. Where a device presents a notification of a pending software update or security patch, the employee must apply the update within the timeframe specified in the notification rather than deferring it indefinitely for convenience. Devices that have not received current security patches present a known and unnecessary vulnerability and will be identified through the IT Security function's device management systems. An employee who repeatedly defers required security updates will be addressed through the escalation process.
4.14.2 Unauthorized Software and Shadow IT
— Unauthorized Software Must Not Be Installed. Employees must not install any software, application, plugin, browser extension, or other tool on a Company device that has not been formally approved by the IT Security function. Unauthorized software installations create multiple security risks — including the introduction of malware hidden within the software, the creation of uncontrolled data flows through the software's network communications, and the introduction of vulnerabilities in the software itself that are not covered by the Company's patch management program. Where an employee has a legitimate operational need for a software tool that is not currently approved, they must submit a request to IT for assessment and must not begin using the tool before approval is granted and the software is provisioned through the approved installation process.

4.15 Phishing, Social Engineering, and Cyber Threats
4.15.1 Recognizing and Responding to Threats
— Phishing Is the Most Common Entry Point for Cyber Attacks. Phishing — the use of deceptive emails, messages, websites, or phone calls to trick individuals into disclosing credentials, clicking malicious links, or downloading malware — is the most common and most successful method through which external attackers gain unauthorized access to organizational systems. Phishing attacks have become increasingly sophisticated and are no longer easily identified by obvious spelling errors or implausible scenarios. Modern phishing attacks are often highly targeted, use realistic sender addresses and branding, and create convincing scenarios of urgency or authority designed to override the recipient's natural caution. Every employee must treat unexpected or unusual communications with healthy skepticism, regardless of how legitimate they appear.
— Recognizing the Signs of a Phishing Attempt. Common indicators of a phishing or social engineering attempt include unexpected requests for credentials, personal information, or financial authorization — particularly those that create a sense of urgency or threaten negative consequences for non-compliance, email or message senders whose address, on close inspection, does not match the organization they claim to represent, communications that direct the recipient to a website whose URL does not exactly match the legitimate website of the organization referenced, attachments or links in unexpected communications from known or unknown senders, requests to override normal security or authorization processes for apparently compelling reasons, and unusually personal or detailed knowledge of the recipient's role, colleagues, or circumstances that seems designed to establish false credibility. This list is illustrative and not exhaustive. The most important protection against phishing is a habitual willingness to pause, question, and verify before acting on any communication that requests sensitive action.
— Reporting Suspected Threats Immediately. Any employee who receives a communication that they suspect may be a phishing or social engineering attempt — or who believes they may have inadvertently responded to one — must report it to the IT Security function immediately, without attempting to investigate further or take remedial action independently. Prompt reporting enables IT to assess the threat, take protective action across the Company's systems, and provide guidance to the affected employee on any steps they need to take to mitigate the risk. An employee who reports a suspected phishing attempt in good faith — even if it subsequently turns out to be a legitimate communication — will receive no adverse consequence whatsoever. The Company would infinitely prefer to investigate a false alarm than to learn of a real incident after a preventable delay.
4.15.2 Mandatory Threat Awareness Training
— All Employees Must Complete Phishing Awareness Training. All employees are required to complete the Company's mandatory phishing and cyber threat awareness training program within thirty days of joining the Company and to complete a refresher program on at least an annual basis thereafter. This training will cover the current threat landscape, the specific techniques used in phishing and social engineering attacks, practical guidance on identifying and responding to suspected threats, and the procedure for reporting incidents to the IT Security function. Completion of this training is not optional and will be monitored by IT through the HRMS. The Company will also conduct periodic simulated phishing exercises as a practical test of the organization's threat awareness and resilience. The results of these exercises will be used to target additional training to individuals and teams who demonstrate vulnerability, and will not be used for disciplinary purposes for employees who engage with simulated attacks in good faith.

4.16 Data Breach — Detection, Reporting, and Response
4.16.1 What Constitutes a Data Breach
— A Broad and Inclusive Definition. A data breach, for the purposes of this policy, is any incident that results in or creates a risk of the unauthorized access, disclosure, alteration, loss, or destruction of Company data — regardless of whether the incident was caused by a deliberate attack, an accidental act, a system failure, or a human error. This definition is intentionally broad because the harm caused by a data breach depends on the sensitivity of the data affected and the circumstances of the incident, not on whether the breach was the result of a malicious act. Examples of data breach incidents include the loss or theft of a device containing unencrypted Company data, an email containing sensitive data sent to an incorrect recipient, unauthorized access to a Company system by an external attacker or an unauthorized internal user, accidental deletion or corruption of data, a ransomware attack that renders Company data inaccessible, and the unauthorized disclosure of personal data to a third party without appropriate legal basis. This list is illustrative and not exhaustive. Where an employee is uncertain whether an incident constitutes a data breach, they must report it to the IT Security function immediately and allow IT to make the assessment.
4.16.2 The Breach Response Process
— Immediate Reporting Is the Single Most Important Employee Obligation. The single most important thing any employee can do when they discover or suspect a data breach is to report it to the IT Security function and HR immediately — without delay, without first attempting to fix the problem independently, and without waiting to assess whether the breach is serious enough to warrant reporting. The speed of the Company's response to a data breach is one of the most significant factors in determining the scale of harm it causes — both to the individuals whose data is affected and to the Company's legal, regulatory, and reputational position. A delayed report that results in a larger breach than would have occurred with prompt action will be treated as a serious aggravating factor in any subsequent review and will not be mitigated by the employee's assessment that the breach appeared minor at the time.
— The Formal Breach Response Process. Upon receipt of a breach report, the IT Security function will immediately convene the Company's data breach response team, which includes IT Security, HR, Legal, and where appropriate, the Data Protection Officer. The response team will assess the scope and nature of the breach, take immediate technical steps to contain the incident and prevent further exposure, preserve all relevant evidence for investigation and regulatory purposes, assess the notification obligations that arise under applicable data protection law — which in many jurisdictions require notification to the relevant supervisory authority within seventy-two hours of awareness of a qualifying breach — and manage all communications relating to the breach, including any required communications to affected data subjects. No employee other than those formally designated by the response team may communicate externally about a data breach incident, including to clients, regulators, or members of the public, without explicit authorization from the response team.

4.17 Physical Security of Data and Systems
4.17.1 Physical Access Controls
— Access to Secure Areas Is Restricted and Controlled. The Company's premises contain areas — server rooms, IT infrastructure spaces, HR offices, and other locations where sensitive data is processed or stored — that are designated as secure areas and to which access is restricted to authorized individuals. Employees must not enter a secure area without appropriate authorization, must not allow unauthorized individuals to enter a secure area by holding a door open or providing access without verification, and must report any instance of unauthorized or unaccompanied access to a secure area to the security function immediately. Access to secure areas is controlled through physical access systems — including electronic key fobs, access cards, and biometric controls — and any loss or suspected compromise of an access credential must be reported to the security function immediately.
— Protecting Sensitive Data in Shared and Public Spaces. Employees who work with sensitive data in shared office spaces, meeting rooms, or public locations must take active steps to prevent unauthorized individuals from viewing or accessing that data. This includes positioning their screen away from public sightlines, using a physical privacy screen on their laptop when working in a location where the screen may be visible to others, speaking about sensitive matters in a location where the conversation cannot be overheard by unauthorized individuals, and ensuring that physical documents are not left visible on desks or meeting room tables where they may be seen by passing colleagues or visitors.
4.17.2 Clean Desk and Clear Screen Standards
— Every Employee Is Required to Maintain a Clean Desk. At the end of each working day, and whenever leaving their desk for an extended period, every employee must ensure that their desk is clear of any physical documents, notes, or materials containing Company data that could be accessed or read by unauthorized individuals. This standard — known as the clean desk standard — applies to all employees regardless of the sensitivity of the data they typically work with, because even seemingly innocuous documents can provide useful intelligence to an unauthorized party and because the clean desk habit is more reliably maintained when it is applied consistently rather than selectively based on perceived sensitivity.
— Screens Must Be Locked When Unattended. Every employee must lock their computer screen whenever they step away from their workstation — whether for a brief period such as a visit to another part of the office, or for a longer absence. Screen locking must be performed manually upon leaving the workstation and must also be configured to activate automatically after a short period of inactivity. The maximum auto-lock period permitted under this policy is five minutes. An unlocked and unattended screen is an invitation to unauthorized access and constitutes a physical security violation regardless of whether any unauthorized access subsequently occurs.

4.18 Backup and Business Continuity
4.18.1 Data Backup Standards
— Critical Data Must Be Backed Up Regularly and Reliably. The Company's ability to recover from a data loss event — whether caused by a ransomware attack, a hardware failure, an accidental deletion, or a natural disaster — depends entirely on the quality, currency, and integrity of its data backups. The IT Security function administers a formal backup program under which all critical Company data is backed up at defined intervals, stored in geographically separate and secure locations, and tested for recoverability on a regular basis. The backup schedule, retention periods, and recovery objectives for each category of Company data are defined in the Company's Data Backup Standard, which is published in the IT Security guidance library. Employees who maintain data in personal or locally-stored files outside of approved Company systems are responsible for ensuring that such data is transferred to approved Company systems where it will be covered by the backup program, and must not rely on local device storage as the sole copy of any data that cannot be regenerated.
4.18.2 Business Continuity and Disaster Recovery
— The Company Is Prepared for Disruption. The Company maintains a formal Business Continuity and Disaster Recovery program that defines how the organization will continue to operate — and how it will restore full operational capability — in the event of a significant disruption to its IT systems, data, or physical premises. The IT Security function is responsible for maintaining and testing the disaster recovery plan, including periodic failover tests that validate the Company's ability to restore critical systems and data within defined recovery time objectives. All employees must be familiar with the business continuity provisions that apply to their role and function — including any manual processes, alternative communication channels, or interim working arrangements that would be activated in the event of a major system outage — and must cooperate fully with the IT Security function in any business continuity exercise or real activation event.

4.19 Monitoring, Audit, and Logging
4.19.1 System Monitoring and Activity Logging
— The Company Monitors the Use of Its Systems. The Company reserves the right to monitor the use of its IT systems and to log the activity of all users to the extent permitted by applicable law, for the legitimate purposes of detecting and investigating security incidents, identifying policy violations, maintaining the integrity of the Company's data security controls, and meeting the Company's legal and regulatory obligations. All activity on Company systems — including login events, file access and modification, email transmissions, and system configuration changes — is logged by IT and retained for a period consistent with applicable legal requirements and the Company's Data Retention Schedule. Employees should be aware that their use of Company systems may be monitored and must conduct themselves accordingly at all times. The monitoring of Company systems is not targeted at specific individuals without cause. It is a systematic security control that applies to all users equally.
4.19.2 Security Audit Program
— Regular Security Audits Are Part of the Company's Governance Framework. The IT Security function conducts a formal security audit program that includes internal security assessments of all critical systems and processes on at least an annual basis, external penetration testing of the Company's systems and network perimeter at least once per year, regular automated vulnerability scanning of the Company's IT infrastructure, and periodic review of user access rights, security configurations, and compliance with the standards established in this policy. The findings of security audits are reported to the Head of IT, the Chief Human Resources Officer, and the executive leadership team. Where an audit identifies a gap in the Company's security posture — whether a technical vulnerability, a process weakness, or a compliance failure — a remediation plan will be prepared and executed within defined timeframes, and progress against the plan will be tracked and reported to leadership on a monthly basis until closure.

4.20 Artificial Intelligence and Automated Processing
4.20.1 Use of AI Tools and Data Processing Systems
— AI Tools May Only Be Used Within the Approved Framework. The use of artificial intelligence tools — including large language model platforms, automated data analysis tools, AI-powered productivity applications, and any other AI-driven technology — for any purpose that involves Company data is permitted only where the tool has been formally assessed and approved by the IT Security function. The rapid expansion of AI tools in the commercial environment has created a significant and growing shadow IT risk, as employees adopt consumer-grade AI platforms without fully understanding the data implications — including the potential for prompts and inputs to be used to train the AI model, to be retained by the platform provider, or to be accessible to other users of the platform. Employees must not input any Company data — including client data, employee data, financial data, proprietary business information, or any other information classified above Public tier — into any AI tool that has not been formally approved by IT, regardless of how useful or harmless the use might appear.
— Approved AI Tools Are Subject to Data Handling Standards. Where an AI tool has been formally approved for use with Company data, its use remains subject to all applicable data classification and handling standards. Employees using an approved AI tool must not input data of a higher classification tier than the tool has been approved to process, must comply with any specific usage guidelines published by IT for the relevant tool, and must critically evaluate AI-generated outputs before acting on them — particularly where the AI output is used to inform decisions with significant business, legal, or personal consequences. The responsibility for the accuracy and appropriateness of outputs generated by AI tools remains with the employee who uses those outputs, not with the AI system itself.

4.21 Vendor and Supplier Data Security
4.21.1 Vendor Due Diligence and Contractual Obligations
— Vendors Who Process Company Data Must Meet Security Standards. The Company's data security is only as strong as the weakest link in the chain of parties who process that data. Every vendor, supplier, contractor, or service provider who processes Company data — including personal data of employees or clients — on behalf of the Company must be subject to a formal security due diligence assessment before the business relationship commences. This assessment, managed by IT in conjunction with Procurement and Legal, evaluates the vendor's security policies, technical controls, certifications, incident response capability, and compliance with applicable data protection law. A vendor who fails to demonstrate an adequate security posture will not be engaged to process Company data until the identified deficiencies have been remediated and verified.
— Data Processing Agreements Are Mandatory. Every vendor who processes personal data on behalf of the Company must enter into a formal Data Processing Agreement before any personal data is shared. The DPA establishes the purposes for which the vendor may process the data, the security standards they must maintain, their obligations in the event of a security incident, their data retention and disposal obligations, and the Company's right to audit the vendor's compliance. No employee may authorize the sharing of personal data with a vendor who has not executed an approved DPA. Where a vendor requests access to Company data before the DPA is in place, the employee managing the relationship must escalate to Legal and IT immediately and must not share any data until the required agreement is executed.
4.21.2 Ongoing Vendor Security Management
— Vendor Security Is a Continuous Obligation. The Company's security obligations in relation to its vendors do not end at the point of due diligence and contracting. Vendors who process significant volumes of Company data, or who process Confidential or Restricted data, must be subject to ongoing security monitoring — including periodic security reassessment, review of any security incidents affecting the vendor's systems, and assessment of any material changes to the vendor's technology environment or security posture that might affect the data they process on the Company's behalf. Where a vendor experiences a security incident that involves Company data, the vendor must notify the Company within the timeframe specified in the DPA, and the Company's breach response process described in Section 4.16 will be activated immediately.

4.22 Employee Rights in Relation to Data Security
4.22.1 Employee Data Subject Rights
— Employees Have Rights Over Their Own Personal Data. As individuals whose personal data is processed by the Company as part of the employment relationship, all employees have rights under applicable data protection law in relation to that data. These rights include the right to access their own personal data held by the Company, the right to request correction of inaccurate personal data, the right to request erasure of personal data that is no longer required for the purpose for which it was collected, the right to object to certain types of processing, and the right to data portability in certain circumstances. The specific rights available to employees and the process for exercising them are described in full in the Company's Data Protection and Privacy Policy referenced as HR-DPP-001. Employees who wish to exercise their data subject rights must submit a formal request through the HR Helpdesk, which will be processed within the timelines required by applicable law.
— Employees Must Not Use Data Access to Circumvent Security Controls. The right to access personal data does not entitle an employee to access data about other individuals, to access Company operational data beyond what their role permits, or to use data subject rights mechanisms as a means of obtaining data for purposes other than the legitimate exercise of their privacy rights. Requests for data subject rights that appear designed to circumvent security controls or access restrictions will be reviewed carefully and, where misuse is suspected, referred to HR and Legal for appropriate action.

4.23 Violations and Disciplinary Consequences
4.23.1 Categories of Security Violations
— Minor Security Violations. Minor security violations are instances of non-compliant behavior that create a limited security risk and do not involve deliberate disregard for the policy. Examples include an isolated failure to lock a screen upon leaving a workstation, a single instance of storing a low-sensitivity document in an unapproved location without awareness that the location was unapproved, failing to apply a required software update within the specified timeframe on one occasion, or an inadvertent misdirected email containing Internal-tier data that is recognized and reported immediately. Minor violations are typically addressed through a coaching conversation and mandatory completion of targeted security awareness training. Where a pattern of minor violations emerges, the response will be escalated to the moderate violation framework.
— Moderate Security Violations. Moderate security violations are more serious breaches that involve repeated minor violations despite prior feedback, deliberate use of an unauthorized tool or channel for Company data, failure to report a suspected security incident in a timely manner, leaving a device unattended and unlocked in a public or shared location, or a first-time instance of sharing Confidential data through an unapproved channel without realizing the channel was unapproved. Moderate violations are addressed through a formal written warning, mandatory completion of targeted security training, and enhanced monitoring of the employee's compliance for a defined period.
— Serious and Gross Security Violations. Serious security violations involve significant and deliberate breaches of this policy — including deliberate circumvention of security controls, unauthorized access to systems or data for which the employee has not been authorized, deliberate sharing of Confidential or Restricted data with unauthorized parties, installation of unauthorized software that results in a security compromise, failure to report a known data breach, or deliberate introduction of malware or other malicious software into Company systems. Gross security violations — including deliberate data theft, unauthorized disclosure of Restricted data to a competitor or external party, deliberate sabotage of Company systems, or any other conduct that fundamentally destroys the trust and integrity on which the employment relationship depends — will result in immediate termination of employment without notice, potential referral to law enforcement authorities, and civil recovery action where appropriate.
4.23.2 The Disciplinary Response Framework
— Security Violations Are Addressed Under the Code of Conduct. All disciplinary action arising from violations of this Data Security Policy will be administered in accordance with the progressive disciplinary framework established in the Code of Conduct referenced as HR-COC-001. Every alleged violation will be the subject of a formal investigation conducted by IT Security and HR before any disciplinary outcome is determined. The employee will be informed of the specific concern in clear and unambiguous terms, will be given a full opportunity to present their perspective and any relevant context, and will be afforded all rights under the Code of Conduct's disciplinary process, including the right to representation and the right to appeal any formal disciplinary decision. The seriousness of the response will be calibrated to the severity of the violation, the employee's prior conduct record, and the impact of the violation on the Company and any affected individuals.

4.24 Training, Awareness, and Security Culture
4.24.1 Mandatory Security Training
— All Employees Must Complete Data Security Training. All employees are required to complete the Company's mandatory data security training program within thirty days of joining the Company and to complete a comprehensive refresher program annually thereafter. This training will cover the Company's data classification framework, the handling standards applicable to each classification tier, password and credential security, device and network security, phishing and social engineering awareness, the data breach reporting process, and the disciplinary consequences of policy violations. Completion of this training is not optional and will be monitored by IT through the HRMS. Failure to complete mandatory security training within the specified timeframe will be flagged as a compliance concern and addressed through the escalation process.
— Enhanced Training for Higher-Risk Roles. Certain roles and functions carry a heightened data security risk by virtue of the volume or sensitivity of the data they handle — including finance and payroll staff, HR professionals, IT administrators, legal staff, senior leaders, and employees in roles with elevated system access privileges. Employees in such roles will receive additional targeted training covering the specific security risks and obligations relevant to their function, in addition to the general data security training required of all employees. The IT Security function will identify the roles subject to enhanced training requirements and will maintain a current list of those roles in the HRMS.
4.24.2 Building a Culture of Security Consciousness
— Security Is a Daily Practice, Not an Annual Checkbox. The Company recognizes that mandatory annual training, however well-designed and well-delivered, is insufficient by itself to create and sustain a genuinely security-conscious organizational culture. Security must be a daily practice — present in every decision about how data is handled, in every interaction with a system, in every choice about where to store a document or how to share a file. The Company will therefore supplement mandatory training with regular security communications covering emerging threats and practical guidance, targeted awareness campaigns on specific security behaviors and risks, simulated phishing exercises that build practical threat recognition skills, recognition of security-conscious behavior in performance frameworks, and the creation of an organizational environment in which reporting security concerns, asking security questions, and raising security risks is genuinely welcomed, actively encouraged, and never penalized. The goal is an organization in which every employee thinks like a security professional — not because they are required to, but because they genuinely understand why it matters and have been equipped with the knowledge to act on that understanding.

4.25 Policy Compliance, Governance, and Review
4.25.1 Responsibility for Policy Compliance
— Every Employee's Personal Responsibility. Compliance with this Data Security Policy is the personal and non-delegable responsibility of every employee without exception. Employees are responsible for reading and understanding this policy in full, for applying its standards in every aspect of their work that involves Company data, for seeking guidance from the IT Security function or HR when they encounter a situation that is not clearly covered by this policy or where they are uncertain about the correct course of action, for reporting security concerns and incidents through the appropriate channels without delay, and for completing all mandatory security training within the required timeframes. Compliance with this policy is not optional, is not dependent on personal convenience, and is not subject to individual interpretation or selective application.
— Manager and Leadership Responsibility. Managers and leaders bear a heightened and additional responsibility for the security culture within their teams. They must model the security behaviors expected of all employees in their own conduct at all times, must ensure that their team members complete mandatory security training within the required timeframes, must create an environment in which team members feel safe and encouraged to report security concerns without fear of adverse consequence, must not create or condone workarounds that bypass security controls for operational convenience, and must cooperate fully with any security investigation or audit that involves their function or their team.
— IT Security and HR Responsibility. The IT Security function is responsible for the technical implementation and monitoring of this policy's requirements, for administering the data breach response process, for conducting the security audit program, for maintaining the approved tools and services lists, and for providing employees with the training, guidance, and support they need to comply with their obligations under this policy. HR is responsible for administering the employee-facing provisions of this policy, for managing the disciplinary consequences of security violations in conjunction with IT, and for ensuring that this policy is clearly communicated, consistently applied, and formally reviewed on the schedule described in Section 4.25.3.
4.25.2 Employee Acknowledgment
— Formal Acknowledgment Is Mandatory. All employees are required to formally acknowledge that they have read, understood, and agree to comply with this Data Security Policy. Acknowledgment will be captured through the HRMS at the time of onboarding for all new employees and upon each substantive update to the policy for all existing employees. The acknowledgment record will be maintained in the employee's personnel file. Failure to complete the acknowledgment within the timeline specified by HR will be treated as a compliance concern and addressed accordingly. Acknowledgment of this policy does not substitute for completion of the mandatory security training program — both requirements are independent and both must be fulfilled.
4.25.3 Annual Policy Review
— Regular Review to Maintain Relevance and Currency. This Data Security Policy will be formally reviewed on an annual basis by the IT Security function and HR, or earlier if required by a material change in the threat landscape or the Company's technology environment, a significant development in applicable data protection or cybersecurity legislation, the identification of a gap in the current policy revealed by a security incident, audit finding, or regulatory development, or feedback from employees, managers, or external assessors that reveals an area for improvement or updating. All updates will be approved by the Chief Human Resources Officer in conjunction with the Head of IT, communicated to all employees through the HRMS and official Company communication channels, and formally acknowledged by all employees within fifteen working days of communication. The Company's commitment to maintaining a current, rigorous, and genuinely effective Data Security Policy is both a legal obligation and a fundamental expression of its duty of care to the people whose data it holds and the clients whose trust it depends upon.
