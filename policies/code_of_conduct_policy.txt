POLICY 3: CODE OF CONDUCT POLICY

1. PURPOSE
This Code of Conduct sets out the behavioral and ethical standards that the organization expects of every person who acts on its behalf. It defines the values we stand for, the behaviors we require, and the boundaries we enforce. This Code is not merely a set of rules — it is an expression of who we are as an organization and the culture we are committed to building and protecting.

2. SCOPE
This Code applies to:
- ll employees at every level, including senior leaders and executives.
- Members of the Board of Directors.
- Contractors, consultants, agency workers, and temporary staff.
- Business partners, agents, and representatives acting on behalf of the organization.
- No individual is exempt from this Code on the basis of their seniority, tenure, or commercial value to the organization.

3. CORE VALUES
Everything in this Code is grounded in the organization's five core values:
- Integrity: We do what is right, even when no one is watching. We are honest, transparent, and trustworthy in all interactions.
- Respect: We treat every individual with dignity. We celebrate diversity and include everyone.
- Accountability: We take ownership of our actions and their consequences. We do not shift blame.
- Excellence: We hold ourselves to the highest professional standards. We are committed to continuous improvement.
- Collaboration: We work together, share knowledge generously, and succeed as a team.

4. PROFESSIONAL CONDUCT
4.1 General Behavior Standards
All employees are expected to:
- Conduct themselves in a professional, courteous, and respectful manner in all work-related interactions, whether in person, in writing, or online.
- Represent the organization positively when interacting with clients, partners, regulators, and the public.
- Comply with all applicable laws, regulations, and organizational policies at all times.
- Perform their duties with diligence, competence, and commitment.
- Exercise sound judgment and consider the ethical implications of their decisions.
- Maintain appropriate professional boundaries with colleagues, clients, and external parties.

4.2 Punctuality and Reliability
Employees are expected to attend scheduled meetings, meet deadlines, and honor commitments. Habitual non-delivery without legitimate justification is inconsistent with our culture of accountability.
4.3 Workplace Language and Communication
All workplace communication — verbal, written, or digital — must be professional, clear, and respectful. The use of offensive, discriminatory, threatening, harassing, or vulgar language in any workplace communication channel is strictly prohibited.

5. EQUALITY, DIVERSITY & INCLUSION
5.1 Equal Opportunity
The organization is an equal opportunity employer. Employment decisions — including hiring, promotion, compensation, discipline, and termination — are made based on qualifications, performance, and business need, never on the basis of any protected characteristic.
5.2 Non-Discrimination
Discrimination based on any protected characteristic — including but not limited to race, ethnicity, national origin, gender, gender identity or expression, sexual orientation, age, disability, religion, belief, pregnancy or maternity, marital status, socioeconomic background, or political affiliation — is strictly prohibited and will be treated as gross misconduct.
5.3 Anti-Harassment
Harassment of any kind — including sexual harassment, bullying, intimidation, victimization, or creating a hostile work environment — is a serious violation of this Code. Harassment is defined as unwanted conduct related to a protected characteristic that has the purpose or effect of violating a person's dignity or creating an intimidating, hostile, degrading, humiliating, or offensive environment.

Specific examples of prohibited conduct include, but are not limited to:
- Unwanted physical contact of a sexual nature.
- Sending explicit, offensive, or suggestive communications.
- Repeated unwanted romantic attention after a clear rejection.
- Making derogatory jokes or comments about a person's race, religion, gender, disability, or any other protected characteristic.
- Publicly humiliating, undermining, or belittling a colleague.
- Threatening or coercing an employee.
- Excluding or ostracizing a colleague based on a protected characteristic.
All complaints of harassment or discrimination will be investigated promptly, thoroughly, and impartially. Complainants will be protected from retaliation.

6. CONFLICTS OF INTEREST
6.1 Definition
A conflict of interest arises when an employee's personal interests — financial, professional, relational, or otherwise — conflict, or appear to conflict, with their duties and obligations to the organization.
6.2 Disclosure Obligation
Employees must promptly disclose any actual, potential, or perceived conflict of interest to their line manager and HR in writing. Disclosure does not automatically mean the employee has acted improperly — it creates the opportunity for appropriate management.
6.3 Common Conflicts of Interest
Situations requiring disclosure include but are not limited to:
- Employment or directorship with a competitor, supplier, or client.
- A significant financial interest in a competitor, supplier, or client.
- A close personal or romantic relationship with someone in the direct reporting line.
- A close personal or romantic relationship with a client, supplier, or competitor.
- Receiving or giving gifts, entertainment, or hospitality above the permitted thresholds.
- Using corporate resources, opportunities, or information for personal gain.

6.4 Outside Employment
Employees must disclose any secondary employment or self-employment activity that could constitute a conflict of interest, compete with the organization, or interfere with their ability to perform their primary role. Such activities require prior written approval from HR.

7. GIFTS, HOSPITALITY & ANTI-BRIBERY
7.1 Gifts and Hospitality Thresholds
Employees may give or receive modest gifts or hospitality in the normal course of business, provided they are:
- Not given or received in exchange for a business advantage, decision, or favor.
- Reasonable in value (no single gift or hospitality event may exceed the equivalent of USD 100 per person, unless pre-approved by a senior manager and registered in the Gifts Register).
- Appropriate in nature and would not embarrass the organization if disclosed publicly.
- Transparently recorded in the organizational Gifts and Hospitality Register.
7.2 Prohibited Conduct
The following are strictly prohibited under any circumstances:
- Offering, giving, requesting, or accepting bribes in any form — cash, gifts, favors, services, or any other benefit.
- Offering or accepting kickbacks or facilitation payments.
- Using a third party to make or receive payments that would violate this policy if made directly.
Violation of the anti-bribery provisions of this policy constitutes gross misconduct and will be reported to the relevant authorities.
7.3 Anti-Bribery Laws
The organization complies with all applicable anti-bribery and anti-corruption legislation, including the UK Bribery Act 2010, the US Foreign Corrupt Practices Act (FCPA), and equivalent laws in all jurisdictions where the organization operates.

8. CONFIDENTIALITY & INTELLECTUAL PROPERTY
8.1 Confidentiality Obligations
Employees have a continuing duty — during and after employment — to protect the confidentiality of all organizational information. This obligation extends to client data, business strategies, financial information, personnel information, and any other information classified as Confidential or Restricted.
8.2 Intellectual Property
All intellectual property created by an employee in the course of their employment — including inventions, software, designs, processes, reports, and creative works — belongs exclusively to the organization. Employees must not claim ownership of, reproduce, distribute, or misappropriate any organizational intellectual property.
8.3 Non-Solicitation
During employment, and for twelve (12) months following termination, employees must not solicit clients, business partners, or colleagues of the organization for personal commercial gain or for the benefit of a competing entity, unless expressly permitted by a written waiver from the organization.

9. USE OF CORPORATE ASSETS
9.1 General Principle
Corporate assets — including but not limited to office equipment, computers, mobile phones, vehicles, software, proprietary data, corporate credit cards, and office supplies — are provided for business use. Employees are responsible for using corporate assets appropriately, carefully, and in a manner consistent with organizational policy. Employee can not upload any work done on corporate assets to personal accounts or external storage devices without explicit permission.
9.2 Permitted Personal Use
Incidental personal use of corporate assets — such as brief personal phone calls or limited personal internet browsing — is permitted provided it:
- Does not interfere with the employee's work performance.
- Does not consume significant corporate resources.
- Does not involve access to inappropriate content.
- Is not for commercial personal gain.
9.3 Prohibited Uses of Corporate Assets
The following uses of corporate assets are strictly prohibited:
- Using corporate equipment or internet access to view, download, or distribute pornographic, violent, extremist, or otherwise illegal content.
- Using corporate vehicles under the influence of alcohol or drugs.
- Removing corporate assets from company premises without authorization.
- Selling, lending, or disposing of corporate assets without proper authorization.
- Using corporate credit cards or expense accounts for personal expenditure.
- Using corporate software licenses for personal projects or non-business purposes.

9.4 Care and Maintenance
Employees are expected to treat all corporate assets with care, report any damage or malfunction promptly, and return all assets in good condition upon termination of employment. The cost of replacement or repair of assets damaged due to employee negligence or misuse may be recovered from the employee.

10. SOCIAL MEDIA & PUBLIC COMMUNICATION
10.1 Personal Social Media
Employees are free to maintain personal social media accounts but must be mindful that their online conduct can impact the organization's reputation. Employees must not:
- Post, share, or comment on anything that is defamatory, discriminatory, or harassing toward colleagues, clients, or competitors.
- Disclose confidential organizational information.
- Identify themselves as a representative of the organization in personal social media posts without express authorization.
- Engage in political advocacy in a way that could be associated with the organization.

10.2 Corporate Communications
Only authorized personnel from the Marketing and Corporate Communications team may make official statements on behalf of the organization, whether to media, on corporate social media channels, or in public forums.

11. FINANCIAL INTEGRITY
All employees involved in financial processes — purchasing, invoicing, expense reporting, payroll, or financial reporting — must ensure that all transactions are accurate, properly authorized, fully documented, and in compliance with the organization's financial policies. Employees must never approve their own expenses and must never request or process fictitious transactions.

12. HEALTH, SAFETY & WELLBEING
All employees share a responsibility for maintaining a safe, healthy, and supportive working environment. Employees must:
- Follow all workplace health and safety procedures.
- Report unsafe conditions, accidents, or near-misses to the Health and Safety officer promptly.
- Not attend work or operate machinery while under the influence of alcohol, illegal drugs, or prescription medication that impairs judgment.
- Be considerate of the mental and emotional wellbeing of colleagues.
- Support the organization's Employee Assistance Programme (EAP) by referring colleagues in distress where appropriate.


13. WHISTLEBLOWING & REPORTING CONCERNS
13.1 Obligation to Speak Up
Every employee has not only the right but the responsibility to report any genuine concern about a potential breach of this Code, applicable law, regulatory requirement, or organizational policy. Silence in the face of known wrongdoing is itself a violation of this Code.
13.2 Reporting Channels
Concerns may be reported through any of the following channels:
- Direct line manager (where the concern does not involve that manager).
- HR Business Partner.
- Chief Ethics Officer or Legal Counsel.
- The organization's confidential Ethics Hotline (available 24/7, independently operated).
- Anonymous reporting through the Ethics Portal on the intranet.
13.3 Whistleblower Protection
The organization provides robust protections for employees who raise concerns in good faith. No employee will face retaliation — including dismissal, demotion, exclusion, harassment, or any other detriment — for making a protected disclosure. Any employee found to have retaliated against a whistleblower will face immediate disciplinary action.
13.4 Malicious Reports
Knowingly making a false or malicious report about a colleague is a disciplinary offense.

14. DISCIPLINARY CONSEQUENCES
Violations of this Code will be investigated and addressed through the organization's formal disciplinary procedure. Depending on the nature and severity of the breach, consequences range from a verbal warning to immediate termination without notice (gross misconduct). Certain violations — including bribery, fraud, sexual harassment, discrimination, and data theft — will automatically be treated as gross misconduct.
