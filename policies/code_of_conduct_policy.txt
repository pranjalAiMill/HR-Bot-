2. Code Of Conduct Policy

2.1 Purpose and Organizational Philosophy
2.1.1 Why This Policy Exists
— The Foundation of Organizational Integrity. The Code of Conduct Policy is the single most foundational behavioral document in the Company's entire policy framework. It establishes the ethical and professional standards that every individual associated with the Company is expected to uphold at all times, in every interaction, and across every circumstance. Where the Attendance Policy governs presence and time, the Code of Conduct governs character, behavior, and the fundamental ethical commitments that define who the Company is and how it operates in the world. This policy is not a bureaucratic formality. It is a declaration of the Company's values and a binding commitment from every employee to uphold those values in everything they do.
— The Organizational Imperative. The Company's reputation, its ability to retain clients, attract and retain talented people, build lasting partnerships, and operate with credibility in a competitive marketplace rests entirely on the collective behavior of its people. A single serious breach of ethical conduct — a case of fraud, a harassment incident, a conflict of interest mismanaged, a confidentiality breach — can inflict reputational damage that takes years to repair and in some cases can never be fully undone. The Code of Conduct is the organization's primary mechanism for preventing such events by establishing clear, unambiguous expectations before situations arise and enforcing them consistently and fairly when they are violated.
— The Human Imperative. This policy has been written with the genuine recognition that the vast majority of employees come to work every day with good intentions, sound ethical instincts, and a sincere desire to behave with integrity and professionalism. The Code of Conduct is not an expression of distrust in the Company's people. It is a tool of empowerment — a clear, comprehensive guide that equips every employee with the knowledge they need to navigate complex and sometimes ambiguous situations, to identify where ethical lines are drawn, and to understand how the Company will respond when those lines are crossed. It protects employees as much as it governs them.
— The Philosophy of Mutual Trust. This policy is built on the philosophy that the employment relationship is a relationship of mutual trust and respect. The Company trusts its employees to act with integrity, professionalism, and sound ethical judgment in every situation. In return, the Company commits to communicating its behavioral expectations with absolute clarity, applying its standards consistently and without favoritism at every level of the organization, creating an environment in which ethical behavior is recognized and rewarded, and responding to violations with fairness, proportionality, and full respect for due process. Trust must be earned and maintained by both parties, and this policy is the framework within which that mutual trust is honored and protected.
— A Living and Evolving Document. Like all enterprise policies, the Code of Conduct is a living document that will be reviewed, updated, and refined as the organization evolves, as the legal and regulatory environment changes, as social and cultural standards develop, and as practical experience provides insights into how the policy can be strengthened. Every employee is expected to read this policy fully, to raise questions where they need clarification, and to treat it as a meaningful and relevant guide to professional life rather than a document to be acknowledged and forgotten.

2.2 Scope and Applicability
2.2.1 Who This Policy Applies To
— Universal Application Without Exception. This policy applies universally and without exception to every individual engaged with the Company in any employment or engagement capacity. There are no exemptions based on seniority, tenure, professional standing, department, business unit, or geographic location. The most senior executive in the organization is held to exactly the same ethical standards as the most junior team member, and any perception that seniority insulates an individual from accountability under this policy will be actively and firmly rejected by the Company at every level.
— All Categories of Engagement Covered. The policy covers permanent full-time employees who form the core of the workforce, part-time employees engaged to work regular but reduced hours, employees on fixed-term or rolling contracts, interns and trainees engaged in structured learning programs, external consultants working on Company premises or accessing Company systems, temporary or agency staff, and any other individual who represents the Company or operates within its professional environment. Each of these individuals, regardless of the nature or duration of their engagement, is expected to observe the standards established in this policy in full.
— Seniority Confers Heightened Responsibility, Not Exemption. Senior leaders and managers are not exempt from this policy — they are held to a higher standard of conduct because of the influence they exercise over others. A leader who behaves unethically without consequence does more damage to the organization's culture than any junior employee doing the same, because it sends a powerful and destructive signal throughout the organization that ethics is a standard for some and not for all. The Company will not permit this signal to be sent.
2.2.2 Where This Policy Applies
— All Physical and Digital Settings. This policy applies at all times when an employee is acting in a professional capacity, representing the Company, or when their conduct has or may have an impact on the Company, its people, its clients, or its reputation. This includes conduct during working hours at Company premises, during working hours at client sites and external locations, during travel on Company business, at Company-organized events and social functions, in all digital communications made in a professional capacity, and in off-duty conduct that has a genuine connection to the employee's professional role or organizational affiliation.
— Remote and Hybrid Work Environments. The policy applies equally in physical workplaces and in remote or hybrid work environments. Working from home does not relax the conduct standards expected of an employee. Every provision of this policy applies to remote employees with the same force and effect as to on-site employees, and the expectation of professional conduct in all digital interactions — emails, video calls, messaging platforms, and any other communication medium — is identical to the expectation of conduct in physical settings.
2.2.3 Relationship With Other Policies
— The Parent Ethical Framework. The Code of Conduct is the parent ethical framework that underpins every other policy in the Company's policy library. It must be read in conjunction with the Information Security Policy referenced as HR-ISP-001, the Data Protection and Privacy Policy referenced as HR-DPP-001, the Attendance Policy referenced as HR-ATT-001, the Work From Home Policy referenced as HR-WFH-001, and all other applicable Company policies. Where a specific policy governs a particular area of conduct, that specific policy will apply. Where conduct concerns arise that fall outside the scope of any specific policy, this Code of Conduct will be the governing framework.
— Conflict Resolution. In the event of any apparent conflict between this Code and any other Company policy, HR will determine the applicable provision based on the specific circumstances. Employees should seek clarification through the HR Helpdesk rather than acting unilaterally on their own interpretation of a conflict between policies.

2.3 Core Values and Ethical Principles
2.3.1 The Five Core Values
— Integrity. Integrity is the commitment to doing the right thing at all times — even when no one is watching, even when it is inconvenient, and even when a different course of action might appear to offer a personal advantage. Integrity means being honest in communications, accurate and transparent in representations, consistent between stated values and actual behavior, and unwilling to compromise ethical standards for personal gain or professional convenience. An employee of integrity does not misrepresent facts, does not cut corners where doing so would harm others, does not behave differently in private than in public, and does not rationalize unethical behavior through self-serving reasoning. Integrity is not a situational value applied when convenient. It is a constant standard expected at all times, in all circumstances, and without exception.
— Respect. Respect is the active recognition that every individual within and outside the organization — regardless of their role, background, identity, nationality, religion, age, gender, disability, sexual orientation, or any other characteristic — possesses inherent human dignity that must be honored in every interaction without exception. Respect means listening to others with genuine attention, communicating with consideration and care, disagreeing with ideas through constructive and evidence-based dialogue rather than personal attack, treating every colleague as a professional equal deserving of fairness and courtesy, and never using language, behavior, or action that demeans, diminishes, marginalizes, or humiliates another person. Respect is not a luxury extended when convenient or when dealing with senior individuals. It is a baseline standard of conduct required in every interaction, regardless of the relative seniority or status of the individuals involved.
— Accountability. Accountability is the active willingness to take full ownership of one's actions, decisions, and their consequences — including both the intended and unintended outcomes of those decisions. An accountable employee does not deflect responsibility onto colleagues, circumstances, or systemic factors when their own choices contributed to a negative outcome. They acknowledge mistakes openly and honestly, seek to understand the root cause, commit to making things right, and use the experience as a genuine opportunity for growth and improvement. Accountability also encompasses the willingness to hold others to agreed standards in a spirit of constructive mutual responsibility — not as a punitive exercise, but as an expression of genuine care for the collective performance and integrity of the team and the organization.
— Professionalism. Professionalism is the consistent application of the highest standards of competence, conduct, communication, and personal presentation in every aspect of work. It encompasses punctuality, reliability, quality and accuracy of output, clarity and honesty in communication, appropriate conduct in meetings and collaborative settings, constructive engagement with clients and colleagues, and the consistent projection of an image that reflects well on both the individual and the organization. Professionalism is not about conformity or the suppression of individual personality. It is about bringing one's best self to work with consistency and discipline, earning and sustaining the trust and confidence of colleagues, clients, and stakeholders through reliable, high-quality, and ethically grounded professional conduct.
— Compliance. Compliance is the commitment to operating within the boundaries established by applicable law, regulation, and Company policy at all times, in all locations, and in all circumstances. A compliant employee understands the legal and regulatory framework within which the Company operates, keeps themselves informed of changes in that framework as they affect their role, seeks guidance from HR or Legal when uncertain about whether a proposed course of action is compliant, and never knowingly participates in conduct that violates applicable law or Company policy. Compliance is not optional, and it is not a technical box to be ticked. It is a fundamental professional obligation that protects the employee, their colleagues, the Company, and the broader public from harm.
2.3.2 How the Core Values Function as a Decision-Making Framework
— Values Are Integrated, Not Independent. The five core values are not independent silos. They are deeply interconnected and mutually reinforcing. Integrity without accountability becomes aspiration without consequence. Respect without professionalism becomes warmth without structure. Accountability without integrity becomes a punitive exercise disconnected from ethics. The Company expects its employees to embody all five values simultaneously and in integrated fashion in every aspect of their professional conduct, and to recognize when one value is being compromised in the name of another.
— A Practical Test for Ethical Decisions. When an employee faces a situation where they are uncertain about the right course of action, the five core values provide a practical and reliable decision-making framework. The employee should ask themselves five questions: Is this action honest and transparent in all its dimensions? Does it treat every person affected with dignity and respect? Am I prepared to take full and public accountability for the outcome? Does it reflect the highest standard of professional conduct I am capable of? And does it comply fully with applicable law and Company policy? If the honest answer to all five questions is yes, the action is almost certainly consistent with this Code of Conduct.

2.4 Professional Behavior and Workplace Standards
2.4.1 General Standards of Professional Conduct
— The Baseline Expectation. Every employee of the Company is expected to conduct themselves at all times with the level of professionalism that reflects well on the individual and on the organization they represent. This is not a vague aspiration. It is a concrete, day-to-day expectation that has real implications for how employees communicate, engage, manage their responsibilities, and represent the Company in every professional interaction.
— Respectful and Professional Communication. All communications within the Company — whether verbal, written, electronic, or non-verbal — must be conducted with respect, clarity, accuracy, and a tone appropriate to the professional context. Employees must avoid communication that is aggressive, dismissive, demeaning, condescending, or offensive in any form. This standard applies equally in face-to-face conversations, telephone calls, video conferences, emails, internal messaging platforms, and any other medium used in a professional capacity. The fact that a communication is informal or internal does not reduce the obligation to communicate respectfully and professionally.
— Constructive Conflict Resolution. Disagreements, differences of professional opinion, and interpersonal tensions are natural and unavoidable in any organizational environment. What the Company will not tolerate is the manner in which some individuals choose to handle such situations — through aggression, public confrontation, personal attacks, or the involvement of unauthorized third parties. Employees are expected to address disagreements constructively, through direct, respectful, and solution-oriented dialogue in the first instance. Where direct resolution proves impossible, the employee must escalate through the appropriate channels — to their reporting manager, to HR, or through the grievance process described in this policy.
— Reliability and Fulfillment of Professional Commitments. Professional conduct includes the consistent fulfillment of the commitments an employee makes to their colleagues, their manager, and their clients. This means meeting deadlines, completing tasks to the expected standard of quality, attending scheduled meetings, responding to communications in a timely manner, and being genuinely present and engaged during working hours. An employee who consistently fails to honor their professional commitments — regardless of the stated reason — is failing a fundamental standard of professional conduct that will be identified and addressed.
— Professional Conduct Toward Clients and External Stakeholders. Employees who interact with clients, customers, vendors, partners, or any other external stakeholders are representing the Company in those interactions. The conduct standard in such external interactions is at least as high as — and in many respects higher than — the standard applied in internal interactions. Every external interaction is an opportunity to build or damage the Company's reputation, and employees must approach all client and stakeholder interactions with the same respect, professionalism, accuracy, and integrity that the Company's values require.
2.4.2 Conduct That Is Always Unacceptable
— Illustrative Examples of Unacceptable Conduct. The following examples are provided to give employees concrete clarity about conduct that falls below the Company's minimum acceptable standard. This list is illustrative and not exhaustive. Any conduct that is inconsistent with the core values described in Section 2.3, regardless of whether it appears in this list, may be treated as a conduct violation.
— Dishonesty in Any Form. Dishonesty in any form — lying to a manager, misrepresenting qualifications or experience, submitting false expense claims, providing inaccurate information to clients, deliberately concealing material facts, or any other form of deliberate deception — is a fundamental breach of the Company's integrity standard and will be treated with the utmost seriousness, up to and including termination of employment regardless of the seniority or tenure of the individual involved.
— Insubordination. Insubordination — the deliberate and willful refusal to follow a reasonable and lawful instruction from a person in authority — is not acceptable conduct. Employees have the right to disagree with instructions and to raise concerns through appropriate channels. What they do not have the right to do is simply refuse compliance without cause. Where an employee genuinely believes an instruction is unlawful, unethical, or inappropriate, they must raise the concern through the whistleblowing or grievance process rather than unilaterally refusing to comply.
— Theft, Fraud, and Misappropriation of Any Kind. Any form of theft, fraud, or misappropriation of Company property, assets, funds, client property, or colleague property — regardless of the value involved — is an act of gross misconduct that will result in immediate disciplinary action, termination of employment, and referral to law enforcement authorities where circumstances warrant it. The value of what was taken is not the determining factor in the severity of the response. The deliberate dishonesty involved in any act of theft or fraud is what makes it a terminable offense.
— Discrimination and Harassment. Any form of discrimination, harassment, bullying, or victimization based on any protected or personal characteristic is an absolute violation of this Code of Conduct and will be treated with the highest level of disciplinary seriousness. These issues are addressed in dedicated detail in Sections 2.5, 2.6, and 2.7 of this policy.

2.5 Equal Employment Opportunity and Non-Discrimination
2.5.1 The Company's Commitment
— Unconditional Commitment to Equal Opportunity. The Company is fully and unconditionally committed to providing equal employment opportunity to all individuals without distinction of any kind. Every employment decision — recruitment, selection, compensation, promotion, training, performance management, disciplinary action, and termination — will be based solely on the individual's qualifications, competencies, skills, experience, and performance. No personal characteristic that is irrelevant to professional capability will ever be permitted to influence an employment decision.
— Protected Characteristics. The Company prohibits discrimination on the basis of any protected characteristic, including gender, race, caste, color, religion, national or ethnic origin, ancestry, age, disability, sexual orientation, gender identity or expression, marital or civil partnership status, pregnancy or maternity, family status, socioeconomic background, political opinion, or any other characteristic protected by applicable law in the jurisdictions in which the Company operates. This list will be reviewed and updated as applicable legislation evolves.
— Equal Opportunity at Every Stage of the Employment Lifecycle. The commitment to equal opportunity applies at every stage of employment without exception. It applies in recruitment — no candidate will be disadvantaged for who they are rather than what they are qualified to do. It applies in compensation — no employee will be paid less than a comparably performing colleague because of a protected characteristic. It applies in promotion and development — no employee will be denied advancement because of who they are. And it applies in disciplinary processes — no employee will be treated more or less harshly because of a personal characteristic.
2.5.2 Responsibilities Under the Equal Opportunity Commitment
— Every Employee's Responsibility. Every employee is responsible for ensuring that their conduct in all professional interactions is consistent with the Company's equal opportunity commitment. This means making all decisions about colleagues — whether as a manager making employment decisions, as a peer making recommendations, or as a team member engaging with others — based solely on professional merit and relevant competence, without allowing personal characteristics or unconscious biases to influence those decisions.
— Heightened Manager Responsibility. Managers bear a heightened responsibility because they make employment decisions that directly and materially affect the professional lives of the people in their teams. Managers must ensure all employment decisions are based on objective, documented, and professionally relevant criteria. They must be actively conscious of the potential for unconscious bias to influence their decisions and must seek to mitigate this risk through structured decision-making, diverse input where possible, and HR guidance. They must create team environments in which every member feels equally valued, equally supported, and equally empowered to contribute and succeed.
— Reporting Discrimination Concerns. Any employee who believes they or a colleague has been subjected to discrimination must report their concern immediately — to their reporting manager, directly to HR, or through the confidential reporting mechanism described in Section 2.17. Every report will be investigated promptly, thoroughly, and impartially. Employees who raise concerns in good faith will be protected from retaliation as described in Section 2.18.

2.6 Anti-Harassment and Anti-Bullying Policy
2.6.1 Definition and Understanding of Harassment
— What Constitutes Harassment. Harassment is any unwanted conduct that has the purpose or effect of violating an individual's dignity or of creating an intimidating, hostile, degrading, humiliating, or offensive work environment for that individual. Harassment may be based on a protected characteristic or on any other personal characteristic. It may be verbal, written, visual, physical, or psychological in nature. The form of the conduct is not what determines whether it constitutes harassment — the impact on the recipient does.
— A Single Incident Can Constitute Harassment. Harassment does not require a pattern of repeated behavior. A single incident of sufficiently serious conduct can constitute harassment. Employees must not seek comfort in the belief that a one-time incident is insufficient to establish a harassment concern. The Company will assess each situation based on the severity of the conduct and the impact on the recipient, and will act accordingly.
— Unintended Impact Does Not Excuse the Conduct. The fact that an employee did not intend their conduct to be harassing does not exempt them from accountability for the impact it caused. An employee who is informed by a colleague that their conduct has created a hostile or uncomfortable environment is expected to take that feedback immediately and seriously, to modify their behavior without defensiveness, and to report the situation to HR so that appropriate support and intervention can be provided.
2.6.2 Definition and Understanding of Bullying
— What Constitutes Bullying. Bullying is repeated, persistent, and deliberate conduct toward an individual or group that is intended to intimidate, demean, undermine, or harm the target and that a reasonable person would recognize as inappropriate and harmful in a professional environment. Bullying typically involves a real or perceived imbalance of power that the perpetrator exploits to target the victim over a sustained period.
— Examples of Bullying Conduct. Bullying conduct includes persistent unreasonable criticism designed to demean rather than develop, deliberate exclusion from team activities or communications, the setting of unachievable targets designed to create failure, public humiliation in meetings or group settings, spreading malicious rumors or false information, the deliberate withholding of information needed to perform a role, persistent personal insults or mockery, and the use of a managerial position to subject a subordinate to unfair treatment in retaliation for a legitimate complaint or disagreement.
— Legitimate Management Action is Not Bullying. Constructive critical feedback on performance, the setting of demanding but achievable targets, the initiation of a genuine and fair disciplinary process, or direct and factual communication about conduct concerns are not bullying. They are legitimate management actions. What distinguishes legitimate management from bullying is whether the conduct is reasonable, proportionate, professionally motivated, and conducted in a manner that respects the dignity of the individual.
2.6.3 Zero Tolerance Position and Consequences
— Absolute Prohibition. The Company maintains an absolute zero-tolerance position on harassment and bullying of any kind. No instance of harassment or bullying will be dismissed, minimized, or excused on any grounds. Every report will be taken seriously, investigated thoroughly, and addressed with disciplinary consequences proportionate to the severity and nature of the conduct.
— Consequences. Substantiated harassment or bullying will result in disciplinary action ranging from a formal written warning for less severe first-time instances to termination of employment for serious, sustained, or repeated misconduct. The seniority or tenure of the perpetrator will not be treated as a mitigating factor that reduces the consequence. In cases where the conduct may constitute a criminal offense, the Company will refer the matter to law enforcement authorities as appropriate.

2.7 Prevention of Sexual Harassment — POSH Act Compliance
2.7.1 The Company's Commitment Under the POSH Act
— Legal and Ethical Obligation. The Company is fully committed to providing a workplace entirely free from sexual harassment in strict compliance with the Sexual Harassment of Women at Workplace (Prevention, Prohibition and Redressal) Act, 2013 — the POSH Act — and all applicable rules and regulations thereunder. This commitment is simultaneously a legal obligation and a fundamental expression of the Company's values of respect, dignity, and equal treatment.
— Definition of Sexual Harassment. Sexual harassment includes any unwelcome sexually determined behavior — whether directly or by implication — including unwelcome physical contact and advances, a demand or request for sexual favors, making sexually colored remarks, displaying pornographic material, and any other unwelcome physical, verbal, or non-verbal conduct of a sexual nature. The POSH Act recognizes two forms: quid pro quo harassment, where submission to sexual conduct is made a condition of employment or professional benefit, and hostile work environment harassment, where sexually charged conduct unreasonably interferes with work performance or creates an intimidating, hostile, or offensive environment.
— Broader Coverage by the Company. While the POSH Act mandates protections specifically for women, the Company extends its broader anti-harassment protections to all employees regardless of gender, as described in Section 2.6 of this policy. No employee of any gender will be subjected to unwelcome sexual conduct without the Company taking appropriate and serious action.
2.7.2 The Internal Complaints Committee
— Establishment and Composition. In full compliance with the POSH Act, the Company has established an Internal Complaints Committee composed in accordance with the Act's requirements — with a senior woman employee as Presiding Officer, two members committed to the cause of women employees, and one external member from an organization or association committed to women's rights or familiar with sexual harassment issues.
— Making a Complaint. Any woman employee who has been subjected to sexual harassment may submit a written complaint to the ICC within three months of the date of the incident or the last incident in a series of incidents. The ICC will acknowledge receipt within the prescribed period and will conduct the inquiry in accordance with the timelines and procedures established by the POSH Act and the Company's Internal Complaints Procedure.
— Confidentiality of ICC Proceedings. All ICC proceedings — including the identities of the complainant, respondent, and witnesses — will be kept strictly confidential to the fullest extent permitted by law. Any breach of confidentiality by any participant in the process will itself be treated as a serious conduct violation.
— Mandatory POSH Awareness Training. All employees will receive mandatory POSH awareness training upon joining the Company and refreshed at least annually thereafter. Managers will receive additional targeted training on their specific responsibilities under the Act and this policy.

2.8 Conflict of Interest
2.8.1 Understanding and Identifying Conflicts of Interest
— Definition. A conflict of interest arises whenever an employee's personal interests — financial, relational, social, or otherwise — conflict with, or have the potential to conflict with, their professional obligations and duties to the Company. The mere existence of a situation where personal and professional interests are in tension creates a conflict of interest requiring disclosure and management, even if the employee has not yet acted improperly. The risk of influence is what creates the obligation to disclose.
— Types of Conflicts. Financial conflicts arise when an employee holds a material personal financial interest in an organization that does business with or competes with the Company. Relational conflicts arise when an employee has a personal relationship with a client, vendor, candidate, or colleague that may influence or appear to influence professional decisions. Secondary employment conflicts arise when an employee engages in paid work for another organization that creates a tension with their primary professional obligations. Reputational conflicts arise when an employee's external activities or affiliations may embarrass or harm the Company.
— The Paramount Importance of Disclosure. The most important obligation in relation to conflicts of interest is proactive disclosure. Employees who identify an actual or potential conflict must disclose it in writing to their reporting manager and HR at the earliest possible opportunity. Disclosure protects both the employee and the Company. It is always better to disclose a conflict that requires no action than to fail to disclose one that subsequently taints a professional decision.
2.8.2 Managing Disclosed Conflicts
— Review and Response. HR will review every disclosed conflict in consultation with the relevant manager and, where appropriate, senior leadership. The response may range from simply documenting the disclosure and requiring no further action, to requiring the employee to recuse themselves from specific decisions, to requiring divestment of a financial interest, or to reassigning the employee to a different role, depending on the severity of the conflict.
— Failure to Disclose is a Serious Violation. An employee who knowingly fails to disclose an actual or potential conflict of interest — particularly one that subsequently influences or appears to influence a professional decision — is committing a serious breach of the Company's integrity standards. Such failure may result in termination of employment depending on the nature of the conflict and the decisions it affected.

2.9 Confidentiality and Protection of Company Information
2.9.1 What Constitutes Confidential Information
— Broad Definition. Confidential information encompasses all information relating to the Company, its clients, its employees, its operations, its financial performance, its strategic plans, and its intellectual property that is not publicly known and that the Company has a legitimate interest in protecting. This includes technical information, business strategies, client lists and contact details, pricing structures, contractual terms, personnel information, financial data, product development plans, and any other information that a reasonable professional would recognize as sensitive and proprietary.
— The Duty of Confidentiality Applies Always. Every employee has a duty to maintain the confidentiality of Company information at all times — during employment and after its end. This obligation is inherent in the employment relationship and does not depend on the existence of a separate non-disclosure agreement. It is a fundamental expression of the trust the Company places in its employees.
— How Confidentiality is Maintained in Practice. Employees fulfill the confidentiality obligation by never discussing confidential Company information in public settings where it may be overheard, never sharing sensitive information with family members, friends, or external contacts, never transmitting confidential information through unsecured channels or personal devices, never posting confidential information on social media or external platforms, locking screens and securing documents when stepping away from their workstation, and reporting any accidental disclosure to HR and IT Security immediately upon discovery.
2.9.2 Client and Employee Information — Special Obligations
— Client Information is Among the Most Sensitive. Client information — identifying details, contact data, business plans, financial information, and the nature of the Company's services — must be protected with exceptional care, both because of the Company's contractual and ethical obligations to its clients and because unauthorized disclosure of client information can expose the Company to significant legal liability in addition to conduct consequences under this policy.
— Employee Personal Information. Information relating to colleagues — including their compensation, performance ratings, personal circumstances, health conditions, or disciplinary history — is strictly confidential and must never be discussed with or disclosed to any individual who does not have a direct and professional need to know. Employees who have access to such information through their role must handle it with absolute discretion.

2.10 Responsible Use of Company Assets and Resources
2.10.1 The Obligation of Responsible Use
— Assets Exist for Business Purposes. All assets and resources provided by the Company to employees — physical equipment, technology systems, vehicles, financial resources, intellectual property, and the Company's time and professional reputation — are provided exclusively to enable employees to fulfill their professional responsibilities. Employees must use these assets responsibly, carefully, and for legitimate business purposes only, unless the Company has expressly authorized specific personal use in writing.
— Care and Maintenance of Physical Assets. Employees entrusted with Company physical assets — laptops, mobile phones, vehicles, tools, or other equipment — are personally responsible for taking reasonable care of those assets, using them appropriately, reporting damage or malfunction promptly, and returning them in good condition upon request or at the cessation of employment. Willful damage, negligent handling, or unauthorized permanent retention of Company assets is a conduct violation with potential financial and disciplinary consequences.
— Financial Resources and Expense Claims. Employees authorized to incur expenses on the Company's behalf must exercise the same judgment with Company funds as a prudent, ethical professional would apply to their own money. All expenditures must be legitimate, necessary, authorized, and accurately documented with appropriate supporting evidence. The submission of false, inflated, or unauthorized expense claims is a form of fraud and will be treated with the full severity of the disciplinary framework.
2.10.2 Prohibition on Use of Company Resources for Personal Gain
— Absolute Prohibition. Employees must not use Company assets, resources, systems, or their employment position for personal financial gain, personal commercial advantage, or the benefit of external individuals or organizations, except where the Company has expressly and specifically authorized such use. This prohibition covers using Company equipment for personal commercial activities, using Company information for personal investments, using the Company's name to secure personal benefits, and every other form of leveraging Company resources for personal advantage.
— Incidental Personal Use. The Company acknowledges that de minimis incidental personal use of certain resources — a brief personal call on a Company mobile, a short personal task on a Company laptop during a break — is a practical reality that does not constitute a conduct violation provided it is occasional, brief, does not affect productivity or security, and does not involve sensitive information. Systematic, frequent, or prolonged personal use of Company resources is not incidental and will be treated as misuse.

2.11 Information Technology and Digital Resource Usage
2.11.1 Appropriate Use of IT Systems
— IT Systems Are Business Tools. The Company's IT systems — computers, servers, email, communication platforms, cloud storage, software, internet access, and all other digital infrastructure — are provided to enable employees to perform their professional responsibilities. Employees must use these systems responsibly, securely, and in strict accordance with the Company's Information Security Policy referenced as HR-ISP-001.
— Prohibited IT Uses. Employees must not use Company IT systems to access, download, store, or transmit material that is illegal, offensive, obscene, or inconsistent with the Company's values. They must not attempt to access systems or data for which they are not authorized. They must not install unauthorized software on Company devices. They must not use Company systems to conduct personal commercial activities. Any violation of these prohibitions will be treated as a conduct violation with disciplinary consequences.
— Monitoring of IT Systems. The Company reserves the right to monitor the use of its IT systems to the extent permitted by applicable law, for legitimate purposes including ensuring compliance with this policy and the Information Security Policy, detecting security threats, and investigating suspected misconduct. Employees should be aware that their use of Company systems may be monitored and must conduct themselves accordingly at all times.
2.11.2 Password Security and Credential Protection
— Personal Responsibility for Credentials. Every employee is personally responsible for the security of their Company system credentials. Employees must create strong, unique passwords for all Company accounts, must not share credentials with any other individual under any circumstances, must not record credentials in unsecured locations, must enable multi-factor authentication where required, and must change passwords regularly or whenever they believe their credentials may have been compromised.
— Immediate Reporting of Compromise. If an employee believes their Company credentials have been compromised through any means — phishing, malware, or accidental disclosure — they must immediately report this to the IT department and HR. Prompt reporting allows protective action. Delayed reporting that results in a security breach will be treated as a conduct concern in addition to the security incident itself.

2.12 Social Media and External Communication Standards
2.12.1 General Social Media Conduct
— Professional and Personal Identity Are Connected. In the digital age, the boundary between professional and personal identity is often blurred, particularly on platforms where an employee's organizational affiliation is visible. Employees must conduct themselves on all social media platforms in a manner consistent with the Company's values and must be mindful that their online conduct may reflect on the Company's reputation.
— What Employees May and May Not Post. Employees are free to engage personally on social media subject to this section. They may share professional insights, industry observations, and general experiences that are positive and consistent with their confidentiality obligations. Employees must not post confidential Company, client, or colleague information. They must not make statements that could be interpreted as defamatory, discriminatory, harassing, or harmful to the Company, its employees, or any other individual or organization. They must not purport to represent the Company's official position unless expressly authorized to do so.
— Standards Apply Around the Clock. The social media standards in this policy apply at all times — not only during working hours. A post made at midnight on a personal device is subject to the same standards as any other professional communication if it relates to the Company, its people, or its clients.
2.12.2 Authorized External Communications
— Only Authorized Spokespersons May Represent the Company Publicly. All formal external communications on behalf of the Company — including media interviews, press statements, public speeches, published articles, and official social media posts in the Company's name — must be made only by individuals who have been formally authorized to speak on the Company's behalf. Employees who are approached by media or external parties seeking comment on Company matters must redirect the inquiry to the authorized spokesperson and must not respond substantively without authorization.
— Consequences of Unauthorized External Communications. Employees who make unauthorized public statements about the Company, its clients, its employees, or its business affairs — whether in interviews, social media posts, blogs, or any other forum — may cause significant reputational, legal, and commercial harm to the Company. Such conduct will be treated as a serious conduct violation with disciplinary consequences proportionate to the harm caused.

2.13 Substance Abuse and Intoxication Policy
2.13.1 Prohibition on Workplace Substance Use
— Absolute Prohibition. The Company maintains an absolute prohibition on the possession, consumption, distribution, or being under the influence of alcohol, illegal drugs, or any other intoxicating substance while on Company premises, while conducting Company business at any location, while traveling on Company business, or while attending any Company-organized event where the employee is in a professional capacity. This prohibition applies during all working hours and extends to any situation where the employee is representing or acting on behalf of the Company.
— Why This Standard Exists. The prohibition on substance use in the workplace exists for multiple compelling reasons. The safety of every individual in the workplace depends on all colleagues being fully alert, capable of sound judgment, and free from impairment. Many roles within the Company involve equipment, systems, client interactions, or financial decision-making where impairment creates direct and serious risk. Beyond safety, the presence of an intoxicated individual in a professional environment is deeply damaging to the Company's culture and its professional reputation with clients and visitors.
— Company-Sponsored Events. Where the Company organizes events at which alcohol is made available — such as team celebrations or client entertainment — employees are expected to exercise personal responsibility and professional judgment in their consumption. The availability of alcohol at a Company event does not suspend the Company's conduct standards. Employees who become visibly intoxicated at Company events, behave inappropriately as a result of alcohol consumption, or drive under the influence after a Company event will be subject to disciplinary action.
2.13.2 Support for Employees with Substance-Related Challenges
— Compassionate Approach to Dependency. The Company recognizes that substance dependency is a health condition that may require professional support and treatment. Employees who are struggling with alcohol or drug dependency are strongly encouraged to seek help proactively, either through the Company's Employee Assistance Program where available or through independent professional channels, before the dependency creates a conduct or performance issue in the workplace. The Company will approach disclosed dependency with appropriate sensitivity and will work with the affected employee to support their recovery, subject to the employee engaging genuinely and consistently with the support process.
— Limits of Compassionate Support. The Company's compassion for employees with substance-related health challenges does not extend to tolerating workplace intoxication, dangerous behavior arising from substance use, or repeated non-engagement with offered support. Where an employee's substance-related conduct creates safety risks, performance failures, or conduct violations despite offered support, the Company will address the situation through the disciplinary framework.

2.14 Health, Safety, and Workplace Security
2.14.1 The Company's Health and Safety Commitment
— A Fundamental Organizational Obligation. The Company is fully committed to providing and maintaining a workplace that is safe, healthy, and secure for every employee, visitor, client, and contractor who enters its premises or engages with its operations. This commitment is simultaneously a legal obligation under applicable health and safety legislation and a fundamental expression of the Company's duty of care to its people.
— Every Employee Has a Safety Role. Health and safety in the workplace is not solely the responsibility of the Company's leadership or a dedicated safety function. Every employee has a personal responsibility to contribute to a safe working environment. This means following all health and safety procedures and guidelines, using safety equipment and protective gear where required, reporting hazardous conditions or near-miss incidents to the appropriate person promptly, not interfering with or misusing safety equipment, and cooperating fully with the Company's health and safety management processes.
— Reporting Safety Concerns. Employees who identify a safety hazard, a near-miss incident, or a condition that creates a risk to health or safety must report it immediately to their reporting manager, to the designated Health and Safety Officer, or through the Company's established safety reporting mechanism. Reporting safety concerns is not only a right but a professional obligation. An employee who identifies a safety risk and fails to report it is putting themselves and their colleagues at unnecessary risk and may be held accountable for that failure.
2.14.2 Workplace Security
— Physical Security Obligations. Every employee is responsible for contributing to the physical security of the Company's premises. This includes wearing or displaying their Company identification at all times on Company premises, challenging or reporting unaccompanied visitors or individuals without visible identification in restricted areas, following all physical access control procedures, not holding secure doors open for individuals who have not independently authenticated their access, and reporting any suspicious activity or security concern to the security function or management immediately.
— Securing Work Materials and Systems. Employees must lock their workstations when stepping away from their desks, secure physical documents containing sensitive information when not in use, use clear desk practices to prevent unauthorized access to Company information, and ensure that confidential conversations are not held in areas where they may be overheard by unauthorized individuals.

2.15 Anti-Bribery, Anti-Corruption, and Gifts Policy
2.15.1 Absolute Prohibition on Bribery and Corruption
— Zero Tolerance. The Company maintains an absolute zero-tolerance position on bribery and corruption in all their forms, in all jurisdictions in which it operates, and in all dealings with public officials, private individuals, clients, vendors, and any other parties. No employee may offer, give, request, or accept any bribe, kickback, facilitation payment, or other improper financial or non-financial advantage in connection with the Company's business, regardless of local custom or practice, regardless of the value involved, and regardless of whether the other party initiates the offer.
— Why This Standard Cannot Be Compromised. Bribery and corruption distort fair competition, undermine the integrity of business relationships, expose the Company and its employees to severe criminal, civil, and regulatory consequences, and are fundamentally inconsistent with the Company's core value of integrity. No business justification, no competitive pressure, and no instruction from a manager or superior can authorize an employee to engage in bribery or corruption. An instruction to engage in corrupt conduct is not a lawful instruction and must be refused and immediately reported.
— Anti-Corruption Obligations Extend to Third Parties. The Company's anti-bribery and anti-corruption obligations extend to the conduct of third parties acting on its behalf — including agents, intermediaries, distributors, and any other external party engaged to represent or act for the Company. Employees who have oversight of such relationships are required to exercise appropriate due diligence to ensure that third parties are not engaging in corrupt conduct on the Company's behalf.
2.15.2 Gifts, Hospitality, and Entertainment
— The Risk That Gifts Create. The receipt and giving of gifts, hospitality, and entertainment in a business context creates a genuine risk of actual or perceived conflict of interest and can in some circumstances constitute bribery. The Company's approach to gifts and hospitality is therefore one of active, documented management rather than prohibition of all exchanges, which would be impractical in many business environments.
— Permitted Gifts and Hospitality. Gifts of modest value — generally defined as items below a threshold specified in the Company's Gifts Register maintained by HR — that are given or received in the ordinary course of business, that are transparently disclosed and recorded, that would not embarrass the Company if publicly known, and that have no reasonable potential to create a sense of obligation or to influence a business decision, may be accepted or given subject to prior disclosure and registration. The specific monetary threshold will be communicated by HR and reviewed periodically.
— Mandatory Disclosure and Registration. Every gift received by an employee, regardless of value, must be disclosed to HR and recorded in the Company's official Gifts Register. Every gift offered by an employee on the Company's behalf must be pre-approved by the reporting manager and recorded in the same register. An employee who receives a gift that exceeds the permitted threshold must either decline it gracefully, explaining the Company's policy, or immediately surrender it to HR for appropriate disposition. Failure to disclose a received gift is a conduct violation.

2.16 Financial Integrity and Fraud Prevention
2.16.1 The Commitment to Financial Integrity
— Accuracy in All Financial Records. The Company is committed to the highest standards of financial integrity across all its operations, financial reporting, and internal controls. All financial records — including revenue records, expense reports, payroll records, vendor invoices, client billing, and any other financial documentation — must be accurate, complete, honestly prepared, and free from manipulation or misrepresentation of any kind.
— Employee Responsibility. Every employee who has any involvement in the creation, processing, approval, or review of financial records and transactions — regardless of their level or function — bears a personal responsibility for the accuracy and integrity of those records. Employees must never record transactions that do not reflect economic reality, must never approve expenditures that are not legitimate and properly authorized, and must never manipulate records to produce a financial result that differs from the true position.
2.16.2 Fraud — Definition, Prevention, and Response
— What Constitutes Fraud. Fraud encompasses any deliberate act or omission intended to deceive the Company or any of its stakeholders for financial or other personal gain. This includes misappropriation of Company funds or assets, submission of false or inflated expense claims, manipulation of financial records or systems, unauthorized alteration of vendor or payroll records, procurement fraud, and any other conduct designed to create a false financial impression or to obtain a financial benefit to which the perpetrator is not entitled.
— Zero Tolerance and Immediate Response. Every credible allegation or discovery of fraud will trigger an immediate formal investigation conducted by HR in conjunction with Finance and, where appropriate, external forensic specialists or law enforcement authorities. Where fraud is substantiated, the perpetrator will be subject to termination of employment, civil recovery action, and criminal prosecution as appropriate. There is no lower limit on the value of fraud that will trigger this response. Fraud of any amount is treated with the same gravity.
— Creating an Environment for Fraud Prevention. Fraud prevention is a collective organizational responsibility. The Company maintains a system of financial internal controls designed to detect and deter fraudulent activity. Every employee is responsible for adhering to those controls, for reporting any control weakness or unusual financial activity they observe, and for never participating in or facilitating conduct that bypasses established financial controls for any reason.

2.17 Whistleblowing and Reporting Misconduct
2.17.1 The Importance of Speaking Up
— Why Reporting Matters. The Company's ability to identify, investigate, and address misconduct depends in large part on the willingness of employees to speak up when they observe conduct that is inconsistent with this Code of Conduct or with applicable law and regulation. In many cases, the individuals most aware of misconduct are those closest to it — colleagues, team members, and direct reports. The Company therefore places enormous value on the willingness of employees to report concerns, and has put in place multiple accessible, confidential, and protected reporting mechanisms to make speaking up as easy and safe as possible.
— What Should Be Reported. Employees are encouraged and expected to report any conduct that they reasonably believe constitutes a violation of this Code of Conduct, a breach of applicable law or regulation, a financial fraud or irregularity, a safety hazard or health risk, harassment, discrimination, or bullying, a conflict of interest that has not been disclosed, or any other conduct that they believe is harmful to the Company, its employees, its clients, or the public. Employees do not need to be certain that misconduct has occurred before reporting. A genuine and reasonable concern is sufficient to trigger the reporting obligation.
2.17.2 How to Report
— Multiple Reporting Channels. The Company provides multiple channels through which employees may report concerns, to ensure that every employee has a reporting option that they feel comfortable using. These channels include reporting directly to the employee's reporting manager where the concern does not involve the manager, reporting directly to HR where the concern involves the manager or where the employee is more comfortable with HR involvement, reporting to the Head of HR or to the Chief Human Resources Officer for concerns involving senior management, and where available, using the Company's confidential anonymous reporting hotline or ethics reporting platform.
— Anonymous Reporting. Where the Company provides an anonymous reporting facility, employees may submit concerns without identifying themselves. Anonymous reports will be investigated to the fullest extent possible given the information provided. Employees should be aware, however, that anonymous reports may be more difficult to investigate thoroughly than identified reports, and that the Company cannot provide direct feedback or follow-up to an anonymous reporter.
2.17.3 Confidentiality of Reports
— Confidentiality is Protected. All reports made through any of the channels described above will be treated with the utmost confidentiality to the fullest extent permitted by law and by the requirements of a fair investigation. The identity of the reporter will not be disclosed to any person who does not have a direct and necessary role in the investigation, and the existence of the report will not be more widely shared than is strictly necessary for the conduct of a fair and thorough investigation.
— Limits of Confidentiality. In certain circumstances, complete confidentiality may not be achievable — for example, where the nature of the allegation makes it evident who the reporter is likely to be, or where disclosing the reporter's identity is necessary for the respondent to receive a fair hearing. In such cases, the Company will manage the situation with as much care and sensitivity as possible, will discuss the situation with the reporter where feasible, and will take all reasonable steps to protect the reporter from retaliation.

2.18 Retaliation — Prohibition and Consequences
2.18.1 Absolute Prohibition on Retaliation
— Zero Tolerance for Retaliation. Retaliation against any employee who reports a genuine concern in good faith, participates in an investigation, or exercises any right under this Code of Conduct or under applicable law is strictly and absolutely prohibited. Retaliation in any form — including adverse changes to duties or working conditions, reduction in compensation or benefits, exclusion from team activities, unfair performance assessments, threats, intimidation, or any other conduct designed to discourage reporting or punish the reporter — will itself be treated as a serious conduct violation subject to disciplinary action up to and including termination.
— Why This Protection is Essential. Without credible protection from retaliation, no reporting mechanism can be effective. Employees will only speak up about genuine concerns if they trust that doing so will not cost them their career, their relationships, or their professional standing. The Company's absolute commitment to protecting good-faith reporters from retaliation is the foundation on which the entire whistleblowing framework rests. This commitment will be upheld at every level of the organization, and any manager or colleague who retaliates against a reporter will be held fully accountable.
— Protecting Good Faith Does Not Protect Bad Faith. The protection from retaliation applies specifically to reports made in good faith — that is, where the reporting employee genuinely and reasonably believed the reported conduct was a violation of this policy or of applicable law. Employees who make reports that they know to be false, fabricated, or maliciously motivated are not protected from retaliation and may themselves be subject to disciplinary action for making a deliberately false report.

2.19 Political Activities and Personal Beliefs at the Workplace
2.19.1 Personal Political Activities
— The Right to Personal Political Views. The Company respects every employee's right to hold personal political beliefs and to participate in lawful political activities in their private capacity and outside of working hours. These rights are a fundamental aspect of individual freedom that the Company does not seek to curtail.
— Separation of Personal Political Activities from the Company. Employees must maintain a clear and complete separation between their personal political activities and their professional role with the Company. Employees must not use Company time, resources, equipment, or facilities for personal political activities. Employees must not make statements or take actions that could be interpreted as the Company endorsing or opposing any political party, candidate, policy, or cause, unless the Company has formally and explicitly taken such a position through its authorized spokespersons.
- Political Neutrality in the Workplace. Employees must not subject their colleagues to unsolicited political advocacy in the workplace, must not attempt to use their professional position or relationships to advance personal political objectives, and must not allow personal political views to influence professional decisions relating to colleagues, clients, or vendors. The workplace must remain a politically neutral environment in which all employees feel respected and comfortable regardless of their personal political beliefs.

2.20 Intellectual Property and Proprietary Information
2.20.1 Ownership of Work Product
— Company Ownership of Work Created in Employment. All intellectual property created by an employee in the course of their employment — including inventions, innovations, software code, written materials, designs, methodologies, processes, client solutions, and any other creative or technical work product produced using Company time, resources, or information — is the property of the Company. Employees have no personal ownership claim over work product created in the course of their employment, regardless of whether the creation occurred during or outside of standard working hours, provided the creation is connected to the employee's role or to the Company's business.
- Assignment of Intellectual Property Rights. By accepting employment with the Company, every employee assigns to the Company all intellectual property rights in all work product created in the course of their employment. This assignment is comprehensive and global. Employees may be required to sign specific intellectual property assignment agreements as a condition of employment or of participation in specific projects, and must do so promptly upon request.
2.20.2 Respect for Third-Party Intellectual Property
— Prohibition on Unauthorized Use. Employees must not use, copy, reproduce, distribute, or incorporate any third-party intellectual property — including software, written materials, images, designs, data, or any other protected work — in any Company work product or activity without obtaining the appropriate license or permission and ensuring compliance with the terms of that license. Unauthorized use of third-party intellectual property exposes the Company to legal liability and is a conduct violation under this policy.
— Responsibility of Employees Handling Third-Party IP. Employees who regularly work with or make decisions about the use of third-party intellectual property — including software developers, designers, content creators, and procurement professionals — bear a heightened responsibility to understand the intellectual property rights applicable to the materials they work with, to seek appropriate guidance when uncertain, and to escalate concerns about potential IP infringement to the Legal function before any potentially infringing use occurs.

2.21 Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion Standards
2.21.1 The Company's Commitment to DEI
— More Than Compliance. The Company's commitment to diversity, equity, and inclusion goes beyond legal compliance with anti-discrimination obligations. It is a genuine organizational belief that diverse teams — teams that bring together individuals from different backgrounds, experiences, perspectives, and identities — consistently produce better thinking, more creative solutions, more resilient operations, and stronger business outcomes than homogeneous teams. The Company is committed to building and sustaining a workforce that reflects and celebrates the full range of human diversity in all its dimensions.
— What DEI Means in Practice. Diversity means actively recruiting, developing, and retaining individuals from a wide range of backgrounds, identities, and experiences. Equity means ensuring that every employee has access to the opportunities, resources, and support they need to succeed — recognizing that equal treatment does not always produce equal outcomes and that some employees may need different levels of support to reach the same starting point. Inclusion means creating a workplace culture in which every individual feels genuinely welcomed, respected, valued, and empowered to contribute their best.
— Every Employee Has a Role in DEI. The Company's DEI commitment is not the responsibility of HR or of senior leadership alone. It is a collective organizational responsibility in which every employee participates. Every employee contributes to the inclusion of their colleagues through the way they communicate, the way they engage in meetings, the way they make decisions, the way they form working relationships, and the way they respond when they observe exclusionary or discriminatory behavior. DEI is lived in every interaction, not just declared in policy documents.

2.22 Workplace Violence Prevention
2.22.1 Zero Tolerance for Workplace Violence
— Absolute Prohibition. The Company maintains an absolute zero-tolerance position on workplace violence in all its forms. Workplace violence is defined as any act or threat of physical violence, harassment, intimidation, or other threatening or disruptive behavior that occurs at or in connection with the workplace. This includes physical assault or battery, threatening gestures or language, intimidation designed to cause fear, property destruction carried out as an act of aggression, and any other conduct that creates a reasonable fear for personal safety.
— Applies in All Settings. The prohibition on workplace violence applies on Company premises, at client sites and external locations, during business travel, at Company-organized events, and in any other setting where the conduct is connected to the employment relationship. It applies to conduct between employees, between employees and clients, between employees and vendors, and between employees and any other individual encountered in a professional context.
— Immediate Response. Any act or credible threat of workplace violence will trigger an immediate response from management, HR, and where appropriate, security or law enforcement. The Company will not investigate threats of violence through the standard conduct process alone where there is an immediate safety risk. The safety of all individuals in the workplace is the primary and overriding concern, and the Company will take all necessary steps to ensure that safety is protected.

2.23 Personal Relationships in the Workplace
2.23.1 Managing Personal Relationships Professionally
— Personal Relationships Are a Recognized Reality. The Company recognizes that personal relationships between colleagues — whether friendships, romantic relationships, or family connections — are a natural consequence of the time people spend together in a professional environment. The Company does not prohibit such relationships in principle, but it does require that they be managed in a manner that preserves professional boundaries, avoids conflicts of interest, and does not compromise the fairness or integrity of any employment decision.
— Mandatory Disclosure of Romantic Relationships. Where a romantic or intimate relationship develops between two employees who are in a direct reporting relationship — that is, where one employee manages, evaluates, compensates, or makes employment decisions about the other — both employees are required to disclose the relationship to HR promptly. Failure to disclose such a relationship is a conduct violation because it creates an undisclosed conflict of interest that may taint employment decisions and expose the Company to legal risk. Upon disclosure, HR will work with the employees and relevant management to arrange an appropriate structural adjustment — typically a change in reporting line — that removes the direct reporting relationship.
— Conduct Standards Apply Regardless of Personal Relationships. The existence of a personal relationship between two employees does not modify or relax the conduct standards that apply between them. Employees in a personal relationship must conduct themselves in all professional interactions with the same professionalism, respect, and boundary-consciousness that would be expected of any other two colleagues. Public displays of affection, preferential treatment based on personal relationships, and the use of professional channels for personal relationship communications are all inappropriate in a professional environment.

2.24 Conduct in Remote and Hybrid Work Environments
2.24.1 Same Standards, Different Setting
— Remote Work Does Not Relax Conduct Standards. The decision to approve a remote or hybrid working arrangement for an employee reflects trust in that employee's professional discipline and self-management capability. It is not, under any interpretation, a relaxation of conduct standards. Every provision of this Code of Conduct applies to remote and hybrid employees with exactly the same force and effect as to employees working on Company premises. The location from which an employee works does not modify the ethical and professional standards they are required to uphold.
— Professional Conduct in Digital Interactions. The majority of a remote employee's professional interactions occur through digital channels — email, video conferencing, messaging platforms, and collaboration tools. Employees must conduct all such interactions with the same professionalism, respect, and integrity that would be expected in a face-to-face setting. The informal nature of messaging platforms or the relative anonymity of digital communication does not permit a reduction in the standard of professional conduct expected.
— Video Conference Conduct Standards. When participating in video conferences, employees are expected to maintain the same professional standards as in an in-person meeting. This means attending from an appropriate, private, and professional setting, dressing and presenting professionally, maintaining an engaged and attentive posture, muting when not speaking to minimize background noise, ensuring that no confidential Company information is visible in the background or overheard by unauthorized individuals, and participating actively and constructively in the discussion.
2.24.2 Security and Confidentiality in Remote Settings
Point 1 — Heightened Security Responsibility. Remote employees work in environments that present greater security risks than controlled Company premises. Employees must take active steps to mitigate these risks, including using only authorized and secure devices and networks for Company work, never working with confidential information in public locations where it may be observed, ensuring that family members and other household occupants do not have access to Company systems or documents, and reporting any security concern or data breach incident immediately to IT and HR.

2.25 Conduct at Company Events and Social Functions
2.25.1 Company Events Are an Extension of the Workplace
— Conduct Standards Apply at Company Events. Company events and social functions — including team celebrations, client entertainment, conferences, awards ceremonies, holiday gatherings, and any other event organized by or on behalf of the Company — are an extension of the professional workplace. The fact that an event occurs outside normal working hours, in a social setting, or involves the consumption of alcohol does not suspend the professional conduct standards established in this Code. Every provision of this policy applies at Company events with the same force as in any other professional setting.
— Respectful and Inclusive Conduct Is Required. Employees at Company events must conduct themselves with the same respect, professionalism, and inclusivity expected in the workplace. This means ensuring that all interactions with colleagues, clients, and guests are considerate and appropriate, avoiding conduct that demeans, excludes, or makes others uncomfortable, being mindful of the impact of humor and informal banter in a diverse social setting, and never using the relaxed atmosphere of a social event as license for conduct that would be unacceptable in the office.
— Responsible Alcohol Consumption. Where alcohol is available at a Company event, employees are expected to consume it responsibly and in a manner consistent with their professional obligations. Excessive intoxication, conduct driven by alcohol consumption, aggressive or harassing behavior while intoxicated, and particularly driving under the influence following a Company event will all be treated as serious conduct violations. The availability of alcohol at a Company event does not reduce the employee's personal responsibility for their conduct.

2.26 Dress Code and Professional Appearance Standards
2.26.1 The Purpose of Professional Appearance Standards
— Professional Appearance Reflects Professional Standards. The Company's professional appearance standards exist because the way employees present themselves sends a powerful and immediate signal about their professionalism and about the standards of the organization they represent. This is particularly important in client-facing roles, where the first impression created by an employee's appearance can materially influence the client's confidence in the Company's professionalism and capabilities.
— Smart Business Professional Standard. The standard expected in the Company's client-facing and office environments is smart business professional — clothing that is clean, well-maintained, appropriately fitted, and projecting a polished and professional image. What constitutes smart business professional will vary by culture, geography, and role, and the Company respects these variations. However, clothing that is visibly dirty, damaged, extremely casual, or inappropriate in a professional context is not consistent with the Company's standard regardless of local variation.
— Context-Specific Guidance. Different roles and contexts within the organization may have specific dress code requirements that vary from the general standard. Roles involving physical work, specialist environments, or specific client expectations may have additional requirements — such as the wearing of safety equipment, uniforms, or specific protective clothing. Any context-specific dress code requirements will be communicated to employees in those roles by their manager or HR, and compliance with those requirements is mandatory.
2.26.2 Inclusive Approach to Appearance Standards
— Religious and Cultural Dress. The Company fully respects and accommodates the right of employees to wear religious or cultural dress — including head coverings, religious symbols, traditional garments, and any other dress that reflects genuine religious or cultural practice — subject only to the legitimate requirements of workplace safety and the maintenance of a professional standard. No employee will be disadvantaged or treated unfavorably because of their religious or cultural dress, and any concern about the interaction between religious dress and workplace requirements should be raised with HR so that an appropriate accommodation can be identified and documented.

2.27 Progressive Disciplinary Framework for Code of Conduct Violations
2.27.1 Philosophy of Progressive Discipline
— Fair, Graduated, and Documented Response. The Company's approach to disciplinary action for Code of Conduct violations is a graduated sequence of responses calibrated to the nature and severity of the violation, that begins with communication and feedback for minor violations and escalates to termination and potential legal referral for the most serious misconduct. The progressive disciplinary process is not intended to be punitive or designed to rush toward termination. Its purpose is to create a fair, structured, documented process that gives the employee every reasonable opportunity to understand and correct their conduct while protecting the Company's legitimate operational and ethical interests.
— Natural Justice at Every Stage. Every step of the disciplinary process will be conducted in accordance with the principles of natural justice. The employee will be informed of the specific concern in clear, factual, and unambiguous terms. The employee will be given a full and genuine opportunity to present their perspective, to explain any contributing circumstances, and to respond to the concern before any formal outcome is determined. All decisions will be documented formally, explained to the employee, and retained in the employee's personnel file.
2.27.2 Categories of Conduct Violations
— Minor Conduct Violations. Minor conduct violations are instances of conduct that fall below the Company's expected standard but do not involve deliberate dishonesty, harm to others, or serious breach of trust. Examples include occasional failures to meet professional communication standards, isolated instances of unprofessional conduct in a meeting, failure to complete a mandatory training module within the required timeframe, or a one-time breach of the dress code. Minor violations are typically addressed through an informal verbal feedback conversation or, where a pattern is emerging, a formal verbal warning.
— Moderate Conduct Violations. Moderate conduct violations are more serious breaches that involve repeated minor violations despite prior feedback, deliberate breach of a specific policy provision, conduct that has a negative impact on colleagues or the work environment, or a first-time instance of conduct that, while not severe, represents a meaningful departure from the Company's standards. Moderate violations are addressed through a formal written warning with a specified improvement period and monitoring.
— Serious Conduct Violations. Serious conduct violations are significant breaches of the Code of Conduct that involve deliberate misconduct, harm to colleagues or clients, breach of trust, or conduct that has or could have material consequences for the Company. Examples include sustained bullying or harassment, a significant conflict of interest that was not disclosed, misuse of Company assets for personal gain, or a serious breach of confidentiality. Serious violations are addressed through a final written warning or, depending on the severity, through immediate consideration of termination.
— Gross Misconduct. Gross misconduct is conduct of such seriousness that it fundamentally destroys the trust and confidence on which the employment relationship depends and justifies immediate termination of employment without notice and without the preceding stages of the progressive disciplinary process. Examples of gross misconduct include theft or fraud of any amount, physical assault or a serious threat of violence, sexual harassment, deliberate falsification of Company records, disclosure of confidential information to a competitor, and any other conduct that a reasonable person would recognize as fundamentally incompatible with continued employment.
2.27.3 Disciplinary Investigation Process
— Investigation Before Determination. No disciplinary outcome — including any formal warning or termination — will be determined without a prior formal investigation. The investigation will be conducted by HR, with the involvement of the relevant manager and, where appropriate, senior leadership or an independent external party. The investigation will involve a review of all available evidence, interviews with the relevant parties and witnesses, and a balanced and impartial assessment of the facts before any conclusion is reached.
— Suspension During Investigation. Where the nature of the alleged violation is such that the employee's continued presence in the workplace during the investigation would create a risk — whether of evidence tampering, continued harm to colleagues, or disruption to the investigation itself — the Company may suspend the employee on full pay for the duration of the investigation. Suspension is a neutral act, not a disciplinary measure or a presumption of guilt, and will be communicated to the employee as such.

2.28 Employee Rights Under the Code of Conduct
2.28.1 The Right to Fair Treatment
— Right to Be Informed. Every employee subject to a formal conduct concern has the right to be clearly and fully informed in writing of the specific nature of the concern being investigated, including the particular conduct or behavior at issue, the specific provisions of this Code that are alleged to have been breached, and the potential consequences if the concern is substantiated. This information must be provided before any disciplinary meeting so that the employee has adequate time and information to prepare their response.
— Right to Be Heard. Every employee subject to a formal conduct concern has the right to a fair and genuine hearing at which they can present their perspective, explain the context of their conduct, identify witnesses or evidence they believe is relevant, and respond fully to the concern before any outcome is determined. The Company will approach every such hearing with an open mind and a genuine willingness to consider the employee's response before reaching any conclusion.
— Right to Representation. At every formal stage of the disciplinary process, the employee has the right to be accompanied by a colleague of their choice, a trade union representative where the Company recognizes such representation, or another appropriate representative as permitted by applicable law. This right will be communicated clearly to the employee at the outset of any formal disciplinary meeting and will be respected without question.
2.28.2 The Right to Appeal
— Every Formal Disciplinary Decision Is Appealable. An employee who disagrees with any formal disciplinary decision made under this policy — including the issuance of a written warning, a final written warning, or a decision to terminate employment — has the right to formally appeal the decision. The appeal must be submitted in writing to the Head of Human Resources within five working days of the disciplinary decision being communicated to the employee.
— Independent Review. The appeal will be reviewed by an HR representative who was not involved in the original disciplinary decision, and where appropriate by a senior business leader who was not involved in the original decision. The appeal is a genuine mechanism for ensuring fairness, not a procedural formality. The Company will approach every appeal with an open mind and a genuine commitment to identifying whether the original decision was fair, proportionate, and procedurally sound. The outcome of the appeal will be communicated to the employee in writing within ten working days of the appeal being received.

2.29 Training, Awareness, and Code of Conduct Education
2.29.1 Mandatory Training Requirements
- All Employees Must Complete Code of Conduct Training. All employees are required to complete the Company's mandatory Code of Conduct training program within 30 days of joining the Company and to complete a refresher program annually thereafter. This training will cover the key provisions of this policy, the Company's core values, the specific conduct expectations and prohibitions, the reporting mechanisms available, and the consequences of violations. Completion of this training is not optional and will be monitored by HR through the HRMS. Failure to complete mandatory training within the specified timeframe will be flagged as a compliance concern.
— Specific Training for Higher-Risk Roles and Responsibilities. Certain roles and responsibilities carry a heightened conduct risk by virtue of their nature — roles with significant financial authority, client-facing roles, management and leadership roles, roles with access to particularly sensitive information, and roles subject to specific regulatory requirements. Employees in such roles will receive additional targeted training that addresses the specific conduct risks and expectations relevant to their function, in addition to the general Code of Conduct training required of all employees.
— POSH and Anti-Harassment Training. All employees will receive dedicated training on the prevention of harassment and sexual harassment as described in Sections 2.6 and 2.7, including the Company's POSH Act compliance training. This training will be provided on joining and refreshed annually, and will be supplemented by additional training for managers on their specific obligations and responsibilities under the POSH Act and this policy.
2.29.2 Creating a Culture of Ethical Awareness
— Ethics is a Daily Practice, Not an Annual Checkbox. The Company recognizes that annual training, however well-designed, is insufficient by itself to build and sustain a genuinely ethical organizational culture. Ethics must be a daily practice — lived in every decision, every interaction, and every leadership behavior. The Company will therefore supplement mandatory training with regular ethics communications, leadership modeling of the values in this policy, recognition of ethical conduct in performance frameworks, and the creation of an organizational environment in which speaking up about ethical concerns is genuinely welcomed and rewarded rather than merely tolerated.

2.30 Policy Compliance, Governance, and Review
2.30.1 Responsibility for Policy Compliance
— Every Employee's Personal Responsibility. Compliance with this Code of Conduct is the personal and non-delegable responsibility of every employee without exception. Employees are responsible for reading and understanding this policy in full, for conducting themselves at all times in accordance with its provisions, for seeking clarification from HR when facing conduct situations they are uncertain about, and for reporting conduct concerns through the appropriate channels when they arise.
— Manager and Leadership Responsibility. Managers and leaders bear a heightened and additional responsibility for the culture of conduct within their teams. They are expected to model the values and standards of this policy in their own conduct at all times, to address conduct concerns within their teams promptly and through the appropriate channels, to create an environment in which team members feel safe to raise concerns without fear of retaliation, and to cooperate fully with any conduct investigation involving members of their team. A manager who tolerates, condones, or participates in misconduct is failing their most fundamental leadership obligation and will be held fully accountable.
— HR Responsibility. HR is responsible for administering this policy, for ensuring it is clearly communicated and accessible to all employees, for investigating conduct concerns promptly and impartially, for issuing formal disciplinary communications at the appropriate stages of the progressive process, for maintaining the integrity and confidentiality of all conduct records, and for ensuring that the policy is applied consistently and without bias across all employees and all levels of the organization.
2.30.2 Employee Acknowledgment
— Formal Acknowledgment is Mandatory. All employees are required to formally acknowledge that they have read, understood, and agree to comply with this Code of Conduct. Acknowledgment will be captured through the HRMS at the time of onboarding for all new employees and upon each substantive update to the policy for all existing employees. The acknowledgment record will be maintained in the employee's personnel file. Failure to complete the acknowledgment process within the timeline specified by HR will be treated as a compliance concern and addressed accordingly.
2.30.3 Annual Policy Review
— Regular Review to Maintain Relevance and Currency. This Code of Conduct will be formally reviewed on an annual basis by HR, or earlier if required by a material change in applicable legislation or regulation, a significant organizational development, the identification of a systematic conduct concern that reveals a gap in the current policy, or feedback from employees, managers, or audit processes that reveals an area for improvement. All updates will be approved by the Chief Human Resources Officer, communicated to all employees through the HRMS and official Company communication channels, and formally acknowledged by all employees within 15 working days of communication. The Company's commitment to maintaining a current, relevant, and comprehensive Code of Conduct is itself an expression of its values and its seriousness about ethical organizational life.
