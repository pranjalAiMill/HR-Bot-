7. INFORMATION SECURITY POLICY

7.1 Purpose and Organizational Philosophy
7.1.1 Why This Policy Exists
— The Foundation of Information Security. The Information Security Policy is one of the most operationally critical documents in the Company's entire policy framework. It establishes the principles, standards, and mandatory behaviors through which the Company protects its information assets — including data, systems, networks, and intellectual property — from unauthorized access, misuse, loss, corruption, disclosure, and destruction. Where the Data Security Policy referenced as HR-DSP-001 governs how the Company handles data specifically, this policy governs the broader information security framework that encompasses all aspects of protecting information in all its forms — digital, physical, and intellectual. Information is not merely a byproduct of business operations. It is a core strategic asset, and the security of that asset is a fundamental organizational responsibility that extends beyond data protection to encompass the entire information ecosystem.
— The Organizational Imperative. The Company operates in an environment where information security threats are simultaneously increasing in sophistication and frequency. External cyber attackers deploy increasingly advanced techniques including advanced persistent threats, zero-day exploits, and sophisticated social engineering campaigns. Internal risks arise from accidental mishandling by well-meaning employees who may be unaware of the security implications of their actions, from deliberate insider misconduct by individuals who abuse their access, and from system vulnerabilities that emerge as technology evolves. A single security incident — whether caused by a successful phishing attack, a misconfigured system, a lost device, or a deliberate act of sabotage — can cause irreparable harm to the Company's operations, expose the Company to substantial regulatory penalties, destroy client relationships, and inflict reputational damage that organizations rarely fully recover from. This policy exists to prevent those outcomes by establishing clear, unambiguous, and enforceable standards that every employee is required to understand and comply with in every aspect of their work.
— The Human Imperative. This policy has been written with a genuine recognition that information security is not a subject that comes naturally to everyone and that the overwhelming majority of employees who inadvertently create a security risk do so not out of carelessness or indifference but out of a genuine lack of awareness of what security-conscious behavior looks like in practice. This policy is not a declaration of distrust in the Company's people. It is a tool of empowerment — a clear, practical, and comprehensive guide that gives every employee the knowledge they need to recognize security risks, to respond correctly when threats are encountered, and to contribute actively to the organization's security posture. The Company invests in this policy, and in the training and awareness programs that support it, because it genuinely believes that an informed and security-aware workforce is the most powerful defense the organization has against the threats it faces.
— The Philosophy of Shared Responsibility. Information security is not the exclusive responsibility of the IT department, the Information Security function, or the leadership team. It is the shared and personal responsibility of every single individual who handles Company information, accesses Company systems, or interacts with Company technology in any capacity. A security policy that lives only in the IT department is not a security policy — it is a technical program with a policy-shaped wrapper. This policy is built on the conviction that genuine information security requires every employee — regardless of their role, their seniority, their technical background, or their geographic location — to understand, internalize, and apply the standards described here in every interaction with information and technology that they have in the course of their work. Security is a daily practice, not an annual checkbox, and this policy is the foundation on which that practice is built.
— A Living and Evolving Document. Like all enterprise policies, the Information Security Policy is a living document that will be reviewed, updated, and strengthened as the threat landscape evolves, as the Company's technology environment changes, as applicable cybersecurity legislation develops, and as practical experience provides insights into how the policy can be made more effective. Every employee is expected to engage with this policy seriously, to ask questions where clarification is needed, and to treat it as a genuine and relevant guide to one of the most important responsibilities they carry in their professional role.

7.2 Scope and Applicability
7.2.1 Who This Policy Applies To
— Universal Application Without Exception. This policy applies universally and without exception to every individual engaged with the Company in any employment or engagement capacity who handles, accesses, processes, stores, transmits, or interacts with Company information, systems, networks, or technology in any form. There are no exemptions based on seniority, tenure, professional standing, department, business unit, geographic location, or technical background. The most senior executive in the organization is subject to exactly the same information security obligations as the most junior team member, and any perception that seniority or technical expertise grants an individual the right to operate outside the standards established in this policy will be firmly and consistently rejected at every level of the organization.
— All Categories of Engagement Covered. This policy covers permanent full-time employees, part-time employees, employees on fixed-term or rolling contracts, interns and trainees, external consultants and contractors working on Company premises or accessing Company systems, temporary or agency staff, and any other individual who handles Company information or accesses Company systems in any capacity. Each of these individuals, regardless of the nature or duration of their engagement, is required to comply with the full standards established in this policy from the first day of their engagement. The onboarding process will include an information security briefing and acknowledgment for all new engagements regardless of duration or type.
— Seniority Confers Heightened Responsibility, Not Exemption. Senior leaders, managers, and those with elevated system access privileges are not exempt from this policy — they are held to a higher standard because of the greater volume and sensitivity of information to which they have access, the greater potential impact of a security failure at their level, and the powerful signal that their conduct sends to the rest of the organization. A leader who bypasses security controls — even for apparently legitimate operational reasons — communicates to their team that the controls are optional, which is a message the Company will not tolerate being sent at any level.
7.2.2 Where This Policy Applies
— All Physical and Digital Environments. This policy applies in every environment in which Company information is accessed, processed, stored, or transmitted — whether on Company premises, at client sites, at home in a remote or hybrid arrangement, in a hotel or airport while traveling on Company business, or in any other location where an employee has occasion to interact with Company information or systems. The obligation to handle information securely does not diminish based on the employee's physical location. An employee working from a hotel room with sensitive information on their screen has exactly the same security obligations as an employee sitting at a desk in the Company's headquarters.
— All Information Environments and Systems. This policy applies to all systems, platforms, networks, and environments through which Company information is handled — including Company-issued laptops, mobile phones, and tablets, Company servers and cloud-based storage platforms, email and communication systems, third-party software and applications approved for Company use, personal devices used for Company work under the BYOD framework described in Section 4.8, network infrastructure, security systems, and any other technical environment through which an employee accesses, processes, or transmits information.
7.2.3 Relationship With Other Policies
— An Integrated Security Framework. This Information Security Policy operates as one component of the Company's broader security and governance framework. It must be read and applied in conjunction with the Data Security Policy referenced as HR-DSP-001, the Code of Conduct referenced as HR-COC-001, the Data Protection and Privacy Policy referenced as HR-DPP-001, the Work From Home Policy referenced as HR-WFH-001, and all other applicable Company policies. Where a specific policy provides more detailed guidance on a particular aspect of security — such as the Data Security Policy's treatment of data classification and handling — that specific policy governs. Where a security concern arises that falls outside the scope of any specific policy, this Information Security Policy provides the governing framework.
— Conflict Resolution. In the event of any apparent conflict between the standards established in this policy and those established in any other Company policy, employees must seek guidance from the IT Security function or HR before taking action. Employees must not act unilaterally on their own interpretation of any apparent conflict between policies and must not use an apparent conflict as justification for departing from security standards that would otherwise apply.

7.3 Information Security Risk Management Framework
7.3.1 The Purpose of Risk Management
— Why Risk Management Matters. Information security is fundamentally about managing risk — identifying threats to the Company's information assets, assessing the likelihood and impact of those threats, implementing controls to reduce risk to acceptable levels, and continuously monitoring and improving the security posture. The Company's information security risk management framework provides a structured approach to understanding, managing, and mitigating information security risks across the organization. Every employee has a role in risk management — from identifying potential risks in their daily work, to reporting security concerns, to complying with security controls designed to mitigate risk.
— Risk Management as a Continuous Process. Risk management is not a one-time activity that occurs during annual planning. It is a continuous process that includes regular risk assessments, ongoing threat monitoring, continuous improvement of security controls, incident response and recovery, and periodic review of the risk management framework itself. The IT Security function is responsible for administering the formal risk management program, but every employee contributes to risk management through their daily security-conscious behavior and their reporting of security concerns.
7.3.2 Risk Assessment and Treatment
— Regular Risk Assessments. The IT Security function conducts formal information security risk assessments on at least an annual basis, or more frequently when significant changes occur in the threat landscape, the Company's technology environment, or business operations. Risk assessments identify threats to information assets, assess the likelihood and impact of those threats, evaluate the effectiveness of existing controls, and identify gaps that require additional controls or improvements. Risk assessment findings are reported to senior leadership and inform security strategy, resource allocation, and control implementation priorities.
— Risk Treatment Strategies. Identified risks are treated through one or more of the following strategies: risk mitigation through implementation of security controls that reduce the likelihood or impact of threats, risk acceptance where the cost of mitigation exceeds the potential impact and the risk is within acceptable tolerance levels, risk transfer through insurance or contractual arrangements with third parties, or risk avoidance through elimination of activities or systems that create unacceptable risk. The choice of risk treatment strategy is based on risk assessment findings, business needs, cost-benefit analysis, and regulatory requirements.
7.3.3 Threat Intelligence and Monitoring
— Threat Intelligence Program. The Company maintains a threat intelligence program that monitors external threat sources, tracks emerging threats and attack techniques, analyzes threat actor activities, and provides early warning of potential security risks. Threat intelligence informs security strategy, guides control implementation priorities, and enables proactive defense against known threats. The IT Security function is responsible for maintaining the threat intelligence program and for communicating relevant threat information to employees and managers as appropriate.
— Continuous Security Monitoring. The Company maintains continuous monitoring of its information systems, networks, and security controls to detect security events, identify anomalies, and respond to threats in real-time. Security monitoring includes automated detection systems, security event logging and analysis, network traffic monitoring, endpoint detection and response, and other monitoring capabilities appropriate to the Company's risk profile and technology environment. Security monitoring is conducted by the IT Security function, and monitoring capabilities are continuously improved based on threat intelligence and incident experience.

7.4 Security Incident Management
7.4.1 What Constitutes a Security Incident
— A Broad and Inclusive Definition. A security incident, for the purposes of this policy, is any event that compromises or threatens to compromise the confidentiality, integrity, or availability of Company information, systems, or networks. This definition is intentionally broad because security incidents can take many forms and can have varying levels of impact. Examples of security incidents include confirmed or suspected unauthorized access to systems or data, malware infections including ransomware, spyware, or other malicious software, phishing attacks or successful social engineering attempts, denial of service attacks or other network disruptions, loss or theft of devices containing Company information, misdirected emails or other communications containing sensitive information, system misconfigurations that create security vulnerabilities, insider threats including deliberate or accidental misuse of access, and any other event that creates a security risk or violates security policies. This list is illustrative and not exhaustive. Where an employee is uncertain whether an event constitutes a security incident, they must report it to the IT Security function immediately and allow IT to make the assessment.
7.4.2 Incident Severity Classification
— Critical Severity Incidents. Critical severity incidents are those that pose an immediate and severe threat to the Company's information security, operations, or reputation. Examples include confirmed data breaches involving sensitive information, active ransomware attacks that encrypt Company systems or data, full system compromises that grant attackers persistent access, denial of service attacks that disrupt critical business operations, and any incident that requires immediate external notification to regulators, law enforcement, or affected parties. Critical incidents require immediate escalation to the IT Security function, senior leadership, Legal, HR, and external authorities where required by law. Response activities begin immediately, and all available resources are deployed to contain and remediate the incident.
— High Severity Incidents. High severity incidents are those that pose a significant threat but do not require immediate external notification or do not involve confirmed data breaches. Examples include successful phishing attacks that compromise credentials, unauthorized access to systems or data that is detected and contained, significant malware infections that are isolated to specific systems, suspicious activity that indicates potential ongoing attack, and policy violations that create significant security risk. High severity incidents require urgent investigation and response, typically within two hours of detection, and may require escalation to senior leadership depending on the circumstances.
— Medium Severity Incidents. Medium severity incidents are those that pose a moderate threat and require investigation and response but do not require immediate escalation. Examples include suspicious activity that may indicate attempted unauthorized access, unusual login patterns or system behavior, potential policy violations that require investigation, security alerts from monitoring systems that require analysis, and general security concerns that may indicate underlying issues. Medium severity incidents are investigated within twenty-four hours of detection, and response activities are conducted through standard security processes.
— Low Severity Incidents. Low severity incidents are those that pose a minimal threat and can be addressed through standard support processes. Examples include general security queries, minor anomalies that are easily explained, guidance requests, and security awareness questions. Low severity incidents are addressed through standard IT support channels within forty-eight hours and are logged for pattern analysis and awareness purposes.
7.4.3 Incident Response Process
— Immediate Reporting Is the Single Most Important Employee Obligation. The single most important thing any employee can do when they discover or suspect a security incident is to report it to the IT Security function immediately — without delay, without first attempting to fix the problem independently, and without waiting to assess whether the incident is serious enough to warrant reporting. The speed of the Company's response to a security incident is one of the most significant factors in determining the scale of harm it causes — both to the Company's operations and to the Company's legal, regulatory, and reputational position. A delayed report that results in a larger incident than would have occurred with prompt action will be treated as a serious aggravating factor in any subsequent review and will not be mitigated by the employee's assessment that the incident appeared minor at the time.
— The Formal Incident Response Process. Upon receipt of an incident report, the IT Security function immediately activates the incident response process, which includes incident triage and severity assessment, activation of the incident response team appropriate to the severity level, containment activities to prevent further damage or exposure, investigation to determine the scope, cause, and impact of the incident, eradication of threats and remediation of vulnerabilities, recovery of affected systems and data, post-incident review and lessons learned, and documentation and reporting. The incident response process is designed to be rapid, thorough, and effective, and response activities are coordinated to ensure that all aspects of the incident are addressed appropriately.
— Employee Cooperation During Incident Response. Employees are required to cooperate fully with incident response activities, including providing information about what they observed, preserving evidence such as emails or system logs, allowing access to devices or accounts for investigation purposes, following instructions from the incident response team, and maintaining confidentiality about the incident until authorized communications are released. Employees who interfere with incident response activities, who attempt to cover up security incidents, or who fail to cooperate with investigations will face serious disciplinary consequences.

7.5 Access Control and Identity Management
7.5.1 The Principle of Least Privilege
— Access Is Granted Only Where It Is Needed. The Company's access control framework is built on the principle of least privilege — the principle that every employee is granted access only to the systems, data, and functions that they genuinely need in order to perform their specific job responsibilities, and no more. The principle of least privilege is not an expression of distrust. It is a fundamental security design principle that limits the potential impact of any individual account being compromised — whether through external attack, internal misconduct, or accidental misuse. An employee who has access only to the information and systems they need to do their job can cause significantly less unintended harm than an employee whose access is unrestricted.
— Access Rights Must Reflect Role, Not Convenience. Access to systems and information must reflect the employee's actual job responsibilities at the time the access is granted. It must not be granted on the basis of what might be useful someday, what the employee's previous role required, what a colleague in a similar role has access to, or any other basis that does not reflect a specific, current, documented operational need. Employees who believe they need access to a system or dataset that is not currently provisioned to their account must submit a formal access request through the IT Helpdesk and must not attempt to access systems or information for which they have not been authorized.
7.5.2 Access Provisioning, Review, and Deprovisioning
— Provisioning Through a Formal Process. All access to Company systems and information must be provisioned through the formal access request process managed by IT. Access requests must be authorized by the employee's line manager and, for sensitive systems or information, by the relevant data owner and the IT Security function. Access will be granted only after the request has been reviewed, the justification validated, and the appropriate approval obtained. IT will provision access within the agreed service level timeframe following receipt of a fully authorized request and will maintain a complete and auditable record of all access grants.
— Regular Access Reviews Are Mandatory. The IT Security function will conduct formal access reviews for all Company systems on at least a quarterly basis, and for sensitive systems on a monthly basis. During each access review, the access rights of every user will be validated against their current role and responsibilities. Access that is no longer required — because the employee's role has changed, because a project has ended, or because a system is no longer used — will be revoked promptly and without exception. Line managers are responsible for notifying IT of any change in a team member's role that may affect their access requirements, and must do so within one business day of the change taking effect.
— Immediate Deprovisioning Upon Departure. When an employee leaves the Company for any reason — whether through resignation, redundancy, termination, or the natural conclusion of a fixed-term engagement — all of that employee's access to Company systems, information, and facilities must be revoked on the employee's last day of employment, or earlier where circumstances require it. HR is responsible for notifying IT of all departing employees at the earliest possible stage of the offboarding process to enable timely deprovisioning. Any delay in deprovisioning access that results in a former employee retaining system access beyond their last working day will be treated as a serious security failure and investigated accordingly.
7.5.3 Identity and Authentication Management
— Strong Authentication Requirements. All Company systems require strong authentication mechanisms that verify the identity of users before granting access. Authentication requirements include strong, unique passwords that comply with the password standards described in Section 4.6, multi-factor authentication for all systems where MFA functionality is available, and secure password reset and account recovery processes that prevent unauthorized access. The Company does not use weak authentication mechanisms such as default passwords, shared accounts, or authentication methods that can be easily compromised.
— Identity Management Systems. The Company maintains centralized identity management systems that provision, manage, and deprovision user accounts across all Company systems. Identity management systems ensure that access is granted and revoked consistently, that user accounts are properly configured, and that authentication mechanisms are applied uniformly. Employees are assigned a single identity that is used across all Company systems, and identity management systems ensure that access rights are synchronized and consistent.

7.6 Password and Credential Security
7.6.1 Password Standards and Requirements
— Strong Passwords Are a Non-Negotiable Minimum. Every Company account must be protected by a strong, unique password that complies with the minimum standards established by the IT Security function. At the time of publication of this policy, the minimum password standards require a minimum length of twelve characters, including a combination of uppercase letters, lowercase letters, numbers, and special characters, and prohibit the use of passwords that are based on personal information such as names, dates of birth, or common words that appear in any language dictionary. These minimum standards will be reviewed and updated by the IT Security function at least annually and may be strengthened as security requirements evolve. The current minimum standards are published on the Company intranet and in the IT Security guidance library.
— Passwords Must Be Unique Across Accounts. Employees must use a unique password for each Company account and must never reuse a password that is used for a personal account on any Company system, or a password that is used on one Company system on any other Company system. Password reuse is one of the most common and most preventable causes of credential compromise. The Company provides access to an approved enterprise password manager to enable employees to maintain strong, unique passwords across all their accounts without the practical burden of memorizing them all. Employees are strongly encouraged to use the approved password manager for all Company accounts and must not record passwords in insecure locations such as notebooks, spreadsheets, sticky notes, or personal email drafts.
— Credentials Must Never Be Shared. Every Company account is assigned to a specific individual and is that individual's personal responsibility. Employees must never share their credentials with any other person — including their line manager, an IT support agent, or a colleague covering their responsibilities during an absence. Legitimate IT support requests will never require an employee to disclose their password. Any request for an employee's password — regardless of the apparent source or justification — must be treated as a suspected phishing or social engineering attempt and reported to the IT Security function immediately in accordance with Section 4.9.
7.6.2 Multi-Factor Authentication
— MFA Is Mandatory for All Applicable Systems. Multi-factor authentication — the requirement to verify identity through a second factor in addition to a password — is mandatory for all Company systems and applications for which MFA functionality is available. This includes email, the HRMS, financial systems, cloud storage platforms, the VPN, and any other system designated by IT as requiring MFA. Employees must not attempt to circumvent, disable, or bypass MFA requirements on any system, and must not use account recovery options to access systems in a way that bypasses MFA controls. Where an employee experiences a technical issue with their MFA configuration, they must contact the IT Helpdesk through the standard support channel and must not attempt to resolve the issue in a way that compromises the security control.
— MFA Devices and Codes Must Be Protected. The second factor in an MFA process — whether delivered through an authenticator application, a hardware token, or an SMS code — is a security credential that must be protected with the same care as a password. Employees must never share their MFA codes with any other person under any circumstances, must keep their MFA device — whether a personal phone, a hardware token, or any other device — physically secure at all times, and must report the loss or suspected compromise of an MFA device to the IT Security function immediately.

7.7 Device and Endpoint Security
7.7.1 Company-Issued Device Security
— Company Devices Are Security-Controlled Assets. Laptops, mobile phones, tablets, and any other devices issued to employees by the Company are configured by IT with security controls designed to protect the information stored on and accessed through those devices. These controls include device encryption, endpoint security software including antivirus and anti-malware protection, automatic screen lock, remote wipe capability, centrally managed software update policies, and device management systems that enforce security policies. Employees must not attempt to disable, bypass, or modify any of these security controls. Any modification of the security configuration of a Company device — regardless of the apparent operational justification — is a serious security violation that will be addressed under the disciplinary framework.
— Care and Physical Security of Company Devices. Every employee entrusted with a Company device is personally responsible for its physical security. This means never leaving a device unattended and unlocked in any environment, never leaving a device visible and unattended in a vehicle, never checking a device containing Company information as hold luggage during air travel, never lending a device to a family member, friend, or colleague who has not been authorized by IT to use it, and reporting any loss, theft, or damage of a Company device to the IT Security function immediately — regardless of the time of day or day of the week. Prompt reporting of a lost or stolen device enables IT to remotely wipe the device before any unauthorized party is able to access the information it contains. A delayed report that results in a security incident will be treated as a serious aggravating factor in any subsequent disciplinary process.
7.7.2 Personal Device Usage — BYOD
— Personal Devices May Only Be Used Within the BYOD Framework. The Company recognizes that employees may from time to time need to access Company information or systems from a personal device. This is permitted only within the Company's formal Bring Your Own Device framework, which is administered by IT and requires the device to be registered, the installation of approved security software, and compliance with all applicable security standards. Employees who use a personal device for Company work outside the BYOD framework — including accessing Company email through a personal device that has not been registered and secured — are creating an uncontrolled security exposure that the Company cannot monitor, manage, or remediate, and this constitutes a policy violation.
— BYOD Devices Are Subject to Security Standards. Personal devices registered under the BYOD framework are subject to the same security standards as Company-issued devices in relation to the Company information they access and store. This includes the requirement to maintain device encryption, to operate a current and updated operating system, to use a screen lock with a strong PIN or biometric authentication, to install approved security software, and to report the loss or theft of the device to IT immediately. Employees must be aware that registering a personal device under the BYOD framework grants IT the ability to remotely wipe Company information from the device in the event of loss, theft, or compromise. This capability applies only to Company information stored on the device and does not extend to personal data stored separately.

7.8 Network and Communication Security
7.8.1 Secure Network Usage
— The Company VPN Is Required for Remote Access. When accessing Company systems, applications, or information from a location outside of Company premises, employees must connect through the Company's approved Virtual Private Network. The VPN creates an encrypted tunnel between the employee's device and the Company's network infrastructure, protecting the information transmitted through it from interception by unauthorized parties. Employees must not access Company systems or information from a remote location without first connecting to the VPN, and must not bypass or disable the VPN connection while working with Company information from a remote location.
— Public and Unsecured Wi-Fi Networks Are Prohibited for Company Work. Employees must not access Company systems or information through public or unsecured Wi-Fi networks — including networks in coffee shops, hotels, airports, and other public locations — without connecting through the Company VPN. Public Wi-Fi networks are inherently insecure and expose all information transmitted through them to potential interception by third parties. Where an employee needs to access Company information in a public location and does not have access to a secure private Wi-Fi connection, they must use a mobile data connection or a personal mobile hotspot in conjunction with the Company VPN rather than connecting through the public network. This restriction applies regardless of the perceived sensitivity of the information being accessed.
— Network Segmentation and Access Controls. The Company maintains network segmentation that separates different types of systems and information based on security requirements. Network access controls restrict access to sensitive systems and information to authorized users and devices only. Employees must not attempt to bypass network access controls, must not connect unauthorized devices to Company networks, and must comply with all network security requirements.
7.8.2 Email and Electronic Communication Security
— Email Is a High-Risk Channel for Sensitive Information. Email is one of the most commonly used communication channels in any organization and one of the most significant vectors for both outbound information loss and inbound cyber threats. Employees must treat all email interactions as potential security risks and must apply the following standards without exception. Confidential or Restricted information must never be transmitted by email to external recipients without the use of approved encryption or secure file transfer mechanisms. Internal email transmissions of Confidential information must use approved internal channels and must not include unnecessary recipients. Employees must verify the identity of email recipients before sending any sensitive information — particularly where a recipient's email address is being entered manually rather than selected from an approved contact list.
— Misdirected Email Is a Common and Serious Information Loss Risk. One of the most frequent causes of security incidents in any organization is the sending of sensitive information to an unintended recipient — caused by autocomplete selecting the wrong email address, by the careless use of reply-all, or by sending a message to the wrong individual or distribution list. Employees must exercise particular care when composing emails containing sensitive information, must double-check all recipient fields before sending, and must use the delay-send functionality where available to create an opportunity to catch errors before transmission. Where an employee discovers that they have sent sensitive information to an unintended recipient, they must report it to the IT Security function and HR immediately as a potential security incident, in accordance with Section 4.4.
— Phishing and Social Engineering Awareness. Email is a primary vector for phishing attacks and social engineering attempts. Employees must be vigilant for signs of phishing emails, including unexpected requests for credentials or information, suspicious sender addresses, unusual language or formatting, urgent or threatening language designed to prompt immediate action, and unexpected attachments or links. Employees who receive a suspicious email must report it to the IT Security function immediately and must not click on links, open attachments, or respond to the email without verification. The Company conducts regular phishing simulation exercises to build employee awareness and resilience.

7.9 Threat Detection and Response
7.9.1 Recognizing Security Threats
— Phishing Is the Most Common Entry Point for Cyber Attacks. Phishing — the use of deceptive emails, messages, websites, or phone calls to trick individuals into disclosing credentials, clicking malicious links, or downloading malware — is the most common and most successful method through which external attackers gain unauthorized access to organizational systems. Phishing attacks have become increasingly sophisticated and are no longer easily identified by obvious spelling errors or implausible scenarios. Modern phishing attacks are often highly targeted, use realistic sender addresses and branding, and create convincing scenarios of urgency or authority designed to override the recipient's natural caution. Every employee must treat unexpected or unusual communications with healthy skepticism, regardless of how legitimate they appear.
— Recognizing the Signs of a Phishing Attempt. Common indicators of a phishing or social engineering attempt include unexpected requests for credentials, personal information, or financial authorization — particularly those that create a sense of urgency or threaten negative consequences for non-compliance, email or message senders whose address, on close inspection, does not match the organization they claim to represent, communications that direct the recipient to a website whose URL does not exactly match the legitimate website of the organization referenced, attachments or links in unexpected communications from known or unknown senders, requests to override normal security or authorization processes for apparently compelling reasons, and unusually personal or detailed knowledge of the recipient's role, colleagues, or circumstances that seems designed to establish false credibility. This list is illustrative and not exhaustive. The most important protection against phishing is a habitual willingness to pause, question, and verify before acting on any communication that requests sensitive action.
— Reporting Suspected Threats Immediately. Any employee who receives a communication that they suspect may be a phishing or social engineering attempt — or who believes they may have inadvertently responded to one — must report it to the IT Security function immediately, without attempting to investigate further or take remedial action independently. Prompt reporting enables IT to assess the threat, take protective action across the Company's systems, and provide guidance to the affected employee on any steps they need to take to mitigate the risk. An employee who reports a suspected phishing attempt in good faith — even if it subsequently turns out to be a legitimate communication — will receive no adverse consequence whatsoever. The Company would infinitely prefer to investigate a false alarm than to learn of a real incident after a preventable delay.
7.9.2 Malware and Ransomware Protection
— Malware Protection Is Mandatory. All Company devices must have approved antivirus and anti-malware software installed and kept up to date. Malware protection software scans files, emails, and system activity for signs of malicious software and blocks or quarantines threats automatically. Employees must not disable malware protection software, must ensure that software updates are applied promptly, and must report any malware detections or suspicious system behavior to the IT Security function immediately.
— Ransomware Is a Critical Threat. Ransomware attacks — in which malicious software encrypts files and systems and demands payment for decryption — are among the most serious security threats facing organizations today. Ransomware protection requires a combination of technical controls including malware protection, email filtering, network segmentation, and backup systems, as well as employee awareness and training. Employees must be particularly vigilant for signs of ransomware, including unexpected file encryption, ransom notes, or system lockouts, and must report any suspected ransomware activity immediately. The Company maintains comprehensive backup systems that enable recovery from ransomware attacks without payment, but prompt detection and response are essential to minimize impact.
7.9.3 Incident Response and Recovery
— Immediate Response to Security Threats. When a security threat is detected, the IT Security function immediately activates incident response procedures appropriate to the severity of the threat. Response activities may include isolating affected systems, blocking malicious network traffic, revoking compromised credentials, deploying additional monitoring, and taking other containment measures. Employees are required to cooperate fully with incident response activities and to follow instructions from the incident response team.
— Recovery and Restoration. After a security incident has been contained and threats have been eradicated, the Company works to restore affected systems and data to normal operation. Recovery activities include restoring data from backups, rebuilding compromised systems, verifying system integrity, and gradually restoring services while monitoring for any residual threats. Recovery timelines depend on the severity and scope of the incident, and the Company maintains recovery objectives that guide recovery activities.

7.10 Software, Application, and System Security
7.10.1 Patch Management and Software Updates
— Security Patches Must Be Applied Without Delay. Software vulnerabilities — flaws in the code of operating systems, applications, and system components — are one of the primary vectors through which external attackers gain unauthorized access to computer systems and the information they contain. Software vendors regularly release patches and updates that address known vulnerabilities, and the speed with which those patches are applied is one of the most important factors in determining whether a vulnerability can be exploited before it is closed. The Company operates a formal patch management program under which security patches for all Company systems are assessed, tested where required, and deployed within defined timeframes based on the severity of the vulnerability they address. Critical security patches are applied within forty-eight hours of release. High-severity patches are applied within seven days. Medium and low-severity patches are applied within the standard monthly maintenance cycle.
— Employee Responsibility for Device Updates. Employees are responsible for ensuring that their Company-issued devices are configured to receive and apply IT-managed software updates without delay. Where a device presents a notification of a pending software update or security patch, the employee must apply the update within the timeframe specified in the notification rather than deferring it indefinitely for convenience. Devices that have not received current security patches present a known and unnecessary vulnerability and will be identified through the IT Security function's device management systems. An employee who repeatedly defers required security updates will be addressed through the escalation process.
7.10.2 Unauthorized Software and Shadow IT
— Unauthorized Software Must Not Be Installed. Employees must not install any software, application, plugin, browser extension, or other tool on a Company device that has not been formally approved by the IT Security function. Unauthorized software installations create multiple security risks — including the introduction of malware hidden within the software, the creation of uncontrolled information flows through the software's network communications, and the introduction of vulnerabilities in the software itself that are not covered by the Company's patch management program. Where an employee has a legitimate operational need for a software tool that is not currently approved, they must submit a request to IT for assessment and must not begin using the tool before approval is granted and the software is provisioned through the approved installation process.
— The Prohibition on Shadow IT. The use of unapproved technology tools, applications, or platforms — whether cloud-based or locally installed — for Company work is a practice known as shadow IT, and it is absolutely prohibited under this policy. Shadow IT creates uncontrolled and unmonitored information flows outside the Company's approved security perimeter, exposes Company information to risks that IT cannot assess or mitigate, and undermines the integrity of the Company's information security controls. Employees who identify a genuine operational need for a tool or platform that is not currently on the approved list must submit a request to IT for assessment and approval. They must not begin using the tool before approval is granted, regardless of how pressing the operational need appears to be.

7.11 Cloud Services and Third-Party Platform Security
7.11.1 Approved Cloud Platforms and Services
— Only Approved Cloud Services May Be Used for Company Information. The use of cloud platforms and services for Company work — including cloud storage, cloud-based collaboration tools, cloud-hosted applications, and any other internet-based service through which Company information is processed or stored — is permitted only where the platform or service has been formally assessed and approved by the IT Security function. A complete list of approved cloud services is maintained by IT and published on the Company intranet. Employees must not use any cloud service that does not appear on the approved list — including popular personal services such as personal Google Drive accounts, personal Dropbox accounts, personal iCloud storage, personal WeTransfer accounts, or any other service that has not been assessed and approved — for any purpose that involves Company information.
— Cloud Security Requirements. Approved cloud services must meet the Company's security requirements, including encryption of data in transit and at rest, strong authentication and access controls, regular security assessments and audits, compliance with applicable data protection law, and contractual commitments to security standards. The IT Security function assesses cloud services against these requirements before approval and conducts periodic reassessments to ensure continued compliance.
7.11.2 Third-Party Vendor Security Assessment
— Approved Vendors Must Meet the Company's Security Standards. The Company's information is only as secure as the vendors and service providers to whom it is entrusted. Every third-party vendor, service provider, or cloud platform that processes Company information on behalf of the Company must be assessed against the Company's security requirements before the relationship commences, and must contractually commit to maintaining those standards for the duration of the relationship. The vendor security assessment process is managed by IT in conjunction with the Procurement and Legal functions. Employees must not engage a new vendor who will process Company information without first ensuring that the vendor assessment process has been completed and that the relevant contractual information processing agreements are in place.

7.12 Remote and Hybrid Work — Information Security
7.12.1 Security Standards for Remote Employees
— Remote Work Does Not Relax Security Standards. The decision to approve a remote or hybrid working arrangement reflects the Company's trust in the employee's professionalism and self-discipline. It is not, under any interpretation, a relaxation of information security standards. Every provision of this policy applies to remote and hybrid employees with exactly the same force and effect as to employees working on Company premises. The physical distance from a Company office does not modify an employee's information security obligations in any respect. Remote employees who handle sensitive information must apply the same controls, the same care, and the same vigilance that would be expected of them in the office — and in some respects more, given the additional risks that home and public working environments present.
— Securing the Remote Working Environment. Employees working from home or other remote locations must take active steps to ensure that their working environment does not create unauthorized exposure of Company information. This includes positioning their screen so that it cannot be viewed by other household members or visitors, using a privacy screen filter where working in shared spaces or public locations, ensuring that sensitive conversations are not conducted where they can be overheard by unauthorized individuals, securing printed Company documents in a locked location when not in active use, and ensuring that household members — including family members — do not have access to Company devices, Company accounts, or Company documents of any sensitivity.
4.12.2 Physical Information Security in Remote Settings
— The Heightened Risk of Physical Exposure in Remote Environments. Remote environments present physical information security risks that are typically well-controlled in a Company office but may be significantly less controlled in a home or public setting. Employees working remotely must be particularly attentive to the physical security of their devices and documents, must ensure that Company devices are not left logged in and unattended in a location accessible to other people, must apply the clean desk and clear screen standards described in Section 4.17 to their remote working environment as far as practicable, and must dispose of physical documents containing Company information through secure means rather than placing them in household recycling or general waste.

7.13 Encryption Standards
7.13.1 Encryption in Transit and at Rest
— Encryption Is the Primary Technical Control for Sensitive Information. Encryption is the process of converting information into a format that can only be read by parties who possess the appropriate decryption key, and it is the primary technical control through which the Company protects sensitive information from unauthorized access — both when the information is in transit across a network and when it is stored at rest within a system or device. The Company applies encryption standards that are consistent with current industry best practice and with the requirements of applicable data protection legislation, and those standards are reviewed and updated by the IT Security function at least annually.
— Encryption in Transit. All Company information classified as Confidential or Restricted that is transmitted across any network — whether internal or external — must be encrypted using approved protocols. This applies to email transmissions using approved encrypted email services or secure file transfer tools, to remote access connections using the Company VPN, to web-based system access using HTTPS-secured connections, and to any other form of electronic information transmission. Employees must not transmit Confidential or Restricted information through channels that do not provide appropriate encryption, and must verify that any external platform used to transmit such information supports the required encryption standards before use.
— Encryption at Rest. All Company devices that store Company information — including Company-issued laptops, mobile phones, tablets, and server infrastructure — must be configured with full disk encryption. This configuration is applied by IT to Company-issued devices as part of the standard provisioning process. Employees must not disable disk encryption on any Company device and must ensure that any personal devices used for Company work under the BYOD framework are also configured with device encryption. Restricted information stored in cloud platforms or on servers must be encrypted at rest using approved encryption configurations, and the IT Security function must verify that the required encryption is active before any Restricted information is stored in a new environment.

7.14 Physical Security of Information and Systems
7.14.1 Physical Access Controls
— Access to Secure Areas Is Restricted and Controlled. The Company's premises contain areas — server rooms, IT infrastructure spaces, HR offices, and other locations where sensitive information is processed or stored — that are designated as secure areas and to which access is restricted to authorized individuals. Employees must not enter a secure area without appropriate authorization, must not allow unauthorized individuals to enter a secure area by holding a door open or providing access without verification, and must report any instance of unauthorized or unaccompanied access to a secure area to the security function immediately. Access to secure areas is controlled through physical access systems — including electronic key fobs, access cards, and biometric controls — and any loss or suspected compromise of an access credential must be reported to the security function immediately.
— Protecting Sensitive Information in Shared and Public Spaces. Employees who work with sensitive information in shared office spaces, meeting rooms, or public locations must take active steps to prevent unauthorized individuals from viewing or accessing that information. This includes positioning their screen away from public sightlines, using a physical privacy screen on their laptop when working in a location where the screen may be visible to others, speaking about sensitive matters in a location where the conversation cannot be overheard by unauthorized individuals, and ensuring that physical documents are not left visible on desks or meeting room tables where they may be seen by passing colleagues or visitors.
7.14.2 Clean Desk and Clear Screen Standards
— Every Employee Is Required to Maintain a Clean Desk. At the end of each working day, and whenever leaving their desk for an extended period, every employee must ensure that their desk is clear of any physical documents, notes, or materials containing Company information that could be accessed or read by unauthorized individuals. This standard — known as the clean desk standard — applies to all employees regardless of the sensitivity of the information they typically work with, because even seemingly innocuous documents can provide useful intelligence to an unauthorized party and because the clean desk habit is more reliably maintained when it is applied consistently rather than selectively based on perceived sensitivity.
— Screens Must Be Locked When Unattended. Every employee must lock their computer screen whenever they step away from their workstation — whether for a brief period such as a visit to another part of the office, or for a longer absence. Screen locking must be performed manually upon leaving the workstation and must also be configured to activate automatically after a short period of inactivity. The maximum auto-lock period permitted under this policy is five minutes. An unlocked and unattended screen is an invitation to unauthorized access and constitutes a physical security violation regardless of whether any unauthorized access subsequently occurs.

7.15 Backup and Business Continuity
7.15.1 Information Backup Standards
— Critical Information Must Be Backed Up Regularly and Reliably. The Company's ability to recover from an information loss event — whether caused by a ransomware attack, a hardware failure, an accidental deletion, or a natural disaster — depends entirely on the quality, currency, and integrity of its information backups. The IT Security function administers a formal backup program under which all critical Company information is backed up at defined intervals, stored in geographically separate and secure locations, and tested for recoverability on a regular basis. The backup schedule, retention periods, and recovery objectives for each category of Company information are defined in the Company's Information Backup Standard, which is published in the IT Security guidance library. Employees who maintain information in personal or locally-stored files outside of approved Company systems are responsible for ensuring that such information is transferred to approved Company systems where it will be covered by the backup program, and must not rely on local device storage as the sole copy of any information that cannot be regenerated.
7.15.2 Business Continuity and Disaster Recovery
— The Company Is Prepared for Disruption. The Company maintains a formal Business Continuity and Disaster Recovery program that defines how the organization will continue to operate — and how it will restore full operational capability — in the event of a significant disruption to its IT systems, information, or physical premises. The IT Security function is responsible for maintaining and testing the disaster recovery plan, including periodic failover tests that validate the Company's ability to restore critical systems and information within defined recovery time objectives. All employees must be familiar with the business continuity provisions that apply to their role and function — including any manual processes, alternative communication channels, or interim working arrangements that would be activated in the event of a major system outage — and must cooperate fully with the IT Security function in any business continuity exercise or real activation event.

7.16 Monitoring, Audit, and Logging
7.16.1 System Monitoring and Activity Logging
— The Company Monitors the Use of Its Systems. The Company reserves the right to monitor the use of its IT systems and to log the activity of all users to the extent permitted by applicable law, for the legitimate purposes of detecting and investigating security incidents, identifying policy violations, maintaining the integrity of the Company's information security controls, and meeting the Company's legal and regulatory obligations. All activity on Company systems — including login events, file access and modification, email transmissions, and system configuration changes — is logged by IT and retained for a period consistent with applicable legal requirements and the Company's Data Retention Schedule. Employees should be aware that their use of Company systems may be monitored and must conduct themselves accordingly at all times. The monitoring of Company systems is not targeted at specific individuals without cause. It is a systematic security control that applies to all users equally.
7.16.2 Security Audit Program
— Regular Security Audits Are Part of the Company's Governance Framework. The IT Security function conducts a formal security audit program that includes internal security assessments of all critical systems and processes on at least an annual basis, external penetration testing of the Company's systems and network perimeter at least once per year, regular automated vulnerability scanning of the Company's IT infrastructure, and periodic review of user access rights, security configurations, and compliance with the standards established in this policy. The findings of security audits are reported to the Head of IT, the Chief Human Resources Officer, and the executive leadership team. Where an audit identifies a gap in the Company's security posture — whether a technical vulnerability, a process weakness, or a compliance failure — a remediation plan will be prepared and executed within defined timeframes, and progress against the plan will be tracked and reported to leadership on a monthly basis until closure.

7.17 Artificial Intelligence and Automated Processing Security
7.17.1 Use of AI Tools and Data Processing Systems
— AI Tools May Only Be Used Within the Approved Framework. The use of artificial intelligence tools — including large language model platforms, automated data analysis tools, AI-powered productivity applications, and any other AI-driven technology — for any purpose that involves Company information is permitted only where the tool has been formally assessed and approved by the IT Security function. The rapid expansion of AI tools in the commercial environment has created a significant and growing shadow IT risk, as employees adopt consumer-grade AI platforms without fully understanding the information implications — including the potential for prompts and inputs to be used to train the AI model, to be retained by the platform provider, or to be accessible to other users of the platform. Employees must not input any Company information — including client information, employee information, financial information, proprietary business information, or any other information classified above Public tier — into any AI tool that has not been formally approved by IT, regardless of how useful or harmless the use might appear.
— Approved AI Tools Are Subject to Information Handling Standards. Where an AI tool has been formally approved for use with Company information, its use remains subject to all applicable information classification and handling standards. Employees using an approved AI tool must not input information of a higher classification tier than the tool has been approved to process, must comply with any specific usage guidelines published by IT for the relevant tool, and must critically evaluate AI-generated outputs before acting on them — particularly where the AI output is used to inform decisions with significant business, legal, or personal consequences. The responsibility for the accuracy and appropriateness of outputs generated by AI tools remains with the employee who uses those outputs, not with the AI system itself.

7.18 Vendor and Supplier Information Security
7.18.1 Vendor Due Diligence and Contractual Obligations
— Vendors Who Process Company Information Must Meet Security Standards. The Company's information security is only as strong as the weakest link in the chain of parties who process that information. Every vendor, supplier, contractor, or service provider who processes Company information — including personal information of employees or clients — on behalf of the Company must be subject to a formal security due diligence assessment before the business relationship commences. This assessment, managed by IT in conjunction with Procurement and Legal, evaluates the vendor's security policies, technical controls, certifications, incident response capability, and compliance with applicable data protection law. A vendor who fails to demonstrate an adequate security posture will not be engaged to process Company information until the identified deficiencies have been remediated and verified.
— Information Processing Agreements Are Mandatory. Every vendor who processes personal information on behalf of the Company must enter into a formal Information Processing Agreement before any personal information is shared. The IPA establishes the purposes for which the vendor may process the information, the security standards they must maintain, their obligations in the event of a security incident, their information retention and disposal obligations, and the Company's right to audit the vendor's compliance. No employee may authorize the sharing of personal information with a vendor who has not executed an approved IPA. Where a vendor requests access to Company information before the IPA is in place, the employee managing the relationship must escalate to Legal and IT immediately and must not share any information until the required agreement is executed.
7.18.2 Ongoing Vendor Security Management
— Vendor Security Is a Continuous Obligation. The Company's security obligations in relation to its vendors do not end at the point of due diligence and contracting. Vendors who process significant volumes of Company information, or who process Confidential or Restricted information, must be subject to ongoing security monitoring — including periodic security reassessment, review of any security incidents affecting the vendor's systems, and assessment of any material changes to the vendor's technology environment or security posture that might affect the information they process on the Company's behalf. Where a vendor experiences a security incident that involves Company information, the vendor must notify the Company within the timeframe specified in the IPA, and the Company's incident response process described in Section 4.4 will be activated immediately.

7.19 Employee Rights in Relation to Information Security
7.19.1 Employee Privacy Rights
— Employees Have Rights Over Their Own Personal Information. As individuals whose personal information is processed by the Company as part of the employment relationship, all employees have rights under applicable data protection law in relation to that information. These rights include the right to access their own personal information held by the Company, the right to request correction of inaccurate personal information, the right to request erasure of personal information that is no longer required for the purpose for which it was collected, the right to object to certain types of processing, and the right to information portability in certain circumstances. The specific rights available to employees and the process for exercising them are described in full in the Company's Data Protection and Privacy Policy referenced as HR-DPP-001. Employees who wish to exercise their data subject rights must submit a formal request through the HR Helpdesk, which will be processed within the timelines required by applicable law.
— Employees Must Not Use Information Access to Circumvent Security Controls. The right to access personal information does not entitle an employee to access information about other individuals, to access Company operational information beyond what their role permits, or to use data subject rights mechanisms as a means of obtaining information for purposes other than the legitimate exercise of their privacy rights. Requests for data subject rights that appear designed to circumvent security controls or access restrictions will be reviewed carefully and, where misuse is suspected, referred to HR and Legal for appropriate action.

7.20 Violations and Disciplinary Consequences
7.20.1 Categories of Security Violations
— Minor Security Violations. Minor security violations are instances of non-compliant behavior that create a limited security risk and do not involve deliberate disregard for the policy. Examples include an isolated failure to lock a screen upon leaving a workstation, a single instance of storing a low-sensitivity document in an unapproved location without awareness that the location was unapproved, failing to apply a required software update within the specified timeframe on one occasion, or an inadvertent misdirected email containing Internal-tier information that is recognized and reported immediately. Minor violations are typically addressed through a coaching conversation and mandatory completion of targeted security awareness training. Where a pattern of minor violations emerges, the response will be escalated to the moderate violation framework.
— Moderate Security Violations. Moderate security violations are more serious breaches that involve repeated minor violations despite prior feedback, deliberate use of an unauthorized tool or channel for Company information, failure to report a suspected security incident in a timely manner, leaving a device unattended and unlocked in a public or shared location, or a first-time instance of sharing Confidential information through an unapproved channel without realizing the channel was unapproved. Moderate violations are addressed through a formal written warning, mandatory completion of targeted security training, and enhanced monitoring of the employee's compliance for a defined period.
— Serious and Gross Security Violations. Serious security violations involve significant and deliberate breaches of this policy — including deliberate circumvention of security controls, unauthorized access to systems or information for which the employee has not been authorized, deliberate sharing of Confidential or Restricted information with unauthorized parties, installation of unauthorized software that results in a security compromise, failure to report a known security incident, or deliberate introduction of malware or other malicious software into Company systems. Gross security violations — including deliberate information theft, unauthorized disclosure of Restricted information to a competitor or external party, deliberate sabotage of Company systems, or any other conduct that fundamentally destroys the trust and integrity on which the employment relationship depends — will result in immediate termination of employment without notice, potential referral to law enforcement authorities, and civil recovery action where appropriate.
7.20.2 The Disciplinary Response Framework
— Security Violations Are Addressed Under the Code of Conduct. All disciplinary action arising from violations of this Information Security Policy will be administered in accordance with the progressive disciplinary framework established in the Code of Conduct referenced as HR-COC-001. Every alleged violation will be the subject of a formal investigation conducted by IT Security and HR before any disciplinary outcome is determined. The employee will be informed of the specific concern in clear and unambiguous terms, will be given a full opportunity to present their perspective and any relevant context, and will be afforded all rights under the Code of Conduct's disciplinary process, including the right to representation and the right to appeal any formal disciplinary decision. The seriousness of the response will be calibrated to the severity of the violation, the employee's prior conduct record, and the impact of the violation on the Company and any affected individuals.

7.21 Training, Awareness, and Security Culture
7.21.1 Mandatory Security Training
— All Employees Must Complete Information Security Training. All employees are required to complete the Company's mandatory information security training program within thirty days of joining the Company and to complete a comprehensive refresher program annually thereafter. This training will cover the Company's information security framework, threat recognition and response, password and credential security, device and network security, phishing and social engineering awareness, the security incident reporting process, and the disciplinary consequences of policy violations. Completion of this training is not optional and will be monitored by IT through the HRMS. Failure to complete mandatory security training within the specified timeframe will be flagged as a compliance concern and addressed through the escalation process.
— Enhanced Training for Higher-Risk Roles. Certain roles and functions carry a heightened information security risk by virtue of the volume or sensitivity of the information they handle — including finance and payroll staff, HR professionals, IT administrators, legal staff, senior leaders, and employees in roles with elevated system access privileges. Employees in such roles will receive additional targeted training covering the specific security risks and obligations relevant to their function, in addition to the general information security training required of all employees. The IT Security function will identify the roles subject to enhanced training requirements and will maintain a current list of those roles in the HRMS.
7.21.2 Building a Culture of Security Consciousness
— Security Is a Daily Practice, Not an Annual Checkbox. The Company recognizes that mandatory annual training, however well-designed and well-delivered, is insufficient by itself to create and sustain a genuinely security-conscious organizational culture. Security must be a daily practice — present in every decision about how information is handled, in every interaction with a system, in every choice about where to store a document or how to share a file. The Company will therefore supplement mandatory training with regular security communications covering emerging threats and practical guidance, targeted awareness campaigns on specific security behaviors and risks, simulated phishing exercises that build practical threat recognition skills, recognition of security-conscious behavior in performance frameworks, and the creation of an organizational environment in which reporting security concerns, asking security questions, and raising security risks is genuinely welcomed, actively encouraged, and never penalized. The goal is an organization in which every employee thinks like a security professional — not because they are required to, but because they genuinely understand why it matters and have been equipped with the knowledge to act on that understanding.

7.22 Policy Compliance, Governance, and Review
7.22.1 Responsibility for Policy Compliance
— Every Employee's Personal Responsibility. Compliance with this Information Security Policy is the personal and non-delegable responsibility of every employee without exception. Employees are responsible for reading and understanding this policy in full, for applying its standards in every aspect of their work that involves Company information or systems, for seeking guidance from the IT Security function or HR when they encounter a situation that is not clearly covered by this policy or where they are uncertain about the correct course of action, for reporting security concerns and incidents through the appropriate channels without delay, and for completing all mandatory security training within the required timeframes. Compliance with this policy is not optional, is not dependent on personal convenience, and is not subject to individual interpretation or selective application.
— Manager and Leadership Responsibility. Managers and leaders bear a heightened and additional responsibility for the security culture within their teams. They must model the security behaviors expected of all employees in their own conduct at all times, must ensure that their team members complete mandatory security training within the required timeframes, must create an environment in which team members feel safe and encouraged to report security concerns without fear of adverse consequence, must not create or condone workarounds that bypass security controls for operational convenience, and must cooperate fully with any security investigation or audit that involves their function or their team.
— IT Security and HR Responsibility. The IT Security function is responsible for the technical implementation and monitoring of this policy's requirements, for administering the security incident response process, for conducting the security audit program, for maintaining the approved tools and services lists, and for providing employees with the training, guidance, and support they need to comply with their obligations under this policy. HR is responsible for administering the employee-facing provisions of this policy, for managing the disciplinary consequences of security violations in conjunction with IT, and for ensuring that this policy is clearly communicated, consistently applied, and formally reviewed on the schedule described in Section 4.22.3.
7.22.2 Employee Acknowledgment
— Formal Acknowledgment Is Mandatory. All employees are required to formally acknowledge that they have read, understood, and agree to comply with this Information Security Policy. Acknowledgment will be captured through the HRMS at the time of onboarding for all new employees and upon each substantive update to the policy for all existing employees. The acknowledgment record will be maintained in the employee's personnel file. Failure to complete the acknowledgment within the timeline specified by HR will be treated as a compliance concern and addressed accordingly. Acknowledgment of this policy does not substitute for completion of the mandatory security training program — both requirements are independent and both must be fulfilled.
7.22.3 Annual Policy Review     
— Regular Review to Maintain Relevance and Currency. This Information Security Policy will be formally reviewed on an annual basis by the IT Security function and HR, or earlier if required by a material change in the threat landscape or the Company's technology environment, a significant development in applicable cybersecurity legislation, the identification of a gap in the current policy revealed by a security incident, audit finding, or regulatory development, or feedback from employees, managers, or external assessors that reveals an area for improvement or updating. All updates will be approved by the Chief Human Resources Officer in conjunction with the Head of IT, communicated to all employees through the HRMS and official Company communication channels, and formally acknowledged by all employees within fifteen working days of communication. The Company's commitment to maintaining a current, rigorous, and genuinely effective Information Security Policy is both a legal obligation and a fundamental expression of its duty of care to the people whose information it holds and the clients whose trust it depends upon.
