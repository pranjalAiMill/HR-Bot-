POLICY 5: PERFORMANCE MANAGEMENT POLICY

1. PURPOSE
This Performance Management Policy establishes a consistent, transparent, and fair framework for setting expectations, evaluating performance, providing feedback, supporting development, and addressing underperformance across the organization. The organization views performance management as a continuous, two-way conversation — not an annual administrative event — designed to unlock the potential of every employee.

2. SCOPE
This policy applies to all permanent and fixed-term employees at all levels. Probationary employees are subject to the Probation Review process as set out in Section 6.

3. PERFORMANCE MANAGEMENT PHILOSOPHY
The organization believes that performance management, done well, is one of the most powerful tools for employee development, engagement, and retention. Our approach is built on three principles:
Clarity: Every employee knows what is expected of them, how they will be measured, and how they are performing against those expectations at all times.
Fairness: Performance is evaluated based on objective evidence and measurable outcomes, not on subjective opinion, personal relationships, or bias.
Growth: Performance management is forward-looking. Even where performance is below expectation, the primary goal is to support improvement, not to document a path to dismissal.

4. THE PERFORMANCE MANAGEMENT CYCLE
The organization operates a twelve-month performance cycle aligned with the calendar year (January to December). The cycle comprises the following key stages:
4.1 Goal Setting (January — February)
At the beginning of each performance cycle, every employee and their line manager collaboratively set individual performance goals using the SMART framework:
- Specific: The goal is clearly defined and unambiguous.
- Measurable: Progress and achievement can be objectively measured.
- Achievable: The goal is realistic given the employee's role, resources, and experience.
- Relevant: The goal is aligned with team and organizational objectives.
- Time-Bound: The goal has a defined deadline or milestone schedule.
Each employee must have between three (3) and six (6) SMART goals, encompassing both quantitative KPIs (Key Performance Indicators) and qualitative behavioral competencies. Goals, once agreed, must be documented and signed off by both the employee and manager in the HRMS.
4.2 Continuous Feedback & Check-ins (Ongoing throughout the year)
Formal one-to-one check-in meetings between the employee and their manager must occur at least monthly. The purpose of these meetings is to:
- Review progress against goals and identify obstacles.
- Provide real-time, constructive feedback — both positive reinforcement and developmental coaching.
- Discuss workload, wellbeing, and any emerging concerns.
- Revise goals if business priorities have significantly shifted.
- Feedback provided during check-ins must be documented in the HRMS and accessible to both parties.
4.3 Mid-Year Review (June — July)
A formal mid-year review is conducted at the midpoint of the performance cycle. The mid-year review:
- Formally assesses performance against goals to date.
- Identifies whether goals need to be adjusted for the second half of the year.
- Provides a preliminary performance rating to enable the employee to self-correct where needed before the year-end review.
- Discusses learning and development priorities for the second half of the year.
The mid-year review outcome is documented in the HRMS and signed off by both the employee and manager.

5. PERFORMANCE RATINGS
The organization uses a five-point performance rating scale:
5 — Exceptional: Performance consistently and significantly exceeds all goals and expectations. The employee demonstrates outstanding leadership, innovation, and impact. Reserved for the top 5–10% of the workforce.
4 — Exceeds Expectations: Performance consistently meets and frequently exceeds goals and expectations. The employee demonstrates strong competencies and makes significant contributions beyond their core role.
3 — Meets Expectations: Performance consistently meets all or most goals and expectations. The employee is a solid, reliable contributor who fulfills their role effectively.
2 — Partially Meets Expectations: Performance meets some but not all goals and expectations. Improvement is required in specific areas. A Performance Improvement Plan may be initiated.
1 — Does Not Meet Expectations: Performance consistently falls below the minimum required standards. A formal Performance Improvement Plan is required.

6. PROBATIONARY REVIEW
All new employees are subject to a probationary period of three (3) to six (6) months, as defined in their employment contract. During this period:
- A structured review is conducted at the midpoint (typically weeks six to eight) of the probationary period to assess progress and provide early feedback.
- A final probationary review is conducted no later than two (2) weeks before the end of the probationary period.
- The outcome of the final review is one of: (a) Confirmation in post, (b) Extension of probation by up to three (3) months, or (c) Termination of employment during or at the end of the probationary period.
- Employees whose probation is terminated will be provided with specific, documented reasons.


7. BEHAVIORAL COMPETENCIES
In addition to goal achievement, all employees are assessed against a set of core organizational behavioral competencies. These competencies reflect the values, behaviors, and mindsets the organization requires for sustained success:
- Communication: Communicates clearly, confidently, and constructively across all levels and channels. Actively listens and adapts style to the audience.
- Collaboration & Teamwork: Works effectively with others, shares information openly, and contributes positively to team dynamics.
- Problem-Solving & Innovation: Identifies problems, thinks critically, and develops effective and creative solutions.
- Accountability & Ownership: Takes full responsibility for their work, follows through on commitments, and does not deflect blame.
- Customer Focus: Understands internal and external customer needs and consistently strives to deliver exceptional service.
- Adaptability & Resilience: Responds positively to change, manages pressure effectively, and maintains performance in ambiguous or challenging situations.
- Leadership (for People Managers): Inspires, develops, and empowers team members. Sets a clear direction, provides coaching, and makes sound decisions.
Each competency is rated on the same five-point scale as overall performance.

8. PERFORMANCE IMPROVEMENT PLAN (PIP)
8.1 Purpose
A Performance Improvement Plan is a formal, structured support tool used when an employee's performance is rated as 2 (Partially Meets Expectations) or 1 (Does Not Meet Expectations). The PIP is not a disciplinary document — it is a genuine attempt to support the employee to achieve the required standard of performance.
8.2 PIP Structure
A PIP must contain:
- A clear description of the current performance shortfall, supported by specific examples and evidence.
- The specific, measurable performance standards the employee is expected to achieve.
- A defined timeframe for the PIP, typically sixty (60) to ninety (90) calendar days.
- The support the organization will provide (coaching, training, mentoring, resources).
- The frequency and format of review check-ins during the PIP period (typically weekly or bi-weekly).
- Clear statement of the consequences if the required standard is not achieved within the timeframe.

8.3 PIP Process
Step 1 — The line manager, with HR guidance, prepares a draft PIP.
Step 2 — A formal PIP meeting is held with the employee, manager, and HR. The employee is given the opportunity to respond and contribute to the plan.
Step 3 — The PIP is signed by the employee, manager, and HR. A copy is provided to the employee.
Step 4 — Regular check-in meetings are held throughout the PIP period to provide feedback and track progress.
Step 5 — At the conclusion of the PIP:
— If the required standard has been met: The PIP is formally closed with written confirmation to the employee.
— If the required standard has not been met: The organization may initiate formal disciplinary proceedings, which may result in demotion, role change, or dismissal on the grounds of capability.
8.4 Employee Rights During a PIP
An employee subject to a PIP has the right to:
- Be accompanied to all PIP meetings by a trade union representative or a workplace colleague.
- Appeal the initiation of the PIP through the Appraisal Appeal Process.
- Receive all PIP documentation in writing.
- Raise a grievance if they believe the PIP is being used unfairly or punitively.


9. APPRAISAL APPEAL PROCESS
If an employee believes their performance rating is unfair, inaccurate, or the result of bias, they may appeal through the following process:
Step 1 — The employee submits a written appeal to the HR Business Partner within ten (10) working days of receiving their final rating, clearly setting out the grounds for the appeal and supporting evidence.
Step 2 — HR acknowledges the appeal within three (3) working days.
Step 3 — An independent appeal panel comprising the skip-level manager and a senior HR representative (neither of whom was involved in the original appraisal) reviews the evidence and conducts an appeal meeting with the employee within fifteen (15) working days.
Step 4 — The appeal panel issues a written decision within five (5) working days of the appeal meeting. The decision may be to uphold, partially uphold, or dismiss the appeal.
Step 5 — The decision of the appeal panel is final within the internal process. This does not preclude the employee from pursuing external legal remedies where applicable.

10. LINK TO COMPENSATION & CAREER DEVELOPMENT
Performance ratings directly inform the annual compensation review cycle and career progression decisions. The following indicative framework applies:
- Employees rated 5 (Exceptional): Eligible for maximum merit increase and a performance bonus. Priority consideration for promotion and accelerated development programs.
- Employees rated 4 (Exceeds Expectations): Eligible for above-average merit increase and performance bonus.
- Employees rated 3 (Meets Expectations): Eligible for standard merit increase in line with market benchmarks.
- Employees rated 2 (Partially Meets Expectations): Typically ineligible for a merit increase until performance improves to the required standard.
- Employees rated 1 (Does Not Meet Expectations): No merit increase. Subject to a PIP. Ineligible for bonus.

11. MANAGER ACCOUNTABILITY
Managers are themselves evaluated on the quality of their performance management practices, including the timeliness and quality of appraisals, effectiveness of feedback, the development of their team members, and their ability to address underperformance constructively. Consistently poor performance management by a manager will itself be treated as a performance issue.
