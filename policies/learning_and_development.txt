6. LEARNING AND DEVELOPMENT POLICY

6.1 Purpose and Organizational Philosophy
6.1.1 Why This Policy Exists
— The Foundation of Organizational Capability. The Learning and Development Policy is one of the most strategically critical documents in the Company's entire policy framework. It establishes the principles, standards, and frameworks through which the Company invests in the continuous growth and development of its workforce — enabling employees to enhance their capabilities, advance their careers, and contribute at their highest potential while ensuring that the organization maintains the skills, knowledge, and competencies required to achieve its strategic objectives. Where the Employment Policy referenced as HR-EP-001 governs the terms and conditions of employment, and the Performance Management Policy referenced as HR-PMP-001 governs how performance is evaluated and managed, this policy governs how the Company invests in human capital development — which in the modern business environment is among the most important determinants of organizational success and competitive advantage. Learning and development is not a discretionary benefit provided to employees. It is a strategic investment in organizational capability, and this policy is the framework through which that investment is made.
— The Organizational Imperative. The Company operates in a rapidly evolving business environment where the pace of change in technology, market dynamics, regulatory requirements, and competitive landscapes requires organizations to continuously adapt, innovate, and evolve. The skills and knowledge that were sufficient yesterday may be inadequate tomorrow, and the capabilities that drive success today may become obsolete as new challenges and opportunities emerge. A workforce that is not continuously learning and developing cannot maintain the organizational capability required to compete effectively, serve clients successfully, or achieve strategic objectives. This policy exists to ensure that the Company maintains a systematic, comprehensive, and effective approach to workforce development that enables the organization to adapt to change, seize opportunities, and sustain competitive advantage through the continuous enhancement of human capital.
— The Human Imperative. This policy has been written with a genuine recognition that professional growth and career development are among the most important factors in employee engagement, satisfaction, and retention. Employees join organizations not only to perform work and receive compensation, but to grow professionally, to develop new skills and capabilities, to advance their careers, and to achieve their professional aspirations. An organization that does not invest meaningfully in employee development cannot attract, retain, or fully engage talented individuals, and cannot unlock the full potential of its workforce. This policy is not a declaration of the Company's generosity. It is a recognition of mutual benefit — a clear, comprehensive, and accessible framework that enables employees to pursue their professional growth objectives while ensuring that the organization benefits from enhanced capability, improved performance, and sustained competitive advantage. The Company invests in this policy, and in the programs and resources that support it, because it genuinely believes that employee development is essential to both individual fulfillment and organizational success.
— The Philosophy of Shared Investment. Learning and development is a shared investment between the Company and its employees. The Company invests financial resources, time, infrastructure, and expertise to provide learning opportunities, development programs, and growth pathways. Employees invest their time, effort, commitment, and engagement to participate in development activities, to apply learning in their work, and to contribute their enhanced capabilities to organizational success. This policy is built on the conviction that both parties to the development relationship have responsibilities that must be fulfilled — the Company's responsibility to provide meaningful development opportunities and support, and the employee's responsibility to engage actively, apply learning, and contribute enhanced capability. Development is most effective when both parties are fully committed to the investment, and this policy ensures that commitment is clear, mutual, and sustained.
— A Living and Evolving Document. Like all enterprise policies, the Learning and Development Policy is a living document that will be reviewed, updated, and strengthened as the business environment evolves, as the Company's strategic objectives change, as new learning methodologies and technologies emerge, and as practical experience provides insights into how the policy can be made more effective. Every employee is expected to engage with this policy seriously, to take advantage of the opportunities it provides, and to treat it as a genuine and relevant guide to one of the most important aspects of their professional relationship with the Company.

6.2 Scope and Applicability
6.2.1 Who This Policy Applies To
— Universal Application to All Employees. This policy applies universally and without exception to every individual engaged by the Company in any employment capacity — regardless of seniority, tenure, professional standing, department, business unit, geographic location, or technical background. The most senior executive in the organization is entitled to the same development opportunities and subject to the same development expectations as the most junior team member, and the Company's commitment to employee development extends to every level of the organization. While specific program eligibility may vary based on role requirements, performance, and business needs, the fundamental principle that every employee has access to meaningful development opportunities applies universally.
— All Categories of Employment Covered. This policy covers permanent full-time employees, part-time employees, employees on fixed-term or rolling contracts, interns and trainees, and any other individual engaged in an employment relationship with the Company. Each of these individuals, regardless of the nature or duration of their employment, is entitled to development opportunities appropriate to their role and circumstances, and is expected to engage in development activities that enhance their capability and contribution. The specific application of certain policy provisions may vary based on employment classification — for example, eligibility for high-investment programs may be prioritized for permanent employees — but the fundamental framework of development opportunities and expectations applies to all employees.
— Seniority Confers Enhanced Opportunities, Not Exemption. Senior leaders, managers, and those with elevated organizational responsibilities are not exempt from this policy — they are provided with enhanced development opportunities because of the greater impact of their roles, the more complex challenges they face, and the critical importance of their continued growth to organizational success. Leadership development is a particular focus of this policy, and leaders are expected to model commitment to continuous learning, to invest in their own development, and to support the development of their team members actively.
6.2.2 Where This Policy Applies
— All Work Locations and Arrangements. This policy applies in every location and arrangement in which Company work is performed — whether on Company premises, at client sites, at home in a remote or hybrid arrangement, while traveling on Company business, or in any other location where an employee is performing work on behalf of the Company. Development opportunities are available to employees regardless of their physical location, and the Company provides remote and virtual learning options to ensure that all employees can access development programs regardless of where they work.
— All Phases of the Employment Relationship. This policy applies throughout the entire lifecycle of the employment relationship — from onboarding and initial skill development, through ongoing capability enhancement, during role transitions and promotions, and throughout the employee's career with the Company. Development is not a one-time activity that occurs during onboarding or annual training cycles. It is a continuous process that supports employees at every stage of their career journey with the Company.
6.2.3 Relationship With Other Policies
— An Integrated Development Framework. This Learning and Development Policy operates as one component of the Company's broader human resources and talent management framework. It must be read and applied in conjunction with the Employment Policy referenced as HR-EP-001, the Performance Management Policy referenced as HR-PMP-001, the Code of Conduct referenced as HR-COC-001, and all other applicable Company policies. Where a specific policy provides more detailed guidance on a particular aspect of development — such as the Performance Management Policy's treatment of development planning — that specific policy governs. Where a development concern arises that falls outside the scope of any specific policy, this Learning and Development Policy provides the governing framework.
— Conflict Resolution. In the event of any apparent conflict between the standards established in this policy and those established in any other Company policy, employees must seek guidance from HR or the Talent Development function before taking action. Employees must not act unilaterally on their own interpretation of any apparent conflict between policies and must not use an apparent conflict as justification for departing from development standards that would otherwise apply.

6.3 Learning Categories and Development Framework
6.3.1 Mandatory Compliance Training
— Why Mandatory Training Exists. Mandatory compliance training is non-negotiable and applies to all employees without exception. These programs are designed to ensure that every individual understands and adheres to the legal, regulatory, ethical, and policy obligations that govern their conduct within the organization and in relation to external stakeholders. Compliance training is not discretionary because the consequences of non-compliance — including legal liability, regulatory penalties, reputational damage, and harm to individuals — are too serious to allow for optional participation. The Company has both a legal obligation and a moral responsibility to ensure that every employee understands their compliance obligations, and mandatory training is the mechanism through which that understanding is achieved.
— Scope of Mandatory Training. Mandatory compliance training covers topics including but not limited to the Code of Conduct and ethical standards, data protection and information security requirements, prevention of sexual harassment and workplace discrimination, anti-bribery and anti-corruption standards, workplace health and safety requirements, and any other topics required by applicable law, regulation, or Company policy. The specific topics covered in mandatory training may vary by role, location, or regulatory requirements, and employees are required to complete all mandatory training applicable to their role and circumstances.
— Completion Requirements and Timelines. All employees are required to complete mandatory compliance training within the timelines prescribed by HR, typically within the first thirty days of joining the Company and annually thereafter. New employees receive mandatory training as part of their onboarding process, and existing employees complete annual refresher training to ensure that their understanding remains current. Employees who join mid-year or who have role changes that require additional mandatory training must complete the required training within thirty days of the change. Non-completion of mandatory training within the stipulated timeline will be flagged by HR and may be treated as a compliance breach, with escalation to the reporting manager and relevant department head.
— Tracking and Compliance Monitoring. Completion records for all mandatory training are maintained in the HRMS and are available for audit and regulatory review at any time. HR monitors completion rates, identifies employees who have not completed required training, and escalates non-compliance through appropriate channels. Managers are responsible for ensuring that their team members complete mandatory training on time, and failure to complete mandatory training may affect an employee's eligibility for certain benefits, promotions, or other opportunities.
6.3.2 Functional and Technical Training
— Purpose of Functional and Technical Development. Functional and technical training is role-specific and is designed to equip employees with the domain knowledge, technical skills, and process expertise required to perform their responsibilities at the highest level of competence. In a rapidly evolving business environment, technical skills can become obsolete quickly, and functional knowledge must be continuously updated to reflect changes in processes, technologies, regulations, and best practices. Functional and technical training ensures that employees maintain the expertise required to excel in their roles and that the organization maintains the technical capability required to serve clients effectively and compete successfully.
— Identification of Development Needs. Functional and technical development needs are identified through a combination of role requirements analysis, skill gap assessments, manager inputs, performance review discussions, employee self-assessments, and evolving business needs. The Company maintains a skills framework that defines the technical and functional competencies required for each role, and development needs are identified by comparing current capability against role requirements. Employees are encouraged to proactively discuss their functional development needs with their reporting manager during goal-setting, performance review, and development planning conversations.
— Delivery Methods and Approaches. Functional and technical training may be delivered through internal subject matter experts, external trainers, industry certifications, online courses, workshops, on-the-job training, mentoring, or a blended approach depending on the nature of the skill being developed and the most effective learning method. The Company maintains relationships with external training providers, certification bodies, and educational institutions to ensure that employees have access to high-quality functional and technical development opportunities. Role-critical certifications that are required for regulatory or client compliance must be completed within the timelines specified by the relevant department head and HR.
— Onboarding and Initial Functional Development. Newly hired employees receive a structured functional induction plan as part of their onboarding to ensure they are equipped with the foundational knowledge required to perform their role from day one. Functional onboarding includes role-specific training, process documentation, system training, and introductions to key stakeholders and resources. The functional onboarding process is designed to accelerate time-to-productivity and ensure that new employees can contribute effectively as quickly as possible.
6.3.3 Leadership Development
— The Strategic Importance of Leadership Development. Leadership development programs are designed to build a strong, capable, and future-ready leadership pipeline within the organization. Effective leadership is one of the most critical determinants of organizational success, and the Company's ability to achieve its strategic objectives depends fundamentally on having leaders at every level who can inspire, guide, and enable their teams to perform at their highest potential. Leadership development is not a luxury reserved for senior executives. It is a strategic investment in organizational capability that ensures the Company has the leadership talent required to navigate challenges, seize opportunities, and sustain success over the long term.
— Program Structure and Content. Leadership development programs may include structured management development courses covering leadership fundamentals, strategic thinking, people management, and organizational effectiveness, executive coaching that provides personalized development support for senior leaders, mentoring arrangements that pair developing leaders with experienced mentors, cross-functional project exposure that provides leadership experience in different contexts, participation in external leadership forums or institutions, and other development experiences designed to build leadership capability. Leadership programs are designed to be comprehensive, practical, and aligned with the Company's leadership model and values.
— Participant Identification and Selection. Identification of participants for leadership development programs is carried out through the annual talent review and succession planning process, in consultation with senior leadership and HR. Participants are selected based on demonstrated leadership potential, performance track record, readiness for increased responsibility, alignment with the Company's values and leadership model, and business need for leadership capability in specific areas. The selection process is designed to be fair, transparent, and based on objective assessment criteria.
— Expectations and Commitments. Employees nominated for leadership development programs are expected to engage fully, complete all program requirements, apply their learning within their teams and day-to-day responsibilities, and demonstrate commitment to their own development and to the organization. Leadership development is viewed as a long-term investment by the Company, and participants are expected to demonstrate a commensurate commitment to the organization in return. Participants may be required to sign service commitment agreements for high-investment programs, and outcomes from leadership programs will be considered in succession planning, promotion decisions, and role progression discussions.
6.3.4 Behavioral and Soft Skills Training
— Why Behavioral Skills Matter. Behavioral and soft skills training addresses the interpersonal, communication, and professional effectiveness dimensions of an employee's development — capabilities that are often just as critical to success as technical expertise. In a collaborative, client-focused, and team-oriented business environment, the ability to communicate effectively, work collaboratively, manage time and priorities, resolve conflicts constructively, and demonstrate emotional intelligence can be the difference between adequate performance and exceptional performance. Behavioral excellence is a hallmark of a high-performance culture, and the Company actively supports employees in developing these capabilities at every stage of their careers.
— Scope of Behavioral Development. Programs under this category include but are not limited to communication and presentation skills, time management and productivity, emotional intelligence and self-awareness, conflict resolution and negotiation, collaboration and teamwork, customer service excellence, cross-cultural awareness and inclusion, change management and adaptability, and other behavioral competencies that enhance professional effectiveness. Behavioral development needs may be identified through performance reviews, 360-degree feedback, peer assessments, manager observations, employee self-assessments, or as part of an individual's personal development plan.
— Availability and Encouragement. Behavioral and soft skills programs are available to all employees and are particularly encouraged for those transitioning into new roles, taking on team management responsibilities, or identified as requiring development in specific behavioral areas through the appraisal process. The Company strongly believes that behavioral excellence is achievable for every employee regardless of their role or background, and provides resources, support, and encouragement to help employees develop these capabilities. Behavioral development is not viewed as remedial — it is viewed as continuous enhancement that benefits both the individual and the organization.

6.4 Training Delivery Methods and Modalities
6.4.1 Instructor-Led Training
— The Value of Instructor-Led Programs. Instructor-led training provides a structured, interactive, and collaborative learning environment that is ideal for complex topics that benefit from real-time discussion, facilitation, and group learning dynamics. Instructor-led programs enable participants to ask questions, engage in discussions, practice skills in a supportive environment, and learn from the experiences and perspectives of other participants. This method is particularly effective for leadership development, behavioral skills training, and complex technical topics that require explanation, demonstration, and guided practice.
— Formats and Structures. Instructor-led training may be delivered in-person, virtually, or in a hybrid format depending on the topic, audience, and logistical considerations. Programs may be delivered as full-day workshops, multi-day courses, or series of shorter sessions spread over time. The Company maintains internal subject matter experts who can deliver instructor-led training, and also engages external trainers and consultants when specialized expertise is required. Instructor-led programs are designed to be engaging, practical, and immediately applicable to participants' work.
6.4.2 E-Learning and Online Training
— The Flexibility of E-Learning. E-Learning modules offer flexibility and self-paced learning, enabling employees to access training content at a time and place that suits their schedule. This method is particularly valuable for mandatory compliance training, foundational knowledge programs, and topics that can be effectively delivered through structured content and assessments. E-Learning enables the Company to provide consistent training content to large numbers of employees across different locations and time zones, and allows employees to learn at their own pace and review content as needed.
— E-Learning Platform and Content. The Company maintains an e-learning platform that provides access to a library of training courses covering compliance topics, functional skills, technical competencies, and behavioral development. The platform is accessible from any device with internet connectivity, and employees can access courses on-demand based on their development needs and schedules. E-learning content is regularly updated to ensure accuracy and relevance, and new courses are added based on identified development needs and emerging topics.
6.4.3 Workshops and Seminars
— Focused Learning Experiences. Workshops and seminars provide focused, intensive learning experiences that are particularly effective for skill application, case study discussions, and peer-to-peer learning. These formats enable participants to dive deep into specific topics, practice skills in a controlled environment, and learn from the experiences and insights of other participants. Workshops and seminars may be delivered internally by Company subject matter experts or externally by industry experts, and may be organized as standalone events or as part of larger development programs.
— Application and Practice. Workshops and seminars are designed to be highly interactive and application-focused, with opportunities for participants to practice skills, discuss real-world scenarios, and receive feedback from facilitators and peers. These formats are particularly effective for behavioral skills development, where practice and feedback are essential for skill acquisition and improvement.
6.4.4 External Certifications and Professional Qualifications
— The Value of External Credentials. External certifications allow employees to gain recognized professional qualifications that enhance both their individual credibility and the organization's capability profile. Industry certifications demonstrate expertise in specific domains, provide external validation of skills and knowledge, and may be required for regulatory compliance or client requirements. The Company supports employees in pursuing relevant external certifications that align with their role requirements and career objectives.
— Sponsorship and Support. External certifications may be fully or partially sponsored by the Company based on relevance to the employee's role, business need, and alignment with organizational objectives. Employees who wish to pursue external certifications must obtain approval from their manager and HR, and may be required to sign service commitment agreements for high-cost certifications. The Company provides support for certification preparation, including study materials, time allocation, and in some cases, reimbursement of examination fees. Employees who successfully complete Company-sponsored certifications are expected to apply their new knowledge and credentials to their work and to share their expertise with colleagues.
6.4.5 Blended Learning Approaches
— Combining Methods for Maximum Effectiveness. The Company may leverage blended learning approaches, combining multiple delivery methods to create a richer and more effective development experience. Blended learning recognizes that different learning objectives and topics are best served by different delivery methods, and that combining methods can create a more comprehensive and engaging learning experience. For example, a leadership development program might combine e-learning modules for foundational knowledge, instructor-led workshops for skill practice, mentoring for personalized support, and on-the-job application for real-world experience.
— Customization and Flexibility. Blended learning approaches allow the Company to customize development programs to meet specific needs, to accommodate different learning styles and preferences, and to maximize the effectiveness of development investments. The choice of delivery methods for any given program is determined based on the nature of the content, the target audience, learning objectives, cost considerations, and desired outcomes.

6.5 Development Planning and Individual Development Plans
6.5.1 The Purpose of Development Planning
— Development Planning as a Foundation for Growth. Development planning is the process through which employees and their managers identify development needs, set development objectives, and create plans for achieving those objectives. Effective development planning ensures that development activities are purposeful, aligned with role requirements and career aspirations, and integrated with performance management and career development processes. Development planning is not a one-time activity that occurs during annual reviews. It is an ongoing process that is reviewed and updated regularly to reflect changing needs, emerging opportunities, and evolving career objectives.
— Alignment with Performance and Career Objectives. Development planning is closely integrated with performance management and career development processes. Development needs are identified through performance reviews, skill assessments, and career discussions, and development plans are designed to address those needs while supporting career advancement and organizational objectives. Development planning ensures that employees understand what skills and capabilities they need to develop, why those developments are important, and how they can achieve their development objectives.
6.5.2 Creating and Maintaining Individual Development Plans
— Collaborative Development Planning. Individual Development Plans (IDPs) are created collaboratively between employees and their managers, typically during performance review discussions or dedicated development planning sessions. The IDP process includes assessment of current capabilities against role requirements and career objectives, identification of development needs and gaps, setting of specific development objectives, selection of development activities and methods, establishment of timelines and milestones, and agreement on support and resources required. IDPs are documented in writing, stored in the HRMS, and reviewed regularly to track progress and make adjustments as needed.
— Components of an Effective IDP. An effective Individual Development Plan includes clear development objectives that are specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound, identification of the skills, knowledge, or behaviors to be developed, selection of appropriate development activities such as training courses, on-the-job experiences, mentoring, or other methods, establishment of timelines and milestones for development activities, identification of resources and support needed, and agreement on how development progress will be measured and evaluated. IDPs are living documents that are updated as development needs change, as objectives are achieved, and as new opportunities emerge.
6.5.3 Manager Support and Accountability
— Manager Responsibility for Development Support. Managers are responsible for supporting their team members' development by identifying development needs, facilitating access to development opportunities, providing on-the-job coaching and feedback, creating stretch assignments and learning experiences, and removing barriers to development. Managers are expected to have regular development conversations with their team members, to review IDPs regularly, to provide feedback on development progress, and to recognize and reward development achievements. Manager support for employee development is evaluated as part of the manager's own performance review.
— Employee Ownership of Development. While managers play a critical role in supporting development, employees are ultimately responsible for their own development and for taking ownership of their learning journey. Employees are expected to proactively identify their development needs, to seek out development opportunities, to engage actively in development activities, to apply learning in their work, and to take initiative for their own growth. The Company provides resources and support, but employees must take responsibility for their own development and for making the most of the opportunities provided.

6.6 Eligibility, Prioritization, and Governance
6.6.1 Principles of Fair Access
— Commitment to Equitable Access. The Company is committed to making learning and development opportunities accessible to as many employees as possible, recognizing that development is a fundamental aspect of the employment relationship and a key factor in employee engagement and retention. However, given the need to prioritize resources effectively and to ensure that development investments generate maximum value, participation in specific programs may be governed by eligibility criteria, prioritization frameworks, and business needs. The Company strives to balance equitable access with strategic investment, ensuring that development opportunities are distributed fairly while prioritizing investments that generate the greatest organizational value.
— Transparent Eligibility Criteria. Eligibility criteria for development programs are established transparently and communicated clearly to employees. Criteria may include role requirements, performance ratings, tenure, business need, and other factors relevant to program objectives. Employees are informed of eligibility criteria when programs are announced, and managers are provided with guidance on nominating employees for programs. The Company does not use arbitrary or discriminatory criteria for program eligibility, and all eligibility decisions are based on objective, job-related factors.
6.6.2 Prioritization Frameworks
— Performance-Based Prioritization. Employees with strong or developing performance ratings will be given priority access to development programs, particularly high-investment or competitive nominations. Performance-based prioritization recognizes that development investments generate the greatest return when made in employees who are performing well and have demonstrated potential for growth. This approach is not intended to exclude lower-performing employees from development opportunities, but to ensure that high performers have access to the development they need to advance and that development investments are made where they will have the greatest impact.
— Manager Nomination and Support. Many programs require formal nomination by the reporting manager, who is best placed to assess the employee's readiness, relevance of the program to their role, and potential return on the development investment. Manager nomination ensures that development activities are aligned with role requirements and business needs, and that employees have manager support for their development. Managers are expected to nominate employees fairly based on objective criteria, to provide honest assessments of readiness and fit, and to support nominated employees throughout the development process.
— Business Requirements and Strategic Alignment. Certain programs will be prioritized based on immediate or emerging business needs, ensuring that organizational capability keeps pace with strategic demands. When the Company identifies critical skill gaps or emerging capability requirements, development programs may be prioritized to address those needs quickly. Business-driven prioritization ensures that development investments support strategic objectives and that the organization has the capabilities it needs to succeed.
— Talent Development Strategy. Program access may also be guided by the Company's broader talent management strategy, including succession planning priorities and high-potential identification. Employees identified as high-potential talent or as successors for critical roles may receive priority access to development programs that prepare them for future responsibilities. Talent-driven prioritization ensures that the Company maintains a strong leadership pipeline and that critical roles have capable successors.
6.6.3 Service Commitment Agreements
— When Service Commitments Are Required. For high-cost external programs, certifications, or sponsored education, employees may be required to sign a service commitment agreement stipulating a minimum period of continued employment following completion of the program. Service commitment agreements protect the Company's investment in employee development and ensure that the organization receives a return on its development investment. The terms of service commitment agreements are clearly communicated and mutually agreed upon before the training commences, and employees have the opportunity to review and understand the commitment before signing.
— Terms and Conditions. Service commitment agreements specify the training or program being sponsored, the cost of the investment, the minimum period of continued employment required, and the consequences of early departure. The commitment period is typically proportional to the cost of the investment and may range from six months for moderate-cost programs to two years or more for high-cost programs such as executive education or advanced degrees. Employees who voluntarily resign before the agreed commitment period may be required to reimburse a proportionate share of the training investment, as outlined in the agreement.
— Fairness and Reasonableness. Service commitment agreements are designed to be fair and reasonable, balancing the Company's need to protect its investment with the employee's right to pursue career opportunities. The Company does not use service commitments punitively or to restrict legitimate career mobility, and commitment periods are set at reasonable levels that reflect the actual investment made. Employees who have concerns about service commitment terms are encouraged to discuss them with HR before signing.

6.7 Career Impact and Development Outcomes
6.7.1 Development and Career Advancement
— Development as a Pathway to Advancement. Learning and development is intrinsically linked to an employee's career trajectory within the organization. The Company views demonstrated commitment to growth and the practical application of learning as key indicators of an employee's potential and readiness for advancement. Employees who actively engage in development, who apply learning effectively in their work, and who demonstrate continuous growth are more likely to be considered for promotions, role expansions, and career advancement opportunities. Development is not a guarantee of advancement, but it is a critical factor in advancement decisions.
— Promotion Considerations. Completion of relevant training programs and demonstration of applied learning will be considered as part of promotion decisions, particularly where a role requires specific competencies that have been developed through formal learning. When evaluating candidates for promotion, the Company considers not only current performance but also demonstrated growth, acquired capabilities, and readiness for increased responsibility. Employees who have invested in their development and who have acquired the skills required for advancement are more likely to be selected for promotion opportunities.
6.7.2 Development and Compensation
— Capability Development and Compensation Adjustments. Sustained capability development and the acquisition of new skills may positively influence compensation adjustments during appraisal cycles, particularly where the new skills directly contribute to business value. When employees develop capabilities that enhance their contribution to the organization, that enable them to take on additional responsibilities, or that are in high demand, the Company may recognize that enhanced contribution through compensation adjustments. Development-related compensation adjustments are based on the value of the new capabilities to the organization, not merely on the completion of training programs.
— Market Value and Skill Premiums. Employees who develop skills that are in high demand in the market may receive compensation adjustments that reflect the market value of those skills. The Company recognizes that certain skills command premium compensation in the market, and employees who develop those skills may receive adjustments that align with market rates. Skill-based compensation adjustments ensure that the Company can attract and retain employees with valuable capabilities while recognizing the investment employees have made in their development.
6.7.3 Development and Succession Planning
— High-Potential Identification. Employees identified through development programs as high-potential talent will be considered in succession planning discussions, positioning them for critical roles as the organization grows and evolves. Participation in leadership development programs, demonstration of leadership capability, and successful application of learning are factors that contribute to high-potential identification. High-potential employees receive enhanced development opportunities, accelerated career progression, and consideration for critical roles and succession positions.
— Succession Readiness. Development programs are designed not only to enhance current capability but also to prepare employees for future roles and responsibilities. Employees who engage actively in development, who demonstrate growth potential, and who acquire the capabilities required for advancement are more likely to be identified as successors for critical roles. Succession planning considers both current performance and future potential, and development is a key indicator of future potential.
6.7.4 Role Progression and Mobility
— Internal Mobility and Role Transitions. Active participation in learning and development will support role progression, including lateral moves into new functions, expanded responsibilities, or readiness for more senior positions. Employees who develop diverse skills, who gain experience in different areas, and who demonstrate adaptability are more likely to be considered for internal mobility opportunities. The Company encourages internal mobility as a means of career development and organizational capability building, and development is a key enabler of successful role transitions.
— Cross-Functional Development. Employees who develop skills and experience across multiple functions are better positioned for role progression and internal mobility. The Company provides opportunities for cross-functional development through project assignments, job rotations, cross-functional training, and other experiences that expose employees to different areas of the business. Cross-functional development enhances both individual career prospects and organizational capability.

6.8 Employee Responsibilities and Expectations
6.8.1 Active Engagement in Development
— Proactive Participation. Employees are responsible for engaging proactively with available learning opportunities, for identifying their own development needs, for seeking out development activities that align with their goals, and for taking ownership of their personal and professional development. The Company provides resources and opportunities, but employees must take initiative to make the most of those resources. Passive participation in development activities is insufficient — employees are expected to engage actively, to ask questions, to seek feedback, and to take responsibility for their own learning.
— Completion of Assigned Training. Employees are responsible for completing assigned training within prescribed timelines, including mandatory compliance training, role-specific training, and other development activities that are required or recommended for their role. Failure to complete required training may affect eligibility for certain opportunities, may be reflected in performance reviews, and may be treated as a compliance issue where mandatory training is involved. Employees are expected to prioritize development activities and to allocate time and effort to their own growth.
6.8.2 Application of Learning
— Putting Learning into Practice. Employees are responsible for applying their learning in their day-to-day work, for demonstrating new skills and capabilities, and for contributing enhanced value to the organization as a result of their development. Development is not complete when a training program ends — it is complete when learning is applied effectively in work. Employees are expected to seek opportunities to apply new learning, to experiment with new approaches, and to share their knowledge and expertise with colleagues.
— Sharing Knowledge and Expertise. Employees who develop new skills and knowledge are expected to share that expertise with colleagues, to contribute to team capability, and to support the development of others. Knowledge sharing may take the form of informal coaching, formal training delivery, documentation of best practices, or participation in communities of practice. The Company values employees who not only develop their own capabilities but also contribute to the development of others.
6.8.3 Development Planning and Self-Assessment
— Maintaining Development Plans. Employees are responsible for maintaining an updated personal development plan in consultation with their manager, for reviewing development progress regularly, and for adjusting plans as needs and opportunities change. Development planning is a collaborative process, but employees are expected to take ownership of their plans and to drive their own development agenda. Employees are encouraged to discuss their development goals with their managers, to seek feedback on their progress, and to advocate for development opportunities that align with their aspirations.
— Self-Assessment and Reflection. Employees are expected to engage in regular self-assessment of their capabilities, to identify their own development needs, and to reflect on their learning and growth. Self-assessment helps employees understand their strengths and areas for development, enables them to set meaningful development objectives, and supports continuous improvement. The Company provides tools and frameworks for self-assessment, but employees are responsible for engaging in honest self-reflection and for using assessment insights to guide their development.

6.9 Manager Responsibilities and Expectations
6.9.1 Identifying Development Needs
— Assessment and Gap Analysis. Managers are responsible for identifying development needs within their teams, for assessing current capabilities against role requirements, and for identifying skill gaps that need to be addressed. Development need identification occurs through performance reviews, observation of work, discussion with employees, analysis of team capability, and consideration of business needs. Managers are expected to have a clear understanding of the capabilities required for each role in their team and to identify where development is needed to meet those requirements.
— Individual and Team Development Planning. 6.11 Measurement and Evaluation of Development Effectiveness
6.11.1 Why Measurement Matters
— Demonstrating Value and Impact. The Company recognizes that development investments must generate value, and that measuring development effectiveness is essential to understanding whether programs are achieving their objectives, whether resources are being used efficiently, and whether the development strategy is working. Measurement enables the Company to make data-driven decisions about development priorities, to identify programs that are effective and those that need improvement, and to demonstrate the return on development investments to stakeholders.
— Continuous Improvement Through Evaluation. Evaluation of development programs provides insights that enable continuous improvement, identification of best practices, and refinement of the development strategy. The Company is committed to measuring development effectiveness not to justify investments but to improve them, and evaluation results are used to enhance programs, adjust strategies, and maximize the impact of development activities.
6.11.2 Evaluation Methods and Metrics
— Participant Feedback and Satisfaction. Participant feedback and satisfaction surveys provide immediate insights into program effectiveness, participant engagement, and areas for improvement. Feedback is collected after every development program and is analyzed to identify strengths, weaknesses, and opportunities for enhancement. High satisfaction scores indicate that programs are meeting participant needs, while low scores identify areas that require attention.
— Learning Assessment and Knowledge Retention. Learning assessments measure whether participants have acquired the intended knowledge and skills, whether learning objectives have been achieved, and whether knowledge is retained over time. Assessments may take the form of tests, practical exercises, demonstrations, or other evaluation methods appropriate to the learning objectives. Assessment results help the Company understand whether programs are effective in transferring knowledge and skills.
— Application and Behavior Change. The ultimate measure of development effectiveness is whether learning is applied in work and whether it results in improved performance or behavior change. Application is measured through observation, performance reviews, manager feedback, and self-assessment. Programs that result in meaningful application and behavior change are considered successful, while programs that do not translate into improved performance may need to be redesigned or discontinued.
— Business Impact and Return on Investment. The Company measures the business impact of development programs through metrics such as improved performance ratings, increased productivity, enhanced capability, reduced errors, improved customer satisfaction, and other indicators of organizational value. Return on investment calculations compare the cost of development programs to the value generated, helping the Company understand whether development investments are generating adequate returns. Business impact measurement is challenging but essential to demonstrating the value of development investments.
6.11.3 Using Evaluation Results
— Informing Development Strategy. Evaluation results inform the development strategy by identifying effective programs that should be expanded, ineffective programs that should be improved or discontinued, emerging needs that should be addressed, and best practices that should be replicated. The Company uses evaluation data to make strategic decisions about development priorities, resource allocation, and program design.
— Continuous Improvement. Evaluation results drive continuous improvement by identifying specific areas for enhancement, providing feedback to program designers and facilitators, and enabling iterative refinement of development programs. The Company is committed to using evaluation insights to improve programs continuously, ensuring that the development offering becomes more effective over time.

6.13 Mentoring and Coaching Programs
6.13.1 The Strategic Value of Mentoring and Coaching
— Mentoring and Coaching as Development Accelerators. Mentoring and coaching programs are among the most effective development interventions available to organizations, providing personalized guidance, support, and development that accelerates learning and enhances performance. Mentoring relationships pair less experienced employees with more experienced colleagues who can provide career guidance, share knowledge and expertise, offer perspective on challenges, and support professional growth. Coaching relationships provide structured, goal-oriented support that helps employees develop specific capabilities, overcome obstacles, and achieve development objectives. Both mentoring and coaching recognize that development is not only about acquiring knowledge and skills, but also about gaining wisdom, perspective, and confidence that comes from learning from others' experiences and receiving personalized support.
— Differentiating Mentoring from Coaching. While mentoring and coaching are both valuable development tools, they serve different purposes and are structured differently. Mentoring relationships are typically longer-term, relationship-focused partnerships that provide broad career guidance and professional development support. Mentors share their experience, provide career advice, help mentees navigate organizational dynamics, and offer perspective on professional challenges. Coaching relationships are typically shorter-term, goal-focused partnerships that help employees develop specific capabilities or achieve particular objectives. Coaches use structured methodologies, ask probing questions, provide feedback, and support skill development. The Company provides both mentoring and coaching opportunities, recognizing that different employees and situations benefit from different approaches.
6.13.2 Mentoring Program Structure
— Formal Mentoring Programs. The Company maintains formal mentoring programs that match mentors and mentees based on career objectives, development needs, and compatibility factors. Formal mentoring programs are structured with clear objectives, defined timeframes, regular meeting schedules, and support resources. Mentors are selected based on their experience, expertise, willingness to invest in others' development, and alignment with mentee needs. Mentees are selected based on their development needs, career aspirations, and readiness to engage actively in the mentoring relationship. Formal mentoring programs may be organized by function, by career level, by development focus, or by other relevant criteria.
— Informal Mentoring Relationships. While formal mentoring programs provide structured opportunities, the Company also encourages and supports informal mentoring relationships that develop organically based on mutual interest and compatibility. Informal mentoring relationships may be just as valuable as formal relationships, and the Company provides resources and support to help employees establish and maintain effective informal mentoring relationships. Employees are encouraged to seek out mentors who can support their development, and managers are encouraged to facilitate mentoring connections within their networks.
— Reverse Mentoring and Peer Mentoring. The Company recognizes that mentoring is not always unidirectional, and that valuable learning can occur in reverse mentoring relationships where junior employees mentor senior colleagues on topics such as technology, emerging trends, or generational perspectives. Peer mentoring relationships, where employees at similar levels support each other's development, are also valuable and encouraged. The Company supports diverse mentoring models that recognize that everyone has something to teach and something to learn.
6.13.3 Coaching Programs and Support
— Internal Coaching Resources. The Company maintains internal coaching resources, including trained internal coaches who can provide coaching support to employees on topics such as leadership development, performance improvement, career development, and skill acquisition. Internal coaches are employees who have received coaching training and certification, who have demonstrated coaching capability, and who are committed to supporting colleagues' development. Internal coaching is typically provided at no cost to employees and is integrated into the broader development framework.
— External Coaching Support. For senior leaders, high-potential employees, or employees with specific development needs that require specialized expertise, the Company may provide access to external professional coaches. External coaching is typically provided for defined engagements with specific objectives, and coaches are selected based on their expertise, methodology, and fit with the employee's needs. External coaching investments may require service commitment agreements for high-cost engagements, and coaching objectives and progress are tracked to ensure value is being delivered.
— Manager as Coach. The Company recognizes that managers play a critical coaching role in supporting their team members' development, and provides training to help managers develop coaching skills. Managers are expected to provide regular coaching to their team members, to ask questions that promote learning, to provide feedback that supports growth, and to create development experiences within the work context. Manager coaching is integrated into the performance management process and is evaluated as part of the manager's own performance review.

The Company aspires to be a learning organization — one that learns from experience, that adapts based on insights, that shares knowledge effectively, and that continuously improves. A learning organization recognizes that learning is not only an individual activity, but also an organizational capability that enables the entire organization to become smarter, more effective, and more competitive over time. Industry certifications demonstrate expertise, provide external validation of skills, and may be required for regulatory compliance or client requirements. The Company may sponsor certification costs, provide study support, and recognize certification achievements. Employees who obtain industry certifications are expected to maintain their credentials, to apply their certified expertise in their work, and to share their knowledge with colleagues.
6.20.3 External Education and Advanced Degrees
— Supporting Continued Education. The Company recognizes that some employees may wish to pursue external education, including advanced degrees, professional qualifications, or specialized courses, and provides support for continued education that aligns with business needs and employee development objectives. External education support may include tuition reimbursement, time off for classes, flexible scheduling, and other accommodations. External education support typically requires service commitment agreements for high-cost programs, and employees are expected to apply their education to their work and to share their learning with the organization.
— Balancing Education with Work Responsibilities. The Company supports employees who pursue external education while working, recognizing that continued education enhances capability and engagement. However, external education must be balanced with work responsibilities, and employees are expected to maintain performance standards while pursuing education. The Company works with employees to create schedules and arrangements that enable both work and education success, but employees are responsible for ensuring that their education pursuits do not compromise their work performance.

6.21 Development Records, Documentation, and Credentialing
6.21.1 Maintaining Development Records
— Comprehensive Development Documentation. The Company maintains comprehensive records of all development activities, including training completion, certification achievements, program participation, development plan progress, and other development-related information. Development records are stored in the HRMS, are accessible to employees and managers, and are used to track development progress, inform development decisions, and demonstrate development investment. Comprehensive documentation ensures that development activities are recognized, that progress is tracked, and that development history is preserved.
— Employee Access to Development Records. Employees have access to their own development records through the HRMS, enabling them to view their training history, track their progress, identify development gaps, and plan future development activities. Development records provide employees with visibility into their development journey and help them understand their growth over time. Employees are encouraged to review their development records regularly and to use them to inform their development planning.
6.21.2 Certification and Credential Management
— Tracking Professional Credentials. The Company maintains records of employee professional certifications, credentials, and qualifications, recognizing that these credentials are important indicators of capability and may be required for regulatory compliance or client requirements. Credential management includes tracking certification expiration dates, ensuring that required certifications are maintained, and supporting employees in maintaining their credentials. Employees are responsible for maintaining their professional credentials, but the Company provides support and reminders to help employees stay current.
— Continuing Education Requirements. Many professional certifications require continuing education to maintain the credential, and the Company supports employees in meeting continuing education requirements. The Company may provide continuing education opportunities, may sponsor continuing education costs, and may provide time and resources for continuing education activities. Supporting continuing education ensures that employees maintain their professional credentials and that the organization maintains certified capability.

6.22 Feedback Mechanisms and Continuous Improvement
6.22.1 Gathering Development Feedback
— Employee Feedback on Development Programs. The Company actively seeks feedback from employees on development programs, recognizing that employee input is essential to understanding program effectiveness and identifying improvement opportunities. Feedback is gathered through post-program surveys, focus groups, one-on-one discussions, and other mechanisms that enable employees to share their experiences, insights, and suggestions. Employee feedback is taken seriously, is analyzed systematically, and is used to improve development programs continuously.
— Manager Feedback on Development Effectiveness. Managers provide feedback on the effectiveness of development programs based on their observation of employee growth, application of learning, and performance improvement. Manager feedback helps the Company understand whether development programs are achieving their objectives, whether learning is being applied effectively, and whether development investments are generating value. Manager feedback is gathered regularly and is integrated into program evaluation and improvement processes.
6.22.2 Using Feedback for Continuous Improvement
— Iterative Program Enhancement. The Company uses feedback to enhance development programs iteratively, making improvements based on insights from participants, managers, and evaluation results. Continuous improvement recognizes that development programs are never perfect and that there is always room for enhancement. The Company is committed to using feedback to make programs more effective, more relevant, and more valuable over time.
— Innovation in Development Approaches. The Company encourages innovation in development approaches, recognizing that new methodologies, technologies, and practices may provide better ways to support employee growth. The Company experiments with new development approaches, pilots innovative programs, and adopts practices that demonstrate effectiveness. Innovation in development ensures that the Company's development framework remains current, effective, and aligned with best practices.

6.23 Development During Organizational Change and Transitions
6.23.1 Supporting Development During Change
— Development as a Change Enabler. Organizational change, including restructuring, mergers, acquisitions, strategic shifts, and other significant transitions, requires employees to develop new capabilities, adapt to new circumstances, and navigate uncertainty. The Company recognizes that development support is essential during organizational change, helping employees understand new requirements, develop needed capabilities, and successfully navigate transitions. Development during change is not a luxury — it is essential to successful change implementation and organizational adaptation.
— Change-Specific Development Programs. The Company provides development programs specifically designed to support employees during organizational change, including programs that help employees understand new strategies, develop new capabilities, adapt to new structures, and navigate change effectively. Change-specific development recognizes that change creates unique development needs and that specialized support is required to help employees succeed during transitions. The Company invests in change-related development to ensure that organizational changes are implemented successfully and that employees are supported through transitions.
6.23.2 Resilience and Adaptability Development
— Building Change Resilience. The Company recognizes that change is constant in modern business environments, and provides development programs designed to build employee resilience, adaptability, and change management capability. Resilience development helps employees navigate uncertainty, manage stress, maintain performance during change, and adapt successfully to new circumstances. Building change resilience is important for both individual well-being and organizational capability to navigate change effectively.
— Developing Adaptability Skills. Adaptability is a critical capability in rapidly changing business environments, and the Company provides development programs designed to build adaptability skills, including flexibility, learning agility, comfort with ambiguity, and ability to thrive in changing circumstances. Adaptability development helps employees succeed in dynamic environments and positions the organization to respond effectively to change and opportunity.

6.24 Policy Compliance, Governance, and Review
6.24.1 Responsibility for Policy Compliance
— Every Employee's Personal Responsibility. Compliance with this Learning and Development Policy is the personal responsibility of every employee. Employees are responsible for understanding this policy, for taking advantage of development opportunities, for completing required training, for applying learning in their work, and for taking ownership of their own development. Compliance is not merely about completing mandatory training — it is about engaging actively in development, taking responsibility for growth, and contributing enhanced capability to the organization.
— Manager Responsibility for Supporting Development. Managers are responsible for supporting their team members' development, for facilitating access to opportunities, for providing coaching and feedback, and for integrating development into performance management. Managers who fail to support employee development or who create barriers to development participation are not fulfilling their responsibilities and may be subject to performance management themselves.
— HR and Talent Development Responsibility. HR and the Talent Development function are responsible for administering this policy, for designing and delivering development programs, for tracking participation and effectiveness, and for ensuring that the development strategy supports organizational objectives. HR and Talent Development are also responsible for ensuring that development opportunities are accessible, that programs are high-quality, and that the development framework is continuously improved.
6.24.2 Employee Acknowledgment
— Formal Acknowledgment Is Expected. While formal acknowledgment of this policy is not required in the same way as compliance policies, employees are expected to be familiar with this policy, to understand the development opportunities available to them, and to engage actively in their own development. The Company communicates this policy clearly during onboarding and through regular communications, and employees are expected to take responsibility for understanding and utilizing the development framework.
6.24.3 Annual Policy Review
— Regular Review to Maintain Relevance and Currency. This Learning and Development Policy will be formally reviewed on an annual basis by HR and the Talent Development function, or earlier if required by a material change in business strategy, workforce needs, or applicable legislation. The review process includes assessment of policy effectiveness, evaluation of development program outcomes, consideration of feedback from employees and managers, identification of emerging development needs, and updates to ensure that the policy remains relevant and effective. All updates will be approved by the Chief Human Resources Officer, communicated to all employees through the HRMS and official Company communication channels, and integrated into the development framework. The Company's commitment to maintaining a current, comprehensive, and genuinely effective Learning and Development Policy is both a strategic imperative and a fundamental expression of its commitment to employee growth and organizational success.
